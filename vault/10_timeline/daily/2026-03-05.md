Need this info for tomorrow's meeting with Dr. Cat at 11AM: 
Okay, I can see everything. Here's what's important to know upfront: your prescription is **amphetamine-dextroamphetamine 20mg** (generic Adderall), which is a **Schedule II controlled substance**. That matters because Schedule II prescriptions have stricter rules — they typically can't just be "transferred" between pharmacies the way a normal prescription can. This means the preparation you're doing now is especially smart.

Here's what you should ask your current doctor for tomorrow:

**Documents to request:**

1. **A letter confirming your diagnosis and current treatment** — Ask him to write on official letterhead that you have an established ADHD diagnosis, your current medication (amphetamine-dextroamphetamine 20mg, 60 tabs/25 days), how long you've been on it, and that you're stable on this regimen. This is the single most important thing for your new doctor.
2. **A copy of your full medical records** — or at minimum, the records related to your ADHD diagnosis and treatment history. New doctors want to see how you arrived at this medication and dosage. Ask if he can send them electronically or give you a printed copy.
3. **A referral or recommendation** — Ask if he knows any psychiatrists or prescribers in the Boston area he'd recommend. Doctors often have professional networks, and a warm referral can speed things up.
4. **A 90-day prescription (or as long as he's willing)** — This is a bridge so you're not scrambling while you find a new provider. Schedule II meds can't be auto-refilled, so ask if he can write you enough to cover the transition period. Some doctors will do 2-3 months of post-dated prescriptions.
5. **Prior authorization documentation** — Ask if there's any prior authorization on file with Aetna for your medication. If so, get a copy. This saves your new doctor from having to redo that process.

**Key questions to ask:**

- "Will you be available for a phone consultation if my new doctor wants to verify my treatment history?" — Many new prescribers want to speak with your previous doctor before prescribing a controlled substance.
- "Can I continue to do telehealth visits with you until I'm established with someone new?" — Some doctors will see out-of-state patients temporarily, though this varies by state licensing.
- "Is there anything specific a new prescriber in Massachusetts will need from you?" — He may know state-specific requirements.

**On the insurance side**, your Aetna plan is a Standard National Network, which is good — it means your coverage should work in Boston without needing to switch plans. The back of your card also lists a Mental/Behavioral Health line (1-800-424-4047) which you can call to find in-network prescribers in the Boston area before you move.

The main thing to understand: with Schedule II medications, new doctors are often cautious and may want to do their own evaluation before prescribing. Having that letter and full records from your current doctor makes that process much faster and smoother.
