If you’ve been following along, you’ve probably already used AI a few times — a draft email here, a dinner idea there. Each time, you typed something, got something back, and moved on.

But every time you open that blank text box, you’re starting from scratch. You have to think about what to type, how to phrase it, what details to include. And for a lot of people, that blank-page moment is where the momentum dies.

Today we’re building something that fixes that. By the end of this piece, you’ll have three go-to requests — tested and saved — that you can reuse anytime. Think of them as starter templates for the tasks you’ll come back to most.

## What we’re building

A go-to request is a set of instructions you’ve already figured out — tested, tweaked, and ready to reuse. Instead of staring at a blank text box every time, you paste in your go-to, swap out the details, and get a useful result in seconds.

Think of it like a recipe you’ve made enough times that you don’t need the cookbook anymore. You know the proportions. You know where to adjust. The thinking is already done — you just execute.

We’re going to build them together, covering three situations: drafting something, understanding something, and brainstorming something. By the end, you’ll have all of them saved somewhere you can grab them — a note on your phone, a document on your desktop, wherever you keep things you come back to.

### Go-to #1: The quick draft

This one covers any situation where you need to write something and don’t know where to start. Open your AI tool — ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini all work with a free account, no paid subscription needed — and type this:

> *I need to write a \[type of message: email / text / note / letter\] to \[who it’s for\]. Here’s the situation: \[describe what happened or what you need in 2-3 sentences\]. The tone should be \[firm but polite / friendly / casual / professional / apologetic\]. Keep it to \[length: a few sentences / one paragraph / half a page\].*

Try it right now with something real. A message you’ve been meaning to send, or one you sent recently that took longer than it should have.

Read the output. If the tone is off, tell it: *Make this more \[casual / direct / warm\].* If it’s too long, say: *Cut this in half.* Two rounds of adjusting is normal — that’s how the tool works best.

When you have a version you’re happy with, copy the instructions (not the output — the instructions you typed) and save them somewhere. That’s your first go-to.

### Go-to #2: The plain-language explainer

You know those moments where you need to understand something but don’t want to wade through a textbook or a ten-page article? Type this:

> *Explain \[topic or concept\] in plain language, like I’m someone who’s smart but has never encountered this before. Use a real-world example to make it concrete. Keep it under \[number\] sentences.*

Try it with something you’ve been meaning to understand. How does a 401(k) actually work? What’s the difference between a PPO and an HMO? What does “the Fed raising interest rates” actually mean for your mortgage? Pick something real.

If the explanation still feels too abstract, follow up with: *Can you explain that using an example from everyday life — like grocery shopping or planning a vacation?*

Save the instructions.

### Go-to #3: The brainstorm partner

This one’s for when you’re stuck — not because you don’t know what to do, but because you can’t see all your options. Type this:

*I’m trying to \[describe what you’re working on\]. Here’s what I’ve considered so far: \[list what you’ve already thought of, even if it’s vague\]. Give me \[number\] more ideas I haven’t thought of. For each one, tell me in one sentence why it might work.*

Try it with something you’re actually mulling over. Weekend plans with your family. Gift ideas for a hard-to-shop-for friend. Ways to reorganize your home office. A side project you’ve been thinking about starting.

The “here’s what I’ve considered so far” part matters. It stops AI from giving you obvious suggestions you’ve already ruled out and pushes it toward ideas that are actually new to you.

Save the instructions.

## Testing and tweaking

You now have three saved sets of instructions. Before we call them done, let’s make sure they actually work for your life.

Pick the one you think you’ll use most often. Open your AI tool and run it again — but this time, with a completely different situation than the one you tested earlier. Different topic, different context.

The first result probably won’t be perfect. That’s not a failure — that’s normal. Most of these need two or three rounds of adjusting before they really click. Read the output, notice what’s off, and tell AI what to change. “Too formal.” “Missing the part about the deadline.” “Give me more creative options.” Each round gets closer.

If you always want casual tone, make “casual tone” part of the default. Same for length — if your answers always come back too long, put the limit right in the instructions.

The goal isn’t a perfect set of instructions on the first try. It’s a set that gets better every time you use it.

## Want to go further?

Try building a fourth go-to for something specific to your life — something none of the three above covers. Maybe it’s “help me prep for a meeting,” “help me write social media captions,” or “help me plan a lesson for my students.” Use the same structure: describe the situation, give AI the details it needs, and specify what you want back. As you build more of these, you’ll notice a pattern in what makes them work: a clear task, relevant details, and a specific format for the output. That pattern — task, details, format — is the foundation of getting consistently good results from AI. We’ll build on it as we go.

## Try this (10-15 minutes)

Open the three sets of instructions you saved. Run each one with a real situation from your life — not the test cases you used earlier, but new ones. Adjust anything that didn’t work. When all three produce results you’d actually use, you’re done.

You just built your first AI toolkit. It’s small — three go-to requests — but it’s yours, it’s tested, and it’s ready to use whenever you need it.

## What we learned

A go-to request is a reusable set of instructions you’ve tested and saved — it removes the blank-page problem and makes AI useful in seconds instead of minutes. The three most versatile starting points cover drafting, explaining, and brainstorming. The real skill isn’t writing the perfect instructions on the first try — it’s adjusting them until they fit your life.

## Useful Terms

- **Prompt template:** A reusable set of instructions you paste into an AI tool
- **Iterating:** Adjusting your instructions based on what AI gives you back
- **Prompt structure:** The pattern of describing your task, adding relevant details, and specifying the output format
