The standard career advice in 2026 is to build a portfolio. Learn the tools. Ship projects. Show, don’t tell. And that advice isn’t wrong, exactly, but it does lead all of us toward the one thing AI already accelerates better than we do: producing stuff. More projects, more demos, more prototypes, everyone generating faster, shipping more, adding to a pile that grows at machine speed and never stops.

The uncomfortable part is that none of it proves what it used to. Production used to signal competence because production used to be hard, and hard meant effort, and effort meant expertise. That was the whole chain of proof, and AI broke it. The building is easy now. What’s scarce now, the thing that nobody has a reliable system for demonstrating, is whether you actually understood what you built — why it works, what would break, what you chose not to do and why.

That’s not just a junior problem, although juniors feel it most acutely. The mid-career PM who can’t show what she actually contributed last year, the senior engineer deploying systems he can’t fully explain, the exec leading an AI overhaul she’s never tested with her own hands — the break is the same at every level. Everybody knows it. This is the 5pm happy hour conversation, and it’s happening everywhere. And the urgency underneath it is not hypothetical: Oracle cut 30,000 jobs via early-morning email and dead badges, Block cut 4,000 with Dorsey citing AI tools, and Salesforce cut a thousand across marketing, product, and their own Agentforce AI unit. Per Challenger, AI led all stated reasons for job cuts in March at 25% of the total, up from about 5% across all of 2025.

- **Transactions over credentials.** Why static resumes are dead signal in a world where AI generates perfect claims of competence in seconds, and what replaces them.
- **Work in the open.** How to build professional credibility without warm intros, corporate access, or a network built on signals that no longer carry weight.

This isn’t resume optimization — that game is over. The people who figure this out first will have an advantage that compounds. The people who keep optimizing output will wonder why nobody notices.

## The shift without a playbook

Think about what a performance review actually evaluates. Did you ship the feature? Hit the revenue target? Deliver the analysis? Coordinate the launch? These are all production metrics. The actual judgment, whether you understood the system, whether you chose the right problem, whether the tradeoffs were sound, that lives invisibly in your head and in the quality of the output. But when the output can be generated by anyone with the right tools, the quality stops being a reliable signal for the judgment underneath it.

The hiring problem is really a societal allocation problem. How does a company know who to promote? How does a team know who’s actually contributing? And at the broadest level, how does an economy route talent to the work that matters most? All of those questions used to be answered, imperfectly but functionally, by observing production. The proxy was leaky but it worked. Now the proxy is broken and we don’t have a replacement.

The advice to “build more things” is the old framework applied to the new world. It chases the commodity, output, while ignoring the scarcity, comprehension. It’s like telling someone in a world with infinite free food that the way to demonstrate value is to cook more meals. The skill that matters isn’t cooking. It’s knowing what’s worth eating.

So what I want to offer instead: five principles for orienting your work, and proving your value, in a world where generation is free.

## Principle 1: Comprehension over generation

The default instinct when you get access to powerful AI tools is to produce. Build things. Ship things. Generate output. This is the path of least resistance, and it’s the wrong optimization.

The right optimization is to understand what you produce. Deeply. In context. To the point where you can explain not just what it does, but why it does it that way, what would break if you changed it, what tradeoffs you made, and what you deliberately chose *not* to build.

This sounds obvious, but it isn’t. Watch what happens when someone vibe-codes a project: they prompt, they iterate, they get something working, they ship. At no point did they stop and build a mental model of the system they just created. They have a working artifact and no comprehension of it. If you asked them to modify it without AI assistance, they couldn’t. Ask what would fail under load and they’d guess. Ask why the AI chose this architecture over the alternatives and they’d shrug. It works. That’s all they know.

Now multiply that by every person shipping AI-assisted work in every company on earth. The industry is producing at unprecedented speed and comprehending at unprecedented lows. Teams deploy features nobody fully understood. PMs ship prototypes they can’t explain. Engineers merge code they didn’t hold in their heads. The chasm between what software does and what anyone thinks it does is widening every day, and it’s getting people in real trouble. Amazon learned this expensively when an engineer used Kiro, the company’s own AI coding agent — which Amazon had mandated 80% of its engineers use weekly — and Kiro decided the optimal path was to delete and recreate an entire AWS service environment. Thirteen hours of downtime. The official response called it “user error” and “a coincidence that AI tools were involved.” The user was following the corporate mandate to use the corporate tool that made the corporate decision to nuke a live system.

That’s what happens when production outruns comprehension at an organizational level. At an individual level, it looks less dramatic but the failure mode is the same: you’re building things you don’t understand, and eventually something breaks that you can’t diagnose.

The discipline is to force comprehension at the point of creation, not after. Before you ship anything, before you even show it to anyone, sit with it and answer: what does this actually do? What are the dependencies? What’s the blast radius if something goes wrong? What decisions did I make and why? Where is the AI’s work that I chose to keep, and where did I override it?

The point isn’t documentation for its own sake but the practice of understanding your own work. And that practice is the seed of taste. Taste doesn’t come from mysterious aesthetic instinct. It comes from having understood enough things deeply enough that you start to recognize patterns: what works, what breaks, what matters. Comprehension is how you develop taste. And taste is the skill that survives every wave of technological change.

For juniors: this is how you replace the apprenticeship. The old way was years of supervised grunt work: ticket triage, documentation updates, test coverage, data cleanup. Nobody liked doing it, but doing it was the education. You absorbed context, you learned what good looked like by seeing what bad looked like, and you built taste through a thousand small tasks under the supervision of people who already had it. That apprenticeship is gone. Companies automated the grunt work and cut the junior roles that depended on it. They’re eating their seed corn. Every junior role cut in 2024 is a future senior who never develops the judgment the company will desperately need in 2028.

But the learning mechanism underneath the apprenticeship, forced comprehension through repetition, doesn’t require a corporate structure. You can build it yourself. The reps come from forcing yourself to understand every single thing you produce. The volume matters less than the depth. One project you fully comprehend teaches you more than ten projects you vibe-coded and moved on from. The AI accelerates your production. You have to deliberately decelerate your comprehension. Sit with the thing and understand it. That’s the rep.

For seniors: this is how you avoid becoming the person who deploys AI-assisted systems they can’t explain. That’s not a hypothetical. It’s happening everywhere. The senior who forces comprehension at every stage isn’t slowing things down. They’re the reason the system doesn’t break in ways nobody can diagnose.

## Principle 2: Explanation as artifact

If comprehension is the scarce skill, explanation is how you make it visible. Not a blog post about your process, not a case study you write after the fact — a structured explanation that travels *with* the work, inseparable from it, as native to the deliverable as the code itself.

What does that look like? For every piece of work you produce, create a short companion artifact that answers four questions:

**What is this?** Not a marketing description. A clear statement of what the thing does and what problem it addresses.

**Why this approach?** What alternatives existed? What tradeoffs did you evaluate? What did you choose *not* to build and why? This is where taste becomes visible. Anyone can produce an output. The explanation of why *this* output and not the dozen other possible outputs is the signal.

**What would break?** Where are the fragile points? What assumptions is this built on? If the requirements changed, what falls apart? This is the blast radius question, and it’s the one that separates people who understand their systems from people who happen to have working systems.

**What did I learn?** Not in a self-help sense. Concretely: what did you discover during the process that changed your approach? Where did the AI give you something confidently wrong and how did you catch it? What would you do differently next time?

This template is deliberately simple. It’s not a design document or a spec. It’s four questions that force comprehension and make it legible. And the beautiful thing about this format is that it scales with depth. A junior’s answers reveal the beginning of taste, they’re learning to see tradeoffs, starting to develop instincts about quality. A senior’s answers reveal mature judgment, deep pattern recognition, systems thinking, the ability to see second and third-order consequences. The template is the same. The signal it carries is proportional to the understanding behind it.

Think of it this way: the explanation artifact is to the generative era what the commit message is to traditional software engineering. A commit without a message is technically complete. But a commit with a thoughtful message signals that someone understood what they were changing and why. The message is the proof that a human held the system in their head. The explanation artifact is the same proof, for a world where the system was produced by AI.

## Principle 3: Transactions over credentials

The old proof-of-value system was cumulative. You stacked credentials over time — degrees, certifications, job titles, years of experience. Each one was a proxy for competence, a shorthand that saved evaluators the trouble of assessing whether you could actually do anything.

For centuries, these proxies weren’t just economic signals. They were social ones. The professor’s title, the doctor’s degree, the consultant’s pedigree — these were totems in a system of meaning. When you accumulated credentials, you accumulated not just earning potential but identity, position, a story about who you were.

AI broke the proxy. This is the knowledge hyperinflation I’ve been thinking about for a while. The same way that printing money doesn’t create wealth (it redistributes it from savers to borrowers) producing more credentials doesn’t create more signal. It dilutes the signal that exists. Every AI-generated resume in the pile makes every human-generated resume worth less. Every vibe-coded portfolio project makes every genuinely understood project harder to identify. The degree doesn’t prove you learned anything when a significant chunk of students use ChatGPT on every assignment, and they’re rational actors gaming a system that hasn’t adapted. Twenty-first-century tools to game nineteenth-century assessments for eleventh-century credentials.

The replacement isn’t better credentials. It’s *transactions.* Small, verifiable, completed units of real work, each one with the explanation artifact attached.

A transaction is different from a credential in a fundamental way. A credential says “I am qualified.” A transaction says “I did this, and here’s proof I understood it.” Credentials are claims. Transactions are evidence. And the distinction matters because claims are infinitely generatable (AI can produce a perfect claim of competence in seconds) while evidence of genuine understanding is hard to fake. You can generate a working prototype without understanding it. You cannot generate a coherent, deep explanation of a system you don’t understand. The explanation breaks down, the gaps show, and the hand-waving becomes visible.

Transactions compound differently than credentials. A resume is a static document you update every few years. A transaction history is a living record that grows with every piece of work you complete. Over time, the pattern of what you build, how you explain it, and how your understanding develops tells a story that no resume could. It shows your trajectory, which problems you gravitate toward, how your taste sharpens. Someone reviewing your transaction history can see the arc: where you started simple and where you got sophisticated, where you made mistakes and how you adapted, which domains you developed real depth in and which you explored and moved on from. No credential tells that story.

For the hiring side, this solves the evaluation problem that’s been broken since AI collapsed the signal. A hiring manager looking at a transaction history with explanation artifacts can actually assess whether someone understands their work. They’re not decoding a resume. They’re not guessing whether the portfolio was AI-generated. They’re looking at real work and real comprehension, side by side. Which is the signal the industry is desperate for and has no current mechanism to surface.

## Principle 4: Work in the open

Professional development used to happen behind closed doors. You built skills in private, inside a company, observed by a small number of colleagues and managers. Nobody outside your organization saw the reps. The learning was real but invisible. The proof that you were good came from people who’d watched you work, which is why warm introductions have always been the strongest hiring signal.

The problem is that this model requires *access.* You need to be inside a company to be observed. You need a network to get introduced. Juniors don’t have either. Mid-career professionals have networks built on old signals that no longer carry weight. And even seniors. Their reputation was built in a different era, on different skills. The fact that you were a great PM in 2019 doesn’t prove you can work AI-natively in 2026.

Working in the open, producing transactions with explanation artifacts in places where other practitioners can see them, is how you build credibility without needing the old access structures.

Open-source contributions work this way for developers. Maintainers see your PRs, observe your judgment in code review, watch how you handle feedback. But that model never extended beyond code. For the PM, the designer, the strategist, the analyst, there was no equivalent. No place to do visible work that demonstrated professional judgment to people who could evaluate it.

That’s the gap. And it’s not just a junior gap. The mid-career PM trying to demonstrate they’ve adapted to AI-native product work needs somewhere to show it. The senior engineer who wants to prove they actually understand the systems they ship, not just the code they write, needs a place where that comprehension is visible. The exec who claims to understand AI but has never built anything with the tools needs a mechanism to either prove it or be honest about the gap.

Working in the open creates accountability. If your explanation artifacts are public, you can’t hand-wave. You can’t claim comprehension you don’t have. The work and the understanding are there for anyone to evaluate. That’s uncomfortable. But the discomfort is what makes it work — you can’t fake your way through a public comprehension test.

## Principle 5: The proof travels with the work

This is the principle that ties the other four together, and it’s simpler than the others: the proof should never be separated from the thing it’s proving. You did the work in one place and described it in another, and the description was always a lossy compression of what actually happened.

In the new model, the proof is inseparable from the work. The explanation artifact isn’t a resume entry about the project. It’s *part of* the project. When someone encounters your work, they encounter the comprehension layer at the same time. There’s no translation step. There’s no separate document to maintain. The work carries its own evidence of understanding.

This matters because it changes what “showing your work” means. It’s not “show what you built.” It’s “show that you understand what you built, as part of the artifact itself.” The comprehension isn’t metadata about the work. It’s a native layer of the deliverable.

And this applies at every scale. An individual contributor produces a tool with an explanation artifact. A team ships a feature with a comprehension layer that explains the system design and tradeoffs. An organization builds products with architectural decision records and context engineering baked in. The principle is the same at every level: the proof of understanding should never be separated from the thing that was understood.

You vibe-code a real solution in Cursor. It’s a repo that nobody will find. Your AI work is scattered across a dozen platforms with zero context, zero discoverability, and zero way for anyone who matters to find it or evaluate it.

GitHub solved this for developers almost two decades ago. Your commit history became your resume. The work spoke for itself. But that model only works for traditional code, and the new shape of work isn’t traditional repos. PMs prototype workflows, designers build functional components, strategists build dashboards, analysts create interactive models. Juniors vibe-code real solutions to real problems. The most AI-native generation of professionals in history is producing real work, and it has nowhere to live.

LinkedIn isn’t the place anymore. A resume only tells a portion of the story. What’s needed is the co-located “here is me and also my stuff,” your professional identity and your actual work, together, in one place that people can browse. That’s what I built.

## The bigger picture

I want to be honest about what I think is at stake, because it’s bigger than career advice.

We spent decades building increasingly elaborate proxy systems for competence, systems that rewarded credential-gaming, resume optimization, and interview theater over actual ability. Any system where a consultant spent 40% of their billable hours on PowerPoint formatting was not efficiently allocating human talent to human problems. The fact that AI broke those proxies is painful. But the proxies were already failing. They were already unfair. They were already filtering out capable people and filtering in polished performers.

What’s left is the work and whether you understand it. I think we can build on that. I think a world where the proof of value is “here’s what I did and here’s evidence I understood it” is a better world than the one where the proof of value is “here’s a document listing things I claim to have done.”

The question I always get: what if everyone does this? Doesn’t it become saturated?

No. And the reason is structural. If a generation of professionals at every level starts making their understanding visible, with verifiable evidence, that’s not saturation. It’s the talent infrastructure getting rebuilt from the supply side. More people doing this makes the ecosystem healthier, not more competitive.

And the honest answer: most people won’t. Not because it’s hard, but because it requires sitting with your work and articulating what it does, why, and what would break. That’s a filter that doesn’t get gamed, because it’s not a trick. It’s genuine comprehension. The people who do this consistently are developing taste through reps in the open, and they’re creating exactly the signal that lets the economy route talent to meaningful work, which is the thing that’s actually broken right now.

We’re building this. Come build with us.
