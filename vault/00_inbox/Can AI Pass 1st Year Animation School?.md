No matter how old I get, September always puts me in that back-to-school mood. It’s a specific mix of new-binder smell and bumpy bus rides all wrapped up in impending homework dread. It never quite worked its way out of my system.

The most exciting back-to-school moment for me was when I started animation college. The thought of being with other animation nerds as we learned how to make cartoons felt like a kind of magic. Well, a stressful kind of magic, but magic all the same.

In recent years there has been a lot of talk of how well generative AI can do academically. GenAI [famously passed the bar exam](https://www.reuters.com/legal/legalindustry/artificial-intelligence-is-now-an-law-student-study-finds-2025-06-05/); it received a [gold medal at the](http://gold%20medal%20at%20the%20international%20mathematical%20olympiad/) **[International Mathematical Olympiad](http://gold%20medal%20at%20the%20international%20mathematical%20olympiad/)**; recently **[Sam Altman](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy5prvgw0r1o)** [compared](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy5prvgw0r1o) **[ChatGPT5](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy5prvgw0r1o)** [to a PhD student](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy5prvgw0r1o). An expert in any field. That got me wondering how generative AI would handle a first year animation college program.

Let’s find out.

### Methodology

For this experiment, I took six classic animation exercises typically completed in a first year classical animation course. For each exercise I drew the first frame and then fed it into the **[Runway](https://runwayml.com/)** AI-video generation platform with an appropriately detailed prompt. Runway is currently one of the leading image-to-video models out there which makes it a good proxy for today’s best-available consumer tools. I gave it at least three attempts and then chose the best result to post here.

#### Congratulations AI on being accepted into the Life in the Machine Animation Institute. The faculty wishes you the best of luck!

## The Bouncing Ball

This may contain: the basic steps to bouncing ball action

The quintessential animation exercise. I’ve always felt like the bouncing ball test contains all the elements of good animation - timing, spacing, arcs, solid drawing, squash & stretch, weight, and on and on and on. I don’t think there’s an animator alive who hasn’t animated one of these. Let’s see what AI can do.

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/783f9a0e-8af0-4c4b-863b-d0617028d7d5" controls=""></video>

So… not great. I’ll admit, this is the exercise that I put through Runway the most times with some pretty wild results. And I promise this was the best attempt. It has some smooth arcs, but not much else. The ball never actually bounces! There’s no squash & stretch, no sense of weight. It’s just a circle floating around the page with zero context.

#### GRADE: F

## Pendulum Swing

This is a simple exercise meant to teach arcs and spacing. This pendulum action shows up everywhere in animation, so it’s a pretty core concept to build upon.

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/da0bc2f5-ed1e-476d-b490-044e7cf0fae2" controls=""></video>

This is pretty good! We have the eases on either side, the circle maintains volume and it moves in a smooth arc. The length of the string shrinks a little bit during the swing which makes the arc less pronounced, but not bad for first year. It’s perhaps telling that the most mechanical exercise was the most successful for an AI system.

#### GRADE: B+

## Seaweed Wave

Along with pendulum arcs, animating curves in a wave pattern is another building block of animation. In my first year animation course we learned this by animating a piece of seaweed drifting back and forth underwater. (Some schools do a waving flag or a whip — it’s the same concept).

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/6ab17700-edcb-4d6c-941c-341e1af24b4c" controls=""></video>

The movement here is mostly smooth, and the forms are fairly consistent, but it’s missing the main lesson of the assignment which is to have the S-curve reverse as the wave changes course. This is what this lesson is all about.

## Flour Sack Jump

This was always my favourite and a classic **Disney** -style animation test. Can AI make a flour sack feel alive by giving it a sense of personality while applying the concepts of anticipation, action, and reaction along with squash & stretch, weight, timing, and consistent volumes?

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/1155368f-7371-47df-a68d-7fde80a3978a" controls=""></video>

I’ll start with what’s kind of working here. The sack does anticipate down before the jump and there is follow-through with its feet. But there are some obvious huge problems, most notably the design of the sack completely changes by the end of the shot. The spacing and the arcs don’t work either, so there is absolutely no sense of weight. I can’t say this flour sack feels alive to me.

#### GRADE: F

## “Meow” Lipsync

When beginning to learn lipsync, we often start with the word “meow” because it hits most of the major mouth shapes and it’s a good word to practice finding arcs within the mouth and head movements. You got this AI!

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/f2242cd2-7369-425b-9b30-d9bf43190f05" controls=""></video>

I know this student. They spent all night drinking and then tried to pull one over at the last minute. I don’t think so. Try again.

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/3cd0ad5d-d7f1-454d-a8a9-75a0166eccb2" controls=""></video>

Okay, that’s better. The mouth opens the correct way for the design of the character and I like that there’s head movement (albeit a bit stiff). The real problem here is that they didn’t hit the proper mouth shapes. We need to see the M-EE-AH-OO for this exercise to be considered a success otherwise this is just generic lipflap.

#### GRADE: D-

## Pink Panther Walk Cycle

I remember this assignment very clearly. This was our final exam. The entire first year animation course led to this: **The Pink Panther Walk Cycle**. It was our first chance to actually show a full body character in motion applying all of the previous lessons into a single animated clip. Walk cycles are so fundamental **Richard Williams** dedicated a huge portion of his famous **Animator’s Survival Kit** on nothing but walks. Let’s see what AI can do.

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/61acd486-b015-4620-a011-1bcf026e2e6f" controls=""></video>

Okay, okay. The basic locomotion is there. The model seems to understand how the arms should swing and what the passing action should look like. At first glance the foot slide seems basically correct as well. Of course if you stop on any one frame things get a little messed up:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9pdr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbfd61671-7d00-4437-8c0a-4af2b78f6022_591x519.png)

Also, about a second and a half in the arms switch and the back arm suddenly jumps to the front. There is also a general stiffness to the animation, not taking advantage of concepts like drag, offsets, wave action, or smooth arcs. Basically many of the elements that were supposed to be learned from the earlier assignments should be present here, and they’re not.

I’m not sure why the Pink Panther turns into a silhouette towards the end, but I would suggest AI spend less time on colouring and more time focusing on the fundamentals.

#### GRADE: D

*(Although I admit this turned out better than I thought it would)*

## Report Card

#### Sorry to say, given the results of these six assignments AI is not ready for second-year. Might I suggest a year in our Art Fundamentals program?

In all seriousness, the Runway model struggled here not because AI has no future in animation, but because it’s not as plug-and-play as the AI-hype train would have us believe.

It turns out interpreting 2D drawings is tough. There is quite a bit going on in your brain when you see a line drawing and understand the 3D shape it implies. There’s even more involved in creating a second drawing that respects the graphic choices made in the first image while moving it convincingly through space. That’s why AI video generators are much better at photoreal images than stylized ones. This bouncing ball video was also generated by Runway and is miles more successful than the cartoon version.

<video src="blob:https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/17cb8520-e76e-4642-9688-7f2164bceeda" controls=""></video>

Even with precise prompts for simple actions, Runway struggled to manipulate the drawings in ways that most first-year animation students would. It’s a bit like the **[ARC Prize](https://arcprize.org/)** which is a reasoning benchmark of visual puzzles that humans find easy but models often miss.

![ARC-AGI task](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3z31!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F93a2443a-7870-4398-9b34-63121b902248_1398x243.jpeg)

What comes next? via ARC Prize

The other issue is that image-to-video models lack controllability to translate your intent to the screen. It’s sometimes called the **slot-machine effect**, and I’ve [written previously](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/will-ai-video-kill-the-animation) about why this is a main impediment to these tools taking hold in professional pipelines.

What will have more impact are targeted, bespoke tools that help automate certain parts of the process. Tools like **[Animaj](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/pocoyos-ai-era)** [’s sketch-to-pose](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/pocoyos-ai-era), **[Maya](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/inside-mayas-motionmaker)** [’s](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/inside-mayas-motionmaker) **[MotionMaker](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/inside-mayas-motionmaker)**, and a wave of [research coming out of](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/siggraph-2025-recap) **[SIGGRAPH](https://lifeinthemachine.substack.com/p/siggraph-2025-recap)** this year are making much bigger inroads into the animation process. So while **Runway** / **Sora** / **Veo** won’t be enrolling at **CalArts** this fall, generative AI is absolutely changing *how* we make cartoons.

Okay, that’s the bell. Don’t forget the reading. And if you can coax AI into making a flour sack hop (or a ball actually bounce) send it my way. I’d be happy to share in a future newsletter.
