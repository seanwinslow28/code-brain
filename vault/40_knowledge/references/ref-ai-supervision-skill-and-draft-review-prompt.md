You’ve probably noticed that the documents landing in your inbox got faster. Cleaner, too. The strategy doc that used to take someone three days now shows up before lunch, and it’s not just one person — it’s everyone you work with.

A year ago, being the person who could turn around a polished memo in an hour with ChatGPT felt like a real edge, and for a while it probably was. Now everyone got fast at the same time, which means speed is still useful but it isn’t rare anymore. Polish is cheap now too — structure, clean formatting, executive-summary language, the whole package of things that used to make a document look as if somebody had really sat with it.

Today I want to walk through what replaced speed as the visible AI skill, and a small habit you can run on anything AI hands you before you send it. Three things:

- Why “fast and polished” stopped being much of an edge once everyone got access to the same tools.
- Why the next visible skill is supervision: knowing what to trust, what to check, what to cut, and what not to send.
- A prompt for making the model help you inspect the work without handing the decision back to it.

## The new skill

Last year, the visible AI skill was production — getting the draft out, getting it polished, getting it sent. This year, the visible AI skill is supervision: looking at the machine-produced thing and deciding whether it’s good enough to represent you.

When AI could only give you a rough first draft, the valuable person was the one who could get something usable out of it. Now the tool can give almost anyone a usable-looking first draft, which means the valuable person is the one who can tell the difference between usable-looking and actually usable.

That is a different skill from the one most people were practicing last year. It is less about typing the perfect prompt and more about reading the answer with enough context to know what should stay, what should go, what needs checking, and what would make the work more useful to the person receiving it. The prompt still matters, but the prompt is no longer the whole game, because more of the value is moving to the judgment that happens after the model gives you something polished.

This is why a lot of AI advice already feels a little dated. “Use AI to write faster” was the first move, and it was a good one, but if everyone can do that, the edge has to move somewhere else. It moves to editing, verification, taste, context, and knowing what the work is actually supposed to accomplish. It moves to the parts where someone on the other side — your boss, your kid’s teacher, the person reading the email you sent — can tell whether a person with judgment was involved.

One thing worth clarifying: I do not mean supervision in the management sense. I mean supervision in the “can you look at this machine-produced thing and decide whether it is good enough to represent you” sense. That skill is less flashy than speed, but it is also much harder to commoditize.

## What supervision looks like

The supervision skill shows up in a handful of small habits you can run on a real document before it leaves your outbox, and none of them need to become a giant review process. The point is not to perform responsibility or create a workflow that sounds good in a meeting and dies after two days. The point is to put back, deliberately, the human contribution the polish covered up.

Start with the most specific claim in the document and check that one thing: the number, the date, the named source, the customer segment, or the line that sounds like it came from somewhere. AI is often weakest where the prose sounds most confident and specific, and your boss is most likely to ask about exactly that line in tomorrow’s meeting. You don’t need to review the document like a lawyer; you need to catch the line that would be most embarrassing to be wrong about.

Then cut a quarter of the length before you send it, because AI almost always gives you too much: three bullets when one would do, a paragraph of throat-clearing before the answer, and four “key considerations” when only one is actually the point. Cutting a quarter of the length forces you to read the thing like an owner.

## One supervision prompt for the draft before you send it

This is the check I’d run when the draft looks finished, but you still need to decide whether it actually holds up. Paste it with anything AI hands you, before you decide it’s good enough to represent you. It is asking the model to slow down at the places where AI-assisted work is most likely to fool you: the confident claim, the impressive but empty sentence, the thing the reader will immediately notice is missing.

> *You are a senior editorial supervisor. Your one job is catching the gap between “looks polished” and “is actually good.” Be blunt and specific. Diagnose, don’t rewrite.*
> 
> *First ask me two things, then wait for my answers: who’s going to read this, and what it’s supposed to accomplish.*
> 
> *Then review the draft I paste and give me:*
> 
> *1\. The most dangerous line. The single most specific claim — a number, date, named source, or line that reads like it came from somewhere authoritative. Quote it, and tell me why it’s the one that would be most embarrassing to be wrong about in front of that reader. AI is weakest where the prose sounds most confident.*
> 
> *2\. Sounds smart, says nothing. Up to three passages that sound authoritative but aren’t actually saying anything specific or actionable. Quote each and name what’s missing — a real number, a concrete example, an actual recommendation.*
> 
> *3\. The actual point, in one sentence. Then tell me whether the draft leads with that or buries it under throat-clearing.*
> 
> *4\. Fit. Anything that contradicts what the reader already knows or wants, answers a question nobody asked, or leaves out something they’ll immediately go looking for.*
> 
> *End with a fix list ordered by how much damage each issue does if I leave it in — one concrete line each.*
> 
> *Don’t rewrite anything. And don’t manufacture criticism: if it’s good, say so.*

## What we learned today

The thing I would take from this is that nobody is going back to the version of work where every memo starts from a blank page and every update has to be assembled by hand. The useful shift is a little more specific than that.

The visible AI skill shifted from production to supervision. The thing that stands out now is the ability to look at a clean-looking draft and say: this claim needs checking, this paragraph is just sounding smart, this recommendation doesn’t fit what we already know, and this part is the actual point.
