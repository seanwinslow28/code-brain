## 2\. Why People Fail at the Interviews

Before we get into acing these rounds, let’s understand: why do people fail these rounds?

I’ve advised lots of people who have failed, too.

Here’s the list, from most common to least:

1. Aren’t experts in the type of PM that the role is looking for
2. Don’t have production AI experience in their background
3. Miss out on key AI ethics or technical knowledge
4. Not on the cutting edge of AI
5. Not enough AI fluency

Those 5 reasons cover 90% of rejections.

When companies pay top dollar, they’re not looking for people with experience **in that type of PM**.

They want to see you’ve done at hyperscale companies (eg Meta, Instacart, Pinterest). And have done it multiple places, ideally.

[Jake Brill was the first Integrity PM at Facebook and Instacart](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j4gdMHYfHaE). Then he was at OpenAI.

That’s the type of archetype they’re looking for.

## 3\. How to Ace Each Type of AI PM Interview

Now that you understand what companies are looking for and why candidates fail, let’s get tactical.

I’m going to walk you through exactly how to ace each type of AI PM interview you’ll encounter. This section is the result of coaching dozens of candidates through these processes and seeing what actually works.

### 3a. Behavioral

The behavioral interview is where most AI PM candidates lose out. Not because they lack experience, but because they don’t know how to frame it correctly.

### Mastering “Tell Me About Yourself” for AI PM Roles

This is the first impression. Mess it up and you’re fighting uphill the rest of the interview.

Here’s what candidates get wrong: They give a chronological career history. “I started at Company X, then moved to Company Y, then...”

Boring. Ineffective. Doesn’t land the AI PM positioning.

#### The Right Structure: The AI PM Origin Story

Your “tell me about yourself” needs to accomplish three things:

1. **Establish AI credibility** - Show you have real production AI experience
2. **Demonstrate the right type of PM** - Prove you’re the AI/Technical/Platform PM they need
3. **Connect to this specific company** - Explain why you’re here and why now

Here’s the framework I coach candidates on:

Hook → AI Inflection Point → Proof Points → Why Here

#### Part 1 - The Hook (15 seconds)

Start with your current state and AI focus. Not your first job out of college.

Good example: *“I’m an AI PM who’s spent the last 4 years building ML-powered products at scale - most recently leading recommendation systems at Spotify that serve 500M users.”*

Bad example: *“I graduated from Stanford in 2018 and started my career as a software engineer...”*

#### Part 2 - AI Inflection Point (30 seconds)

Explain when and why you became focused on AI. This is your origin story.

Good example: *“My inflection point was 3 years ago at Airbnb. I was a traditional PM working on search when we started experimenting with ML-based ranking. I got fascinated by how dramatically it improved our metrics - 15% increase in bookings - but also how different the development process was. I realized this was going to reshape all of product management, so I went deep. I took fast.ai, started reading papers weekly, and transitioned to leading AI-native products.”*

This shows:

- Specific moment of conversion to AI
- Concrete impact that motivated you
- Initiative in learning (not just stumbled into it)
- Strategic thinking about where the field is going

#### Part 3 - Proof Points (45 seconds)

Now deliver 2-3 concrete examples of AI products you’ve shipped. This is where you establish credibility.

Good example: *“Since then, I’ve shipped three major AI products. First, at Airbnb, I led the personalized pricing model that increased host earnings by $200M annually. The technical challenge was balancing supply and demand in real-time across 7M listings. Second, at my current role at Spotify, I rebuilt our recommendation engine using a two-tower neural network, which improved streaming hours by 12%. And third, I recently launched an LLM-powered playlist description generator that’s now used by 50M users monthly. Each of these required different AI approaches - classical ML, deep learning, and now LLMs.”*

Notice what this does:

- Names specific products, not just companies
- Quantifies business impact
- Shows technical range (classical ML → deep learning → LLMs)
- Demonstrates scale
- Proves you ship, not just strategize

#### Part 4 - Why Here (30 seconds)

Connect to the specific company. This is where most candidates fumble.

Bad example: *“I’m excited about Anthropic because you’re doing cutting-edge AI work and I want to be part of it.”*

Good example: *“I’m here because Anthropic is solving the problem I care most about: making AI systems that are actually safe and reliable at scale. In my current role, I’ve seen firsthand how fragile LLM products are - hallucinations, bias, misuse. The technical work you’re doing on Constitutional AI and RLHF is exactly what’s needed to make AI products trustworthy. And specifically, I’m excited about this role because it’s focused on Applied AI - taking research breakthroughs and turning them into products that millions use, which is exactly what I’ve been doing for 4 years.”*

This shows:

- You understand the company’s mission (not just “cutting-edge AI”)
- You have a personal connection to the problem
- You’ve researched their specific technical approaches
- You connect your experience to their needs

#### The Complete Example

Here’s how it sounds all together:

*“I’m an AI PM who’s spent the last 4 years building ML-powered products at scale - most recently leading recommendation systems at Spotify that serve 500M users.*

*My inflection point was 3 years ago at Airbnb. I was working on search when we started experimenting with ML-based ranking. The 15% increase in bookings showed me that AI was going to reshape all of product management. So I went deep - took fast.ai, started reading papers, and transitioned to leading AI-native products.*

*Since then, I’ve shipped three major AI products: personalized pricing at Airbnb that increased host earnings by $200M annually, recommendation systems at Spotify that improved streaming hours by 12%, and an LLM-powered playlist generator used by 50M users monthly. Each required different approaches - classical ML, deep learning, and LLMs.*

*I’m here because Anthropic is solving the problem I care most about: making AI systems safe and reliable at scale. I’ve seen how fragile LLM products are. Your work on Constitutional AI is exactly what’s needed. And this Applied AI role - taking research and turning it into products millions use - is exactly what I’ve been doing.”*

Total time: 2 minutes. Hits all the marks.

### The Critical Principle: Flipping Weaknesses Into Strengths

Now here’s the thing most candidates miss: **every answer in your behavioral interview needs to flip a potential weakness into a strength.**

This isn’t a standalone question. It’s a technique you apply to every single behavioral answer.

Here’s the technique: Every STAR story should have a sentence that preemptively addresses a weakness.

Example behavioral question: *“Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult trade-off.”*

Bad answer (doesn’t flip weakness): *“At my last company, we had to choose between model accuracy and latency. We chose latency because users were churning. It worked out.”*

Good answer (flips “not technical enough” weakness): *“At Spotify, we faced a trade-off between recommendation accuracy and serving latency. Our ML team wanted to deploy a transformer-based model with 2% better accuracy but 200ms higher latency. I dove into the technical details - studied the architecture, understood why it was slower, and analyzed our latency data showing p95 was already at 400ms. Users were churning due to slow load times. I pushed back on the ML team and proposed we first optimize the existing model architecture. I worked with an ML engineer to profile the code, found we could optimize the embedding layer, and got latency down to 250ms while maintaining accuracy. This showed the team I could think technically while keeping user needs front and center.”*

See what happened? The answer demonstrates:

- Technical fluency (transformer architecture, embedding layers, profiling)
- Ability to push back on ML teams with data
- User-centric thinking
- Actually solved a hard problem

Every answer should do this.

### Case Type 1: AI Product Sense

**Format:** Design an AI feature for \[Product X\] / Build an AI solution for \[Problem Y\]

**Example questions:**

- Design an AI feature to help ChatGPT users write better code
- How would you use AI to improve Google Maps navigation?
- Build an AI-powered study tool for Khan Academy
- Design an AI solution to communicate with pets

#### The Framework: AI-CUPS-PDM

This is my CUPS-PDM framework adapted for AI products:

- **A - AI Applicability:** Assess if AI is the right solution (Don’t skip this!)
- **C - Clarify:** Understand the prompt and company context
- **U - User:** Identify segments and prioritize
- **P - Problem:** Define user problems
- **S - Solutions:** Brainstorm AI and non-AI solutions
- **P - Prioritize:** Choose best solution with AI-specific considerations
- **D - Design:** Describe AI interaction model and UX
- **M - Measurement:** AI-specific metrics + product metrics

**The “A” is Critical**

The biggest mistake is forcing AI into the solution. Start by asking:

- Is this a problem AI can solve better than alternatives?
- Do we have (or can we get) the training data?
- Is the required accuracy achievable?
- Will users trust an AI solution here?
- What’s the cost-benefit?

If yes, proceed. If no, explain why and propose alternatives.

#### Example Walkthrough

**Prompt:** “Design an AI feature to help ChatGPT users write better code.”

**A - AI Applicability** (1 minute) *“Before diving in, let me assess whether AI is appropriate here. Code writing is a strong AI application because we have massive training data from GitHub and Stack Overflow, the problem is well-defined - syntactically correct, functionally accurate code - users already accept AI assistance in this domain, and the cost-benefit is strong since developer time is expensive. So yes, AI makes sense here.”*

**C - Clarify** (1 minute) *“We’re enhancing ChatGPT’s code writing capabilities. OpenAI’s mission is ensuring AGI benefits humanity - one way is making developers more productive. Is that the right frame? And how much time do we have for this case?”*

**U - User** (2 minutes) *“User segments for code writing:*

- *Professional developers (daily use)*
- *Junior developers (learning)*
- *Non-developers (trying to build something)*
- *Data scientists/analysts (occasional coding)*

*I’ll focus on professional developers - largest TAM, highest willingness to pay, strategic for OpenAI. Sound good?”*

**P - Problem** (3 minutes) *“Key problems professional developers face:*

1. *Code works initially but breaks on edge cases*
2. *Hard to iterate - have to re-explain changes*
3. *Doesn’t follow team style/conventions*
4. *No IDE integration*
5. *Can’t verify correctness before using*

*Most acute is #1 - edge case failures. This erodes trust and wastes debugging time. Let me focus there.”*

**S - Solutions** (4 minutes) *“Potential solutions - both AI and non-AI:*

*AI solutions:*

- *AI-powered test generation for edge cases*
- *Adversarial testing - separate model tries to break the code*
- *Fine-tuned model on production-tested code*
- *Multi-agent system - one writes, one reviews*

*Non-AI solutions:*

- *Static analysis tool integration*
- *Community voting on code quality*

*Most promising: multi-agent approach. Mirrors how real dev teams work.”*

**P - Prioritize** (2 minutes) *“I’d prioritize the multi-agent system because:*

- *High impact: Could reduce edge case failures 60-80%*
- *Feasible: We have model infrastructure*
- *Cost-effective: 2x inference acceptable for code*
- *Differentiated: Competitors aren’t doing this*
- *Extensible: Same infrastructure for other reviews*

*Trade-offs: Higher latency (2-3x) and cost (2x inference). But developers will wait for quality.”*

**D - Design** (5 minutes) *“Here’s how it works:*

*User experience:*

1. *User requests code as normal*
2. *Writer agent generates code (2-3 sec)*
3. *Reviewer agent evaluates against failure modes (2 sec)*
4. *If issues found, writer revises (2 sec)*
5. *User sees: final code + review summary + test cases*

*Key design choices:*

- *Make review visible so users understand quality*
- *Allow skipping review for simple requests*
- *Show what edge cases were considered*
- *Let users provide constraints to reviewer*

*Interaction model: Async batch with progressive disclosure. Users see initial code quickly, then reviewed version.”*

**M - Measurement** (3 minutes) *“Success metrics:*

*Primary:*

- *Code correctness: % passing user test suites*
- *Developer satisfaction: Post-use survey*
- *Feature adoption: % enabling it*

*AI-specific:*

- *Review catch rate: % times reviewer finds issues*
- *False positive rate: % times flags non-issues*
- *Revision improvement: Does revision improve correctness?*

*Guardrails:*

- *Latency: Keep p95 under 10 seconds*
- *Cost: Under $0.20 per request*
- *Churn: Ensure no increase*

*I’d A/B test with 5% of Pro users for 2 weeks.”*

**What Makes This Strong:**

- Questioned whether AI was right before assuming
- Connected to company mission
- Brainstormed multiple AI approaches
- Explained technical trade-offs
- Designed interaction model, not just feature
- Included AI-specific metrics

Interviewer: “Nothing significant.”

### Case Type 3: AI Product Design

**Format:** Design the user experience for \[AI Feature\]

**Example questions:**

- Design the UI for an AI code completion feature
- How would you design the experience for AI-generated images?
- Design how users would interact with an AI scheduling assistant
- Create the UX for an AI writing tool

#### The Framework: AI Design CUPS-PDM

Same as AI Product Sense, but much heavier emphasis on the “D” (Design) portion. You should spend 40% of your time on design choices.

**Key AI Design Principles:**

1. **Make AI confidence visible** - Users need to know when AI is uncertain
2. **Design for failure** - AI will fail. How do users recover?
3. **Enable human override** - Never trap users in AI decisions
4. **Show the AI’s reasoning** - Help users understand why
5. **Progressive disclosure** - Don’t overwhelm with AI magic

#### Example Walkthrough

**Prompt:** “Design the user experience for AI code completion in an IDE.”

I’ll skip the CUPS parts (you know these) and focus on the D - Design section where AI Product Design cases differ.

**D - Design** (This is where you spend most time)

*“Let me walk through the end-to-end user experience and key design decisions.*

*Core Interaction Model:* *I’d use inline ghost text that appears as you type - similar to GitHub Copilot. This is the least disruptive model for developers’ flow state.*

*But here are the AI-specific design choices that matter:*

*1\. Confidence Signaling* *Ghost text should have visual indicators of AI confidence:*

- *High confidence (>90%): Solid gray text*
- *Medium confidence (70-90%): Dotted gray text*
- *Low confidence (<70%): Don’t show, or show with warning icon*

*Why: Developers need to know when to trust the AI. We learned from user research that wrong suggestions kill trust more than no suggestions.*

*2\. Multi-Suggestion Browsing* *When AI generates multiple viable options:*

- *Show “3 more suggestions” pill*
- *Tab to cycle through alternatives*
- *Each shows confidence and brief reasoning*

*Why: Code has multiple correct approaches. Let users choose.*

*3\. Contextual Awareness Display* *Show what context the AI used:*

- *Files it referenced*
- *Functions it considered*
- *Patterns it matched*

*Access via Cmd+K on the suggestion. Helps users understand AI reasoning and debug when it’s wrong.*

*4\. Failure Recovery* *When AI generates bad code:*

- *Thumbs down button → opens feedback form*
- *“Ignore this file” option to turn off AI here*
- *Easy undo that remembers state*

*Why: AI will fail. Make recovery fast and learning continuous.*

*5\. Learning Preferences* *AI learns from:*

- *Which suggestions accepted/rejected*
- *User edits to AI code*
- *Team coding standards (if shared)*

*Show “AI is learning your style” indicator when this happens. Makes it feel collaborative, not dictatorial.*

*6\. Progressive Disclosure* *New users:*

- *See only high-confidence inline suggestions*
- *Tooltip on first use explaining how to accept (Tab)*
- *Gradual introduction of advanced features*

*Power users:*

- *Can adjust confidence threshold*
- *Enable multi-line generation*
- *Access full suggestion history*

*Why: Don’t overwhelm new users with AI complexity.*

*7\. Performance Optimization* *Design for latency:*

- *Show suggestion within 200ms*
- *If taking longer, show “AI thinking...” state*
- *Cache common patterns*
- *Prefetch likely next suggestions*

*Why: Anything over 200ms breaks flow state.*

*Key Design Trade-offs:*

*Trade-off 1: Inline vs sidebar suggestions* *Decision: Inline. Sidebar breaks flow, even though it could show more context.*

*Trade-off 2: Always show vs selective suggestions* *Decision: Selective (only high confidence). Builds trust through precision over recall.*

*Trade-off 3: Single vs multiple suggestions by default* *Decision: Single with easy access to alternates. Don’t overwhelm.*

*Wireframe:* *(At this point, I’d screen share and quickly sketch the key states in a tool or even just a doc)*

*These design choices make the difference between an AI feature users love versus one they disable.”*

**What Makes This Strong:**

- Specific interaction model choices
- Explained AI-specific design principles (confidence, failure recovery)
- Addressed both new and power users
- Thought through edge cases and failure states
- Made explicit trade-offs with reasoning
- Connected to underlying technical constraints (latency)

### Case Type 5: AI Metrics

**Format:** What success metrics would you set for \[AI Product\]? / How would you measure \[AI Feature\]?

**Example questions:**

- Define success metrics for ChatGPT
- How would you measure a recommendation system?
- What metrics matter for an AI image generator?
- Set goals for an AI writing assistant

#### The Framework: AI Metrics Framework

- **C - Clarify:** Product, users, value proposition
- **N - North Star:** The one metric that matters most
- **B - Breakdown:** MECE breakdown of north star
- **A - AI-Specific:** Model performance metrics
- **G - Guardrails:** Counter-metrics and safety
- **T - Trade-offs:** How metrics might conflict

Remember it as: **C-NABGT**

#### Example Walkthrough

**Prompt:** “Set success metrics for GitHub Copilot.”

**C - Clarify** (2 min) *“GitHub Copilot is an AI pair programmer that suggests code as you type. Primary value is making developers more productive. Primary user is professional developers. Company goal is building the default development environment. Is that right?”*

Interviewer: “Yes.”

**N - North Star** (3 min) *“For north star metric, I’d choose: **Code Acceptance Rate***

*Why:*

- *Directly measures value (developers accepting AI suggestions)*
- *Leading indicator of retention and engagement*
- *Actionable (we can improve model to increase it)*
- *Captures the core product promise*

*Alternative consideration was “Developer Time Saved” but that’s harder to measure accurately. Acceptance rate is a strong proxy.*

*Does that seem right?”*

**B - Breakdown** (4 min) *“Let me break down Code Acceptance Rate MECE:*

*Code Acceptance Rate = (Suggestions Shown) × (Acceptance Rate per Suggestion)*

*Suggestions Shown depends on:*

- *Active users (MAU/DAU)*
- *Coding sessions per user*
- *Suggestions per session*

*Acceptance Rate per Suggestion depends on:*

- *Suggestion quality (how good is the code)*
- *Suggestion relevance (does it match intent)*
- *Presentation (how it’s shown)*

*So our metric tree:*

```markup
Code Acceptance Rate
├── User Engagement
│   ├── Monthly Active Users
│   ├── Sessions per User
│   └── Suggestions per Session
└── Suggestion Quality  
    ├── Relevance (matches user intent)
    ├── Correctness (code works)
    └── Preference (user’s style)
```

*This gives us clear levers to pull.”*

**A - AI-Specific Metrics** (4 min) *“Beyond product metrics, we need AI-specific metrics:*

*Model Performance:*

- *Offline accuracy: How often model predicts next token correctly*
- *Pass@k: Given k suggestions, probability at least one is correct*
- *Human eval score: Expert developers rate suggestion quality*

*Model Efficiency:*

- *Latency: p50, p95, p99 suggestion generation time*
- *Cost per suggestion: Inference cost*
- *Cache hit rate: How often we can serve from cache*

*Model Behavior:*

- *Hallucination rate: How often model generates invalid code*
- *Bias: Does it perform equally across languages/frameworks*
- *Recency: Does it suggest outdated patterns*

*These inform product metrics but need separate tracking.”*

**G - Guardrails** (3 min) *“Critical counter-metrics:*

*Quality Guardrails:*

- *Security vulnerability rate: AI shouldn’t suggest exploitable code*
- *License violation rate: Don’t copy GPL code into MIT projects*
- *Personally identifiable information: Don’t leak PII in suggestions*

*User Experience Guardrails:*

- *Developer satisfaction (survey)*
- *Disable rate: How many users turn it off*
- *Noise complaints: Are suggestions annoying*

*Business Guardrails:*

- *Churn: Are we losing subscribers*
- *Support tickets: Are we creating problems*

*We should never sacrifice these for acceptance rate.”*

**T - Trade-offs** (3 min) *“Key metric trade-offs:*

*1\. Acceptance Rate vs Quality* *We could show more suggestions (increase acceptance) but annoy users with noise. Need to optimize for acceptance of high-quality suggestions only.*

*2\. Latency vs Accuracy* *Better models take longer. Users need sub-200ms. We’ll cap at p95 < 200ms even if it means slightly lower accuracy.*

*3\. Coverage vs Precision* *Show suggestions for everything vs only when confident. I’d optimize for precision - better to show nothing than wrong code.*

*4\. Current Users vs Growth* *Optimizing acceptance rate for power users might make onboarding harder for new users. Need separate metrics for cohorts.*

*My recommended balance: Prioritize quality and trust over quantity of suggestions.”*

**Summary** (1 min) *“To summarize:*

- *North Star: Code Acceptance Rate*
- *Key drivers: User engagement × Suggestion quality*
- *AI metrics: Latency, accuracy, cost*
- *Guardrails: Security, PII, user satisfaction*
- *Trade-offs: Always favor quality over quantity*

*I’d track this weekly in our product reviews and set quarterly goals for improvement.”*

**What Makes This Strong:**

- Clear north star with reasoning
- MECE breakdown showing levers
- Separated AI metrics from product metrics
- Thought through counter-metrics
- Explicitly addressed trade-offs
- Actionable recommendation

## Nailing the Presentation

Whether you’re presenting homework or doing a live presentation round, these principles matter most.

#### Principle 1 - Send Pre-Read 48 Hours Early

Always send materials in advance.

Email:

> Subject: Presentation Materials for \[Date\]
> 
> Hi \[Name\], looking forward to our conversation. I’ve attached my materials for review.
> 
> I’ll spend 20-25 minutes on key points, then open for discussion.
> 
> Let me know if you’d like me to focus on any particular areas.

Benefits: they come prepared, you don’t read bullets, enables deeper discussion.

#### Principle 2 - Design for Discussion

Don’t monologue for 30 minutes then ask for questions.

Build in pause points:

- “Before I move on - does this prioritization align with how you see it?”
- “I’ve assumed X about your users. Does this match your data?”
- “I’d love your thoughts on this approach before diving deeper.”

Real strategic meetings are conversations, not presentations.

#### Principle 3 - Master Executive Presence

Opening: “Thanks for your time. I’m excited to share my thinking on \[topic\]. I’ll cover three areas: \[X, Y, Z\], then open for discussion.”

Meta-narration: “Let me pause here before the next section...” “This is the key insight that informed my strategy...” “I’ll return to this when we discuss metrics...”

Handling pushback: “Great point I hadn’t fully considered. Let me think...” \[pause\] “Here’s how I’d integrate that feedback...”

#### Principle 4 - Know Your Numbers Cold

If challenged on data (you will be):

“I estimated \[X\] based on \[Y\] and \[Z\] assumptions. If those shift, the number changes. What assumptions does your team use?”

Shows: data-driven thinking, humility about uncertainty, collaborative approach.

#### Principle 5 - Have Backup Material

Build an appendix with:

- Alternative approaches considered
- Deeper analysis on key points
- Customer quotes
- Technical deep dives

When tough questions come: “I anticipated this and prepared additional analysis. Happy to share screen now.”

#### Principle 6 - Carry Yourself at Next Level

Remember: someone interviewed for Manager and got offered Senior Manager because of how they presented.

Signals:

- Use “we” not “I” when discussing implementation
- Reference cross-functional collaboration naturally
- Ask insightful questions about their business
- Speak with conviction but stay open to feedback

The presentation is as much about executive presence as content.
