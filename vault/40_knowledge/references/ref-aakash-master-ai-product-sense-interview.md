## What Makes AI Product Sense Different?

If you’ve read my [complete guide to the product sense interview](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/master-the-product-sense-interview), you know the traditional format well. But [AI product sense](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-product-sense) adds **three critical layers** that *traditional product interviews don’t test*.

#### Layer 1: Model Considerations

You’re not just thinking about features. You’re thinking about how the underlying AI model impacts what’s possible.

Should you work with the research team on model improvements, or focus on application-level changes? Can the model even support what you’re proposing, or are you designing for a capability that doesn’t exist yet?

#### Layer 2: AI Safety & Ethics

Every AI product decision has safety implications.

- How do you prevent misuse?
- What about bias (or debate about bias) in your outputs?
- How do you handle edge cases where AI could cause harm?

These aren’t nice-to-haves - they’re core to the product strategy at companies like Anthropic and OpenAI.

#### Layer 3: Unique AI Metrics

Traditional [product metrics](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/product-metrics-interview) still matter, but AI products require new measurement approaches:

- How do you measure hallucination rates?
- What about model performance versus user satisfaction?
- How do you know if your AI feature is actually solving the problem it claims to solve?

## 1\. The Key Takeaways: Everything You Need To Remember

Before we get into all the details by question type, I want to highlight 10 things for you from the mock to do yourself.

### Takeaway 1 - Build a Unique, Case-Specific Framework

Don’t use the same framework for every question. Create a unique structure based on the specific prompt.

In the mock interview, I took a moment to build this framework on a Miro board:

1. OpenAI mission, vision & strategy
2. Key users
3. User problems
4. Creative solutions
5. Prioritization
6. Metrics & guardrails

This wasn’t CIRCLES. It wasn’t a memorized framework. It was custom-built for the specific question: growing ChatGPT image generation with tight constraints.

Notice what I customized for AI context:

- I added “mission, vision & strategy” because at AI companies, understanding how features fit into the AGI roadmap matters
- I made “metrics & guardrails” one section because safety isn’t optional
- I separated user problems into UI, functionality, and research model

**The setup looks like:**

Interviewer: *Asks question* You: *Clarifying questions* You: “Can I take a minute to structure my response?” *Takes 1-2 minutes* You: “Here’s the framework I want to use: \[Your custom framework\]. Does that work given our time?”

This shows you’re not just memorizing frameworks from Lewis Lin. You’re thinking strategically about how to approach THIS specific problem.

### Takeaway 2 - Check In With Your Interviewer (A Lot)

The best product sense interviews aren’t monologues. They’re discussions.

In my mock, I checked in 17 times during 45 minutes:

- “Does that sound right?”
- “What do you think?”
- “Should I keep going or would you like me to go deeper here?”
- “Am I on the right track?”
- “How is everything progressing so far? Are we using time well?”

This accomplishes three things:

First, it shows you’re collaborative. You work WITH stakeholders, not AT them.

Second, it keeps you on track. When Bart said “by now I’d be expecting you to be more in a prioritization mode,” I immediately adjusted. I didn’t defend my approach. I didn’t finish my thought. I said “You’re right. We should be heading to prioritization.”

Third, it demonstrates executive presence. You’re comfortable leading but also taking input.

One critical moment: When I proposed my framework, Bart said it “felt analytical.” I immediately added a section on user problems to show I wasn’t neglecting the qualitative side.

That flexibility matters more than having the perfect framework from the start.

### Takeaway 3 - Connect Everything Back to Your Unique Experience

Relate your answer back to your experience and knowledge to show your unicorn candidate-market fit.

Throughout the interview, I wove in:

- Gaming background: “I worked at Fortnite with 80M weekly users, similar scale to this”
- Google Cloud experience: “At Google Workspace with 600M users, I learned that not all users are equal”
- Apollo.io insights: “We doubled activation conversion rate by focusing on loading screens”

This isn’t showing off. It’s providing credibility for your recommendations.

When I suggested improving the loading UX, I backed it up with specific experience: “In gaming, we thought a lot about load screens. They should be fun, rewarding, headed to your goal. The ChatGPT thinking UX isn’t any of those things. At Apollo, we mapped time spent by screen and found everybody’s spending time on the loading screen.”

The key is making these connections naturally, not forced. You’re showing the interviewer that you’ve seen similar problems before and you know what actually works.

If you’ve never worked at scale, talk about side projects. Talk about products you use religiously. Show you’re a product thinker, not just someone who memorized case frameworks.

### Takeaway 4 - Use Visual Tools To Organize Your Thinking

I built everything in Miro during the interview. Bart specifically called this out in feedback: “I wouldn’t do as good as you because I wouldn’t do the miro board, which is an awesome learning for me to make it visual.”

Visual frameworks do three things:

**First, they help you organize your own thinking in real-time.** Instead of linear note-taking, you’re building a mind map that shows relationships between ideas.

**Second, they make it easier for the interviewer to follow your logic.** They can see where you’ve been and where you’re going.

**Third, they show you think in terms of systems, not just lists.** When I connected “user problems” to “solutions” to “prioritization,” it showed I understood the flow.

Use Miro, Figma, Excalidraw, or even just Google Drawings. Anything that lets you build a mind map live.

If you’re in person and there’s a whiteboard, use it. Draw boxes, arrows, frameworks. Make it visual.

### Takeaway 5 - Demo The Product Mid-Interview

When discussing UI problems, I literally opened ChatGPT and generated an image live:

“Let’s just go through the process together so we can look at an end-to-end UI evaluation. I’m going to generate an image of me speaking on stage.”

Then I walked through the actual experience:

- “I hit create image”
- “It’s showing this timer... one minute 40 seconds seems high”
- “I’m afraid to click ‘see thinking’ because I might skip the loading”
- “The counter stopped at 28 seconds but it’s still working”

This did two things:

First, it showed I actually USE the product. I’m not just theorizing from blog posts.

Second, it grounded the conversation in real user experience. My user problems became instantly concrete.

Most candidates would never think to do this. They’d describe the UX from memory or theory.

Actually demonstrating it made my recommendations 10x more credible.

### Takeaway 6 - Separate Model-Layer From Application-Layer Explicitly

This is the #1 thing that distinguishes AI product sense from traditional product sense.

In the mock, I created three explicit buckets:

- UI of app
- Functionality of app
- Research model

Then I clarified: “You have a 50 person research team on the model. When you say three engineers, you’re talking about the application engineers, right?”

This showed I understand:

- What I can control (product, UX, features)
- What research controls (model quality, speed, capabilities)
- How to work between both teams

Most candidates blur these lines. They’ll say “we should improve the model” without acknowledging that takes 6+ months and a research team.

Or they’ll only focus on product-layer solutions and ignore that sometimes the model IS the bottleneck.

The best answers show you can operate at both layers.

### Takeaway 7 - Address Safety Proactively, Not When Asked

Even under time pressure, I said:

“We’re trying to create safe AI. We need to make sure people aren’t editing images and likeness of third parties - we need verification. We need to make sure people aren’t creating pornographic or dangerous images. We need red teaming and safety.”

I didn’t wait for Bart to ask “what about safety?”

At OpenAI and Anthropic, safety is core to the mission. If you get to minute 40 without mentioning it once, you’ve signaled you don’t understand AI product management.

The best candidates weave safety into solutions naturally:

- Designing an AI code assistant? Mention preventing malicious code
- Building an image editor? Talk about deepfake prevention
- Creating content recommendations? Discuss filter bubbles and misinformation

### Takeaway 8 - Prioritize Ruthlessly Based On Constraints

The interviewer WILL give you constraints. You WILL be tempted to ignore them and design the perfect solution.

Don’t.

In the mock: 3 engineers, 3 months, need to add 175M users.

I estimated every solution:

- UI changes: 1 week, 50M users
- Editing features: 1.5 months, 100M users
- Infographic editor: 3 weeks, 25M users
- Meme editor: 3 weeks, 25M users
- New app: 3 months, 25M users

Then I literally drew a line: “Above the line / below the line.”

Above the line: UI changes + editing features + infographic editor + meme editor = 200M users, fits in 3 months with 3 engineers

Below the line: Everything else

Then I showed my math: 50M + 100M + 25M + 25M ≈ 200M, which exceeds the 175M target.

This level of rigor separates strong candidates from weak ones.

### Takeaway 9 - Ask About Competitors When Relevant (And Use Them)

When Bart asked “have you tried other image AI solutions?”, most candidates would give a one-sentence answer.

I used it as an opportunity to analyze the competitive landscape. I literally added a “competitors” section to my framework on the fly and broke down:

- MidJourney: Better quality, more styles (research model opportunity)
- Runway: Better editing and selection tools (product opportunity)
- Synthesia: Better avatars that look real (product opportunity)

This served two purposes:

First, it showed I understand the market. I know what’s out there and what they’re good at.

Second, it informed my solutions. The editing features I prioritized came directly from seeing what Runway does well.

Use competitors to strengthen your answer, not just acknowledge them.

### Takeaway 10 - When Given a Curveball, Lean Into It

At one point, Bart asked: “If you had to create that app within three months, what would you do?”

This wasn’t part of my plan. I’d already deprioritized the app idea.

But instead of brushing it off, I leaned in. I took 2 minutes to fully spec it out:

“We’d create an Instagram competitor focused on authentic photos with AI editing. The MVP would be for women who want to authentically show themselves. Core features: AI zoom out for background fill, resolution enhancement, AI filters. We’d focus on this narrow user group and grow virally through sharing.”

Bart was testing if I could think on my feet and fully develop ideas under pressure.

The best candidates see curveballs as opportunities to demonstrate depth, not obstacles to avoid.

## Category 1: AI Product Improvement

Improvement questions test one thing: can you diagnose problems and fix them under real constraints?

Be surgical. Find the specific issue. Address it efficiently.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **Ignoring constraints.** You have 2 months and 5 engineers. They propose solutions needing 6 months and 15 engineers. Always size your work.
- **Staying vague.** “Improve the UX” means nothing. What specifically? How long? What’s the impact?
- **Forgetting the model layer.** AI products have two layers (model and product). Explicitly separate what you control from what research controls.
- **No prioritization.** Listing 10 ideas without drawing the line. Show your math. What’s above? What’s below?
- **Skipping the demo.** Actually open the product during the interview. Ground your answer in reality.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Diagnose → Separate Layers → Prioritize Bang-For-Buck**

This forces you to understand the problem before jumping to solutions. And it explicitly separates what you can control (product) from what research controls (model).

Most people overweight focus early on in the framework. Allocate your time like this:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qdkm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa94e1746-cab3-4040-970b-16a2e3ecb002_982x372.png)

And be sure to check in every 8-10 minutes.

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** Claude’s code generation has a 40% thumbs-down rate from non-technical users. You have 2 months and 5 engineers. What would you build?

#### Clarify and Demo

“Can I actually use Claude’s code generation? I want to see what a non-technical user experiences.”

Open Claude. Ask it to generate code for a simple task. “Create a function that adds up a list of numbers.”

Watch what happens. The code appears:

```markup
def calculate_total(arr):
    return sum(arr)
```

It works. But look at it as a non-technical person would. What’s `arr`? What’s `sum`? No explanation.

Now you understand the problem. The code isn’t wrong. Non-technical users don’t understand it.

Check in: “Does that match what you’re seeing in the data?”

#### Framework Setup

“Let me structure my approach:

- Segment non-technical users to find where the problem is worst
- Separate product layer from research layer
- Brainstorm solutions for both
- Prioritize based on impact and timeline
- Spec the top solution

Sound good?”

Draw this on your Miro board while you say it.

#### Diagnose and Segment

Break down “non-technical users”:

**Complete beginners (30%):** Don’t understand code at all. Need everything explained.

**Occasional coders (50%):** Can read code but don’t write regularly. Forget syntax.

**Technical PMs (20%):** Understand concepts but rusty on implementation.

Now run the math. If 40% overall give thumbs down, where’s it coming from?

Assume:

- Beginners: 80% thumbs down
- Occasional: 35% thumbs down
- Technical PMs: 10% thumbs down

Total: (30% × 80%) + (50% × 35%) + (20% × 10%) = 24% + 17.5% + 2% = 43.5%

Close enough to 40%. The math checks out.

**Insight: Beginners are 55% of the problem. Focus there.**

Check in: “Does this segmentation make sense?”

#### Separate Layers

Create three explicit buckets on your board:

**UI of app (Product team):**

- How code displays
- Explanation interface
- Error messaging

**Functionality (Engineering, 2 months):**

- Code explainer feature
- Interactive debugger
- Prompt templates

**Research model (Research team, 6+ months):**

- Model’s ability to explain reasoning
- Model’s ability to adjust complexity
- Model accuracy

Clarify: “You mentioned 5 engineers. They work on the application layer, right? Research team handles the model separately?”

Wait for confirmation. This shows you know what you control.

#### Solutions and Sizing

Size everything. Engineer-weeks and impact estimates:

**Prompt templates (est 1 week, 1 engineer)** Pre-written prompts for common tasks: “Create a function to...”, “Write code that...” Est impact: +15% satisfaction

**Plain English toggle (est 2 weeks, 2 engineers)** Button that says “Explain in plain English.” Replaces jargon with simple terms. Est impact: +10% satisfaction

**Code explainer (est 4 weeks, 3 engineers)** Interactive UI. Click any line, get an explanation. Shows what each section does. Est impact: +25% satisfaction

**Interactive debugger (est 6 weeks, 4 engineers)** Step through code line by line. See what happens at each step. Est impact: +20% satisfaction Problem: Too long. Blows the timeline.

#### Prioritize

Draw a literal line on your board:

**ABOVE THE LINE:** ✓ Prompt templates (1 week, +15%) ✓ Plain English toggle (2 weeks, +10%) ✓ Code explainer (4 weeks, +25%)

**Timeline check:** With 5 engineers working in parallel, we finish in 6 weeks. Fits in 2 months. ✓

**Impact check:** +15% + 10% + 25% = +50% improvement. Goal is reduce 40% thumbs-down by half (20 points). We exceed this. ✓

**BELOW THE LINE:** ✗ Interactive debugger (6 weeks, blows timeline)

Show the math. Make it visual.

Check in: “This prioritization make sense given our constraints?”

#### Spec Top Solution

Go deep on the code explainer:

**Interface:** Left side: Generated code with line numbers Right side: Explanation panel Click any line → explanation appears instantly

**Explanation content:** “This code does \[high-level goal\]” “Step-by-step: \[what happens\]” For each section: “Lines X-Y: \[What it does\] \[Why it matters\]”

**Example for** `def calculate_total(prices): return sum(prices)`**:** “This code calculates the total of all prices. It takes a list of prices and adds them together using Python’s built-in sum function.”

**Design decisions:** Use analogies (adding items in a shopping cart) Avoid jargon unless necessary Mobile-first design (many users on phone) Sticky explanation panel (stays visible while scrolling)

**Why this works:** Beginners get explanations without asking Occasional coders get reminders of what they forgot Technical PMs can quickly verify the code does what they think

#### Metrics and Safety

**Success metrics:** North star: Thumbs-up rate on code generation Baseline: 60% → Target: 80% Timeline: Within 2 months of launch

**Counter metrics:** Time to generate code (shouldn’t increase >10%) Technical user satisfaction (shouldn’t decrease) Code accuracy (shouldn’t decrease)

**Safety:** Explanations must be accurate (no hallucinations) Validation: tie explanations to actual code behavior Red teaming: test with non-technical users Quality bar: >95% explanation accuracy

Also: warnings for code that accesses sensitive files, makes network requests, or modifies system settings. Non-technical users might generate malicious code by accident.

## Category 2: AI Product Growth

Growth questions test whether you can hit aggressive growth targets despite AI-specific constraints.

The key is understanding the growth model. Where’s the bottleneck? What levers actually move the number?

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **Ignoring unit economics.** AI products have high inference costs. Growth that loses money on every user isn’t growth.
- **Generic growth tactics.** “We’ll do SEO and viral loops” without explaining how AI changes the playbook.
- **No bottleneck analysis.** They treat all parts of the funnel equally. Find where growth is actually blocked.
- **Forgetting AI constraints.** Compute costs, model quality, trust barriers. These limit how fast you can grow.
- **No math on the growth model.** They don’t show how tactics add up to hit the target number.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Define Target → Design Loops → Validate Economics**

This works because you need to know who you’re growing before you design how to grow them. Then check if the economics actually work.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oUoB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbfe1286f-2662-4175-b1d5-00f4fffbb5e6_988x336.png)

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** Perplexity has 10M MAU. You’re building an AI search competitor. What’s your growth strategy?

#### Clarify and Baseline

“Let me make sure I understand. Are we starting from zero users, or do we have some traction?”

Assume: Starting from zero.

“What’s our differentiation from Perplexity? Or are we figuring that out as part of this?”

Assume: You decide.

“Do we have information about Perplexity’s growth channels and what’s working for them?”

Assume: Public information only.

Check in: “Given we’re starting from zero against a 10M MAU competitor, I want to focus on finding a wedge rather than competing head-to-head. Does that make sense?”

#### Framework Setup

“I want to structure this around:

- Understanding Perplexity’s growth model to learn from it
- Identifying our differentiation and target users
- Brainstorming multiple growth loops and choosing the best ones
- Showing the unit economics work
- Defining success metrics

Sound good?”

Draw this on your board.

#### Understand Context

“Let me start by understanding how Perplexity grew:

**SEO:** Their AI-generated answer pages rank in Google for long-tail queries. Every answer is a landing page.

**Word of mouth:** People share answer links when they’re really good.

**Twitter presence:** Their founder is very active. Technical audience.

**Academic/tech early adopters:** People who care about citations.

Their model has really low CAC because the product creates its own distribution through SEO.

But they’ve hit challenges:

- Hard to grow beyond people who already care about ‘better search’
- High compute costs limit free usage
- Google is copying their features

So if we’re competing, we need to either win on distribution, win on product quality, or find a different wedge.”

Check in: “Does this match what you’re seeing in the market?”

#### Define Target

“Let me think about who uses AI search and where we could win.

**Potential user segments:**

Students (500M globally): Homework help, research papers. But low willingness to pay.

General consumers (3B): Googling random questions. Perplexity already owns this with SEO.

Knowledge workers (50M): Consultants, analysts, researchers doing deep research daily.

Developers (25M): Technical documentation, code examples. But GitHub Copilot and ChatGPT own this.

Let me do some math on opportunity:

If we go after general consumers, we’re competing directly with Perplexity’s strength (SEO) and Google (brand). Hard to win.

If we go after students, monetization is tough. Maybe $5/month max. Low LTV.

If we go after knowledge workers:

- 50M addressable market
- High willingness to pay ($20-30/month for productivity tools)
- Specific unmet need: research that needs multiple sources and synthesis
- Est LTV: $20/month × 3 years retention = $720

If we capture just 1% of knowledge workers (500K users), that’s roughly $10M MRR.

**I’d focus on knowledge workers.** Specifically: consultants, analysts, researchers.

Their job-to-be-done:

- Find information across multiple sources
- Compare different perspectives
- Export research in usable formats
- Trust the sources

Perplexity gives you one answer. We’d give you a research brief with multiple perspectives. That’s the wedge.”

Check in: “Does this segmentation make sense?”

#### Design Growth Loops

“Let me brainstorm possible growth loops:

**Option 1: Content Loop (SEO)** Every research session creates a shareable research brief. These briefs rank for ‘\[topic\] analysis.’ People find the brief → sign up. Pro: Low CAC, compounds over time, worked for Perplexity Con: Takes 6+ months to see results, Google could deprioritize AI content

**Option 2: Workspace Loop (Viral)** Users collaborate on research projects. Share with colleagues → they get invited. Pro: K-factor could be high in team environments Con: Requires building collaboration features, might not be viral enough

**Option 3: Integration Loop (Distribution)** Build into Notion, Slack, Google Docs. Search from within their workspace. Pro: Ride on existing distribution Con: Hard to get featured, users might not discover it

**Option 4: Referral Program** Give users credits for inviting colleagues. Pro: Can control the economics Con: Requires budget, feels transactional, doesn’t work well for B2B

**Option 5: Community Loop** Build a community of researchers sharing best practices. Pro: High engagement, brand building Con: Takes time to build, doesn’t directly drive growth

**Option 6: Sales-Led** Direct sales to consulting firms, law firms, research teams. Pro: High contract values, predictable Con: Long sales cycles, expensive, doesn’t scale

Let me evaluate based on: speed to impact, scalability, cost, and fit with our product.

**I’d prioritize three:**

**Content Loop (SEO)** because it’s proven for AI search and has the lowest CAC. The AI output itself creates distribution.

**Workspace Loop (Viral)** because knowledge workers operate in teams. If one consultant uses it, their whole team needs it. Natural K-factor.

**Integration Loop (Distribution)** because these users already work in Notion and Slack. Meet them where they are.

Skip referral (doesn’t work for B2B), skip community (too slow), skip sales-led (we’re early stage, need to prove product first).

These three compound:

- SEO brings cold traffic
- Workspace makes them invite teammates
- Integrations embed us in daily workflow”

Check in: “Do these three make sense as priorities?”

#### Validate Economics

“Quick check on whether this works economically:

**Cost side:** Free users cost us maybe $0.20/month (limited searches, est $0.02 per search) Paid users cost us maybe $8-12/month (heavier usage)

**Revenue side:** Free tier: 90% of users, $0 revenue Paid tier: 10% of users at $20/month

**Does it work?** At 10M users: 9M free users costing roughly $1.8M/month, 1M paid users generating $20M/month Gross margin: roughly 90%

The model works if we can get to 10% paid conversion. That’s the key metric to watch.

The risk: if compute costs are higher than estimated or paid conversion is lower than 10%, we’re burning cash.”

Check in: “Want me to go deeper on unit economics or move on?”

#### Define Metrics

“Let me think through what the north star should be:

**Option 1: Monthly Active Users (MAU)** Pro: Easy to track, shows scale, what investors want to see Con: Doesn’t measure value delivered. Someone could search once and never come back. Perplexity optimizes for this and it leads to shallow engagement.

**Option 2: Research Briefs Created per Week** Pro: Measures actual value creation, directly tied to our differentiation Con: Doesn’t account for quality. Could create junk briefs just to hit a number. Also doesn’t capture people who view briefs but don’t create.

**Option 3: Weekly Active Researchers (people who create at least one brief per week)** Pro: Balances engagement with value. Weekly usage = habit formation. Creating briefs = core action. Con: Might miss casual users who use it monthly but still get value. Might be too high a bar early on.

**Option 4: Time Saved per User per Week** Pro: Measures true impact, what users actually care about Con: Hard to measure accurately. Requires surveys. Can’t track in real-time.

**I’d pick Weekly Active Researchers as the north star.**

Here’s why: it captures people getting ongoing value (weekly = habit) and doing the core action (creating research). If someone creates research briefs every week, they’re probably paying or will pay.

The trade-off I’m making: optimizing for weekly researchers might exclude valuable monthly users. But I think that’s okay in the early days. Monthly users are probably tire-kickers. Weekly users are the core. Once we have 100K weekly active researchers, we can expand to monthly.

**Input metrics that drive it:**

- Organic search traffic to research briefs (SEO loop working)
- Brief view to signup conversion rate (top of funnel quality)
- Workspace invite acceptance rate (viral loop working)
- Integration install rate (distribution working)
- First brief creation rate (activation)

**Counter metrics to watch:**

- Cost per query (if this explodes above $0.10, unit economics break)
- Research brief quality measured by time spent reading (if this drops below 3 min, SEO stops working)
- Paid conversion rate (if this drops below 10%, we’re not monetizing)
- Weekly retention (if people stop coming back after first week, we have a retention problem)

The other decision: should we measure briefs created or briefs viewed? I’m choosing created because that’s where the value is. Viewing is passive. Creating means they trust us enough to use our research in their work.”

Check in: “Does this metrics framework make sense?”

## Category 3: AI Product Launch

Launch questions test whether you can build 0-to-1 with appropriate scoping for AI products.

The key is ruthless scoping. Most candidates try to build everything. You need to build the right thing.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **Building for everyone.** “People who schedule meetings” is too broad. Get specific.
- **Ignoring competitive whitespace.** They don’t explain where existing solutions fail or why AI matters.
- **Underscoping or overscoping.** Either the MVP is too small to be valuable or too big to ship in the timeline.
- **Forgetting AI risks.** Accuracy, trust, privacy, hallucination. Address these proactively.
- **No go-to-market plan.** They design the product but don’t explain how to get the first 100 users.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Define User + Problem → Find Whitespace → Identify AI Value → Scope MVP → Address Risks**

This works because you need extreme clarity on who and what before you can scope well. And AI products have unique risks you can’t ignore.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ni8y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F315a350b-3d3f-4e5b-93c4-063aba6151f7_988x376.png)

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** OpenAI wants to build an AI scheduling assistant. You have 8 months and a team of 10. What do you build?

#### Clarify

“When you say scheduling assistant, I want to make sure I understand the scope. Are we talking about:

- Coordinating meetings between people (like Calendly)?
- Managing your personal calendar (like a smart assistant)?
- Or something else?”

Assume: Coordinating meetings between people.

“Is this a standalone product or a feature within ChatGPT?”

Assume: Standalone product.

“What’s OpenAI’s strategic goal here? Are we trying to show off what AI can do? Or solve a real problem for users?”

Assume: Solve a real problem and show AI capabilities.

Check in: “Got it. So we’re building a standalone AI-powered meeting coordination tool in 8 months. Sound right?”

#### Framework Setup

“Given this is a 0-to-1 product launch, I want to structure around:

- Defining the specific user and their scheduling pain
- Understanding what exists and where the whitespace is
- Identifying where AI provides unique value vs traditional tools
- Scoping ruthlessly for an 8-month MVP
- Planning go-to-market for first 100 users

Sound good?”

Draw this on your board.

#### Define User and Problem

“Let me start by being very specific about who this is for.

**Potential users:**

Individual professionals: Schedule their own meetings. 5-10 meetings per week.

Executive assistants: Schedule for executives. 50-100+ meetings per week.

Sales teams: Coordinate demos with prospects. 20-30 meetings per week.

Recruiters: Coordinate interviews. 30-40 meetings per week.

Let me think about which has the biggest pain:

Individual professionals have pain, but it’s maybe 2-3 hours per week. Tools like Calendly mostly solve this.

Sales teams have pain, but they’re already using Calendly + CRM tools. Switching cost is high.

Recruiters have similar issues to sales.

Executive assistants have the most pain:

- 50-100+ meetings per week
- Juggle preferences across 5-10 executives
- Spend 10-15 hours per week on email tennis
- Evaluated on how efficiently they use executive time
- Current tools don’t handle complex preferences

**I’d focus on executive assistants at mid-large companies.**

Their specific problem: ‘I need to schedule a board meeting with 8 people across 3 timezones, considering that Sarah hates mornings, John refuses video calls before 2pm, the CEO won’t take meetings on Friday, and we need a room that fits 8 in our NY office.’

Traditional tools make them do this manually. AI could handle it.”

Check in: “Does focusing on executive assistants make sense?”

#### Find Whitespace

“Let me map out what exists:

**Calendly:**

- Great for 1:1 scheduling
- Fails for complex multi-person coordination
- No preference learning
- Whitespace: Can’t handle ‘Sarah hates mornings’ type constraints

**x.ai (shut down):**

- Tried AI scheduling but required too much email back-and-forth
- Never learned preferences well enough
- Whitespace: Couldn’t understand implicit preferences

**Motion:**

- Auto-schedules your tasks on calendar
- But doesn’t coordinate with others
- Whitespace: No collaborative scheduling

**Traditional assistants just use email:**

- Manual email tennis
- No intelligence
- Whitespace: Everything

The whitespace: AI that understands complex preferences, learns from past decisions, and negotiates on your behalf.”

#### Identify AI Value

“Here’s what AI can do that traditional software can’t:

**Learn implicit preferences from past decisions:**

- ‘You usually schedule Sarah’s 1:1s on Tuesday afternoons’
- ‘You never book the CEO on Mondays’
- ‘You prefer in-person for board meetings’

Traditional software requires you to manually configure every rule. AI learns by watching.

**Understand natural language constraints:**

- ‘Find a time that works for everyone except I need John there’
- ‘Move anything that’s not critical to make room for this’

No rules engine can handle this flexibility.

**Negotiate across calendars intelligently:**

- Evaluate who has the most flexibility
- Propose times that optimize for everyone’s preferences
- Automatically follow up when someone hasn’t responded

**Draft meeting communications:**

- Write the calendar invite with right details
- Send follow-ups in your tone
- Handle rescheduling communications

This is uniquely AI. You can’t rules-engine your way to this.”

Check in: “Does this make sense as the AI value prop?”

#### Scope MVP

“8 months, 10 people. Here’s what we build:

**IN SCOPE (MVP v1):**

Schedule meetings for one assistant and their executives (max 5 executives)

Handle 1:1 meetings and small group meetings (up to 5 people)

Learn preferences from past 50 meetings

Draft calendar invites

Email-based interface (assistant emails the AI, AI replies with options)

Read-only calendar access (AI suggests, assistant confirms)

**OUT OF SCOPE (v2+):**

Large group meetings (6+ people)

External guest scheduling

Room booking

Video conferencing links

Write access to calendar

Slack/Teams integration

Mobile app

**Why this scope?**

Email interface: Assistants already live in email. No new tools to learn. Can build in est 2 months.

Read-only: Builds trust. Assistants won’t adopt if AI puts things on calendar without approval. Can add write access in v2 once we prove it works.

Internal only: Simpler problem. External guests add complexity (different email domains, security concerns).

Small meetings: These are 80% of assistant’s time and easiest to get right.

This is shippable in 8 months and solves 60% of the problem.”

Check in: “Does this scope feel right for 8 months?”

#### Address Risks

“Here are the AI-specific risks we need to address:

**Accuracy:** If AI schedules wrong time, executives miss meetings. That’s catastrophic. Solution: Always show confidence scores. Require assistant approval. Never book automatically in v1.

**Trust:** Assistants won’t adopt if they don’t trust it. Solution: Show the reasoning. ‘I suggested Tuesday 2pm because Sarah is usually free then and it avoids John’s preferred deep work time.’

**Privacy:** We’re reading executives’ calendars and emails. Solution: Enterprise-only for now. Clear data handling policy. No training on customer data.

**Preference learning:** If AI learns wrong preferences, it gets worse over time. Solution: Let assistants correct preferences. ‘Actually, Sarah prefers mornings, not afternoons.’

**Hallucination:** AI might make up meeting details. Solution: Always pull from actual email thread. Never generate meeting details from scratch. Validate every fact against source data.”

#### Go-to-Market

“How we get first 100 customers:

**Months 1-2: Build alpha with 5 executive assistants**

- Recruit from our personal networks
- Get feedback on email interface
- Validate it actually saves time

**Months 3-5: Private beta with 50 assistants**

- Partner with one mid-size company (500-1000 employees)
- Prove it works for an entire assistant team
- Measure: Est 5+ hours saved per week per assistant

**Months 6-8: Launch to 100 companies**

- Sales motion: Direct to EA teams at companies
- Pricing: $50/month per assistant (ROI is obvious - saves est 20+ hours)
- Success metric: 80% of assistants use it for 10+ meetings per week

We’re not trying to grow fast yet. We’re trying to prove the value is real.”

Check in: “Does this go-to-market plan make sense?”

## Category 4: AI Product Design

Design questions test whether you can design specific AI experiences and interaction patterns.

The key is actually designing, not just talking about design. Show the screens. Walk through the flows.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **Jumping straight to design.** They pick one user and start designing without exploring alternatives.
- **Staying too conceptual.** “The UX should be intuitive” means nothing. Show the actual screens.
- **Ignoring AI-specific patterns.** Loading states, confidence indicators, iteration loops. These are different from traditional software.
- **No error handling.** They design the happy path but forget what happens when AI fails.
- **Forgetting the context.** Who’s using this? Where? On what device? At what time?

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Explore Users → Prioritize Problems → Design Solution → Handle Failures**

This works because you can’t design good UX without understanding who you’re designing for and what problem you’re solving. Most candidates skip straight to design and fail.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nkuW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2aa70c03-9833-4511-9cb9-3dbc325e8671_988x342.png)

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** Design the conversation flow for an AI therapist bot. What guardrails do you need?

#### Clarify

“When you say AI therapist bot, I want to understand the context:

- Is this a licensed therapy service or a mental wellness chatbot?
- Who’s the target user? Age range? Clinical diagnosis?
- Is this standalone or part of a larger health platform?
- What’s the relationship to human therapists? Replacement or complement?”

Assume: Mental wellness chatbot for adults dealing with stress and anxiety. Not a replacement for therapy. Part of a mental health app.

Check in: “So we’re designing a mental wellness chatbot, not clinical therapy. Right?”

#### Framework Setup

“I’ll structure this design around:

- Exploring potential user segments and picking one
- Identifying their key problems with mental health support
- Designing the conversation flow with screens
- Handling failure modes and crisis situations

For therapy, the failure modes are especially critical, so I’ll spend extra time there.”

Draw this on your board.

#### Explore Users

“Let me think about who uses mental health chatbots:

**Potential user segments:**

College students (20M in US): High stress, limited budgets, stigma around therapy.

Young professionals (50M): Work stress, relationship issues, can afford paid tools.

Parents with young kids (30M): Sleep-deprived, overwhelmed, no time for therapy.

Retirees (50M): Loneliness, health anxiety, unfamiliar with apps.

Let me think about where the opportunity is:

College students have high need but low ability to pay. Monetization is hard.

Young professionals have both need and ability to pay. They’re comfortable with apps. This is where companies like Calm and Headspace focus.

Parents have high need but limited time. Hard to build a habit if they only use it once a month.

Retirees have need but low tech literacy. Would need very different UX.

**I’d focus on young professionals aged 25-35.**

Specifically: people dealing with work stress and anxiety who want support but can’t commit to weekly therapy sessions.

Why this segment:

- 50M addressable market
- High willingness to pay ($10-20/month)
- Comfortable with app-based solutions
- Clear use case: need support between therapy sessions or instead of therapy”

Check in: “Does focusing on young professionals make sense?”

#### Prioritize Problems

“Now let me think about what problems this user has with mental health support:

**Problem 1: Can’t afford regular therapy** Weekly therapy costs $100-200/session. That’s $400-800/month. Most insurance doesn’t cover it well.

**Problem 2: Can’t get help when they need it** Therapy requires scheduling weeks in advance. If you have a crisis on Tuesday night, you wait until your Thursday appointment.

**Problem 3: Don’t know if they need therapy** Many people with anxiety don’t think it’s “bad enough” for therapy. They suffer in silence.

**Problem 4: Stigma around seeking help** Even in 2025, people worry about what others think. They don’t want their insurance to know.

Let me think about which problem is most acute:

Problem 1 (cost) is real, but there are cheaper options like group therapy or community mental health.

Problem 2 (timing) is the biggest gap. When you’re anxious at 11pm before a presentation, you need help NOW. Not in 3 days.

Problem 3 (don’t know if need therapy) is important but secondary. Once they try the app, this resolves.

Problem 4 (stigma) is declining and apps already solve this with privacy.

**I’d focus on Problem 2: Can’t get support when they need it.**

Specifically: User is stressed or anxious right now (11pm, before bed). They need to calm down. They can’t wait for their next therapy appointment.

This is where AI provides unique value. Always available. Instant support. Can reference past sessions.”

Check in: “Does solving for immediate support make sense?”

#### Design Solution

“Now let me design the conversation flow. I’m designing for this specific context:

- 26-year-old stressed about work presentation tomorrow
- 11pm, in bed, on phone
- Never used the app before
- Job-to-be-done: Calm down enough to sleep

The app opens to: ‘Hi, I’m here to listen. What’s on your mind tonight?’

User types: ‘I can’t sleep, I’m stressed about work’

AI waits 1-2 seconds (instant feels robotic), then responds: ‘That sounds really hard. Want to talk about what’s making you stressed?’

Design choice: Open-ended question. Not a symptom checklist. The delay makes it feel human. The show typing indicator ‘...’ so they know it’s thinking.

AI asks follow-ups to understand the problem:

- ‘What about the presentation is worrying you most?’
- ‘Have you felt this way before big presentations?’

Then AI reflects back: ‘It sounds like you’re worried you’ll forget what to say and look unprepared. Is that right?’

Design choice: Reflection is a core therapy technique. Shows the AI is actually listening.

When AI suggests an intervention, it explains its reasoning: ‘Based on what you shared about racing thoughts, breathing exercises tend to help. Would you like to try one?’

Design choice: Show confidence by explaining the reasoning. And ask permission. Don’t prescribe.

For the breathing exercise, AI guides them: ‘Let’s breathe in for 4 counts. Ready? 1... 2... 3... 4...’

Show a visual timer with dots filling up. After one round: ‘How are you feeling now?’

If better: ‘Great, let’s do two more rounds’

If not helping: ‘That’s okay, different techniques work for different people. Let’s try something else.’

Design choice: Iteration. Don’t force one solution. Adapt based on feedback.

At the end: ‘You did great. How are you feeling compared to when we started?’

Offer to save the conversation. Offer a reminder: ‘Want me to check in tomorrow after your presentation?’

Design choice: Memory and continuity. Not just a one-off chat. Next time they open the app, it remembers.

Total flow: Est 10-20 minutes from stressed to calmer.

Let me sketch the key screens:

**Opening screen:**

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│  [Back]                  [•]│
│                             │
│  Hi, I’m here to listen.    │
│  What’s on your mind        │
│  tonight?                   │
│                             │
│  ┌─────────────────────────┐│
│  │ [Type your message]     ││
│  └─────────────────────────┘│
│                             │
│  Safe space • Confidential  │
│  Crisis support • (988)     │
└─────────────────────────────┘
```

Exit button visible top right. Crisis number always visible at bottom. Warm but professional tone. Dark mode friendly since it’s 11pm.

**Active conversation:**

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│  [Back]                  [•]│
│                             │
│  You: I can’t sleep, I’m    │
│  stressed about work        │
│                             │
│  Therapist: That sounds     │
│  really hard. Want to talk  │
│  about what’s making you    │
│  stressed?                  │
│                             │
│  ... [thinking]             │
│                             │
│  ┌─────────────────────────┐│
│  │ [Type your response]    ││
│  └─────────────────────────┘│
└─────────────────────────────┘
```

Label says ‘Therapist’ not ‘AI’ or ‘Bot’. Makes it feel more human. Thinking indicator shows it’s processing thoughtfully. No timestamps. This isn’t instant messaging.

**Guided exercise:**

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│  [Back]                  [•]│
│                             │
│  Let’s try a breathing      │
│  exercise together          │
│                             │
│  ┌───────────────────────┐  │
│  │    Breathe in...      │  │
│  │                       │  │
│  │      [●●●●○○○○]      │  │
│  │                       │  │
│  │    4... 3... 2... 1  │  │
│  └───────────────────────┘  │
│                             │
│  [Follow along at your pace]│
│  [That was helpful] [Skip]  │
└─────────────────────────────┘
```

Visual, not just text. Progress dots fill up as they breathe. Option to skip if it’s not working. Immediate feedback buttons to guide the next step.”

Check in: “Does this design make sense for the use case?”

#### Handle Failures

“Now the critical safety guardrails. These are non-negotiable for a mental health app.

If user types anything like ‘I’m thinking about hurting myself’ or ‘I want to die’:

AI immediately shows: ‘I’m really concerned about what you just shared. I’m not equipped to help with thoughts of self-harm, but there are people who can. Can I connect you to a crisis counselor right now?’

Large button: ‘Call 988’ (crisis hotline) Secondary option: ‘Text with counselor’

DO NOT try to handle this in the chatbot. Escalate immediately. This is the most important guardrail.

If user says ‘I need medication for my depression’:

AI: ‘I can’t prescribe medication or diagnose conditions. I can help you find a licensed therapist who can evaluate whether medication would help. Would you like help finding someone?’

Clear boundary. Don’t give medical advice.

If user input is vague like ‘I just feel bad’:

AI: ‘I want to understand better. Is it more like sadness? Anxiety? Frustration? Or something else?’

Use clarifying questions. Don’t guess at their mental state.

If user requests something harmful like ‘Tell me how to manipulate my partner’:

AI: ‘I can’t help with that. Healthy relationships are built on honesty and respect. If you’re having relationship issues, I’d be happy to help you communicate better or work through your feelings.’

Redirect to healthy support.

After every session: ‘How helpful was this conversation?’ (1-5 stars)

If less than 3 stars: Flag for human review within 24 hours If user reports feeling worse: Immediate escalation to human therapist

The safety system has multiple layers:

Content filtering catches crisis keywords (’suicide,’ ‘hurt myself,’ ‘end it all’) and triggers crisis protocol automatically.

Human review for all flagged conversations within 24 hours. Random 10% sample reviewed weekly for quality.

Legal disclaimers every session: ‘This is not a replacement for professional therapy.’ Terms require parent consent for minors. HIPAA compliance for all data.”

Check in: “What other failure modes should I think about?”

#### Wrap Up

“To summarize the key design decisions:

We’re designing for young professionals who need immediate support when they’re stressed. The conversation feels human through pacing (1-2 second delays), reflection (’It sounds like...’), and memory (’Last time you mentioned...’).

The AI explains its reasoning (’Based on what you shared...’) to build trust. It asks permission before suggesting interventions. It adapts when something doesn’t work.

The screens are minimal and calming. Dark mode friendly. Exit always visible. Crisis support always accessible.

The safety guardrails are non-negotiable. Immediate escalation for self-harm. Clear boundaries on medical advice. Human review for quality.

This design solves the core problem: getting support when you need it, not three days later.”

## Category 5: AI Product Strategy

Strategy questions test whether you can think at the company level with multi-year horizons.

The key is making a choice. Strategy is about what you won’t do as much as what you will.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **No clear choice.** They try to do everything. Strategy requires picking a direction.
- **Ignoring competitive dynamics.** They don’t explain why their choice beats alternatives.
- **No trade-offs.** Every strategy has downsides. Show you understand them.
- **Staying too abstract.** “We should focus on enterprise” means nothing without explaining how.
- **No execution thinking.** Strategy without a path to execution is just theory.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Understand Position → Explore Options → Make Choice → Defend It**

This works because you can’t make strategic choices without understanding your starting position. And you can’t convince anyone without showing why your choice beats alternatives.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yLRC!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fafcd8acf-779a-45a3-9b23-aad79c6f1ce9_986x304.png)

Check in throughout. Strategy interviews are conversations, not presentations.

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** Google just announced Gemini will be free and integrated into all Google products. You’re the CPO of Perplexity. What’s your strategy?

#### Clarify

“Let me make sure I understand the situation. When did Google announce this?”

Assume: This week. Rolling out over the next month.

“And to clarify Perplexity’s current position - what’s our user base and business model?”

Assume: 10M monthly active users. Freemium model - free tier with limits, Pro at $20/month.

“Do we have early data on user reactions? Are people saying they’ll switch to Google?”

Assume: Social media shows concern. Some users questioning why they’d pay for Perplexity when Google is free.

“One more thing - what’s our strategic goal here? Maximize users? Maximize revenue? Get acquired?”

Assume: Maximize long-term enterprise value. Stay independent.

Check in: “Got it. So Google just made AI search free and integrated everywhere. We’re a 10M user startup competing against that. Let me think through what this means and how we should respond.”

#### Framework Setup

“I want to approach this by first understanding what Google’s move really signals. Then I’ll think about Perplexity’s unique position. From there, I’ll explore our strategic options - I think there are 3 or 4 realistic paths. Then I’ll make a clear recommendation.

Sound good?”

Draw this on your board.

#### Understand Position

“Let me start by reading Google’s move.

First, they’re betting that AI search is the future of search. This is them protecting their core business. They’re not trying to make money on AI - they’re trying to prevent someone else from taking search revenue.

Second, they’re using integration as their weapon. Gemini everywhere - Gmail, Docs, Chrome. That’s distribution we can’t match.

Third, free pricing signals they’re willing to subsidize this to win. They have search ads to fund it. We don’t.

Now let me think about Perplexity’s position. What do we actually have?

We have 10M users who chose us over Google. Why? Not because we’re integrated everywhere. Because we’re better at something specific. What is it?

I think it’s three things: We’re cleaner - no ads, no distractions. We show sources - transparency matters to researchers. We’re faster to new features - we shipped citations before anyone else.

But here’s what’s vulnerable: Google has infinite resources. If they decide our features matter, they’ll copy them in months. We can’t compete on resources or distribution.”

Check in naturally: “Does that read on Google’s move make sense, or am I missing something about their strategy?”

Interviewer: “What do you think they’re most worried about?”

“I think they’re worried that a new company could do to Google Search what Google Search did to Yahoo. Take the market before they react. So this is defensive. They’re making sure no startup can get big enough to threaten them. That means we need to find a path they can’t easily block.”

#### Explore Options

“Let me think through our strategic options.

**Option 1: Fight Google on AI search.**

We keep doing what we’re doing - better AI search than Google. We compete on quality and win users who care about the best experience.

Why this could work: We’re actually better than Google at AI search right now. We’re faster to ship. We have less baggage. Power users prefer us.

Here’s the problem: Google is now free and integrated everywhere. To win this fight, we need users to actively choose us despite Google being right there in their browser. That’s a huge barrier. Even if we’re 20% better, most people will just use Google because it’s already there.

And long-term, Google will close the quality gap. They have more resources to improve the model, better data from search logs, more engineers. We might be better today. We won’t be better in 12 months.

The unit economics are also scary. If we’re competing on general AI search, we need massive scale to make freemium work. At 10M users with 5% conversion, that’s 500K paying users. At $20/month, that’s $10M monthly revenue. But inference costs for the 9.5M free users might eat half of that. We’d need 50M+ users to make this profitable.

**Option 2: Become the AI research assistant for professionals.**

We stop trying to be general AI search. We focus on one specific use case: researchers, analysts, and knowledge workers who need deep research done right.

Why this could work: Google is optimizing for everyone. We could optimize for people who do research for work. That’s a narrower market but higher willingness to pay. We could charge $50/month and people would pay because it saves them hours.

We’d build features Google won’t: export to research briefs, automatic citation formatting, collaboration on research projects, integration with tools researchers use like Zotero and Notion.

This plays to what we’re already good at - sources and citations. Researchers care about that. Casual users don’t.

Here’s the challenge: we’re abandoning 80% of our current users who just want quick answers. We’re betting we can grow faster in a smaller market. And we’re competing with specialized tools like Elicit and Consensus that are already focused on researchers. Why would researchers choose us over them?

**Option 3: Become the AI search API for other products.**

We stop being a consumer product. We become the infrastructure. Companies building AI features use Perplexity’s API instead of building search themselves.

Why this works: Every company is adding AI features. They need search capabilities. We have a working product. We could be the search layer for AI products the way Stripe is the payment layer.

The economics are better too - B2B pricing means we make more per query than consumer. And we don’t need massive consumer marketing.

But the risks are huge: We’re competing with OpenAI and Anthropic who already have API businesses. Why would someone use our search API instead of just using Claude or ChatGPT? We’d need to be way cheaper or way better.

And we’re giving up on the consumer brand we’ve built. Those 10M users don’t matter if we’re B2B.

**Option 4: Partner with platforms that compete with Google.**

We become the AI search for anti-Google platforms. Safari’s default AI search. Firefox’s AI search. DuckDuckGo’s AI layer. Maybe even Microsoft Edge.

Why this could work: These platforms need AI search to compete with Google. They don’t want to build it themselves. We’re ready to go. And they’d pay us for distribution to their users.

Safari alone has 1B users. Even 1% adoption would be 10M new users. That’s doubling our user base overnight.

The problems: We lose control of our product. We’re at the mercy of platform deals. If Apple decides to build their own AI search or just use OpenAI, we’re done. And these deals take 12+ months to close. Meanwhile Google is winning.”

Interviewer: “Those are interesting options. How do you think about which one gives Perplexity the best chance of surviving as an independent company?”

“That’s the key question. Let me think about what we need to survive: defensibility, path to profitability, and ability to compete long-term despite Google’s resources.”

#### Make Choice

“I’d go with Option 2 - become the AI research assistant for professionals.

Here’s why:

First, it’s the only option where we can actually build a moat. General AI search (Option 1) has no moat - Google can copy everything. API business (Option 3) has no moat - OpenAI will beat us on price and capability. Platform partnerships (Option 4) have no moat - we’re dependent on deals.

But professional research tools have a moat. Once someone builds their workflow around us - they’ve integrated us into Notion, they’ve set up their citation style, they’ve trained their team - they don’t switch easily. That’s sticky.

Second, the economics work. Professional researchers will pay $50/month. Maybe $100/month for teams. That’s 5x higher than consumer pricing. We don’t need 50M users to be profitable. We need 500K professionals. That’s achievable.

Third, it plays to our actual strength. We’re really good at citations and sources. That matters enormously to researchers. It doesn’t matter much to casual users looking up recipes.

Fourth, it’s a market Google won’t prioritize. Google optimizes for billions of users. Professional research tools is maybe 50M people globally. That’s a rounding error for Google. But it’s a huge market for us.

Now let me be clear about what I’m giving up:

We’re abandoning most of our current users. Of our 10M users, maybe 2M are the professional researchers we’re targeting. The other 8M will probably leave. That’s painful. Our user numbers will go down before they go up.

We’re betting that we can out-execute specialized research tools like Elicit and Consensus. They have a head start in this market. We’ll need to build features fast to catch up.

We’re accepting that we won’t be a mass consumer brand. We’ll never have 100M users. That means harder to raise venture capital at huge valuations. But it means we can actually build a sustainable business.

Here’s why I’m not doing the other options:

Not Option 1 (fight Google): We lose. Even if we’re better, Google’s distribution wins. And they’ll close the quality gap.

Not Option 3 (API): OpenAI and Anthropic beat us. We don’t have a unique technical advantage.

Not Option 4 (partnerships): Too risky. We lose control. Deals take too long. Google is winning users now.

The strategic bet: Professional researchers need tools built specifically for them. Google is building for everyone. We can own this niche, charge premium pricing, and be profitable at 500K users.”

Interviewer: “What if professional researchers decide they don’t need a specialized tool and just use free Google?”

“That’s the risk. If I’m wrong about researchers valuing specialized features enough to pay, this fails. But here’s why I think they will: researchers get paid for the quality of their research. Google optimized for speed. We’d optimize for comprehensiveness and accuracy. A consultant billing $200/hour saves 5 hours a week with our tool - that’s $1,000/week in value. $50/month is nothing.

The test is simple: can we get 100 paying professional users in 3 months? If yes, we know there’s a market. If no, we pivot.”

## Category 6: AI Safety & Ethics

Safety questions test whether you can build responsibly without breaking the product.

The key is balancing. Perfect safety often breaks the product. No safety breaks trust.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **One silver bullet.** They propose one solution. Safety requires layers.
- **Ignoring false positives.** They design for catching bad actors but block good users too.
- **No trade-offs.** Every safety decision has costs. Show you understand them.
- **Too abstract.** “We’ll use content moderation” means nothing. How? What triggers it?
- **No urgency thinking.** They treat all safety issues equally. Some need immediate fixes.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Identify Harm → Root Cause → Layered Defense → Balance Friction**

This works because you need to understand the specific harm before designing defenses. And you need multiple layers because no single solution catches everything.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CleR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb4279e40-854f-4930-bb6b-36437d3ca396_996x348.png)

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** Your AI writing assistant is being used to generate fake product reviews. You have 2 weeks to ship a solution. What do you do?

#### Clarify

“Let me understand the situation. How did we discover this? User reports? Media coverage? Internal monitoring?”

Assume: E-commerce platforms started flagging reviews. Some media coverage.

“What’s the scale? Handful of bad actors or widespread?”

Assume: Unknown scale but growing. Several thousand flagged reviews traced back to our tool.

“Do we have a policy against this?”

Assume: Yes, terms prohibit it. But no enforcement.

“What’s driving the 2-week timeline? Legal pressure? Platform partnerships at risk?”

Assume: Amazon threatened to block links from our tool.

Check in: “So we have 2 weeks because Amazon is threatening action. This is both legal and business risk. Let me think about how to solve this.”

#### Framework Setup

“I want to structure this around understanding what harm is happening and why, then designing multiple defense layers, and finally making sure we’re not blocking legitimate users.

Safety problems need multiple solutions, not one fix.”

Draw layers on your board.

#### Identify Harm

“Let me be specific about the harm:

**Legal harm:** Fake reviews violate FTC regulations. We could face regulatory action.

**Business harm:** Amazon might block our tool entirely. That cuts off legitimate users who actually use us for authentic reviews.

**Reputation harm:** Media coverage damages our brand. Harder to sign enterprise customers.

**Scale:** Several thousand flagged reviews is probably 1-5% of review content written with our tool. But enough to cause platform action. And growing.

**Severity:** High. Not life-threatening, but high enough to threaten our business in 2 weeks.”

Check in: “Does that match the severity you’re seeing?”

#### Root Cause

“Why is this happening?

**Capability issue:** Our tool generates persuasive reviews. We trained it to write compelling content. That’s exactly what fake reviewers need.

**Design issue:** Too easy. User types ‘Write a 5-star review for this blender,’ we write it. No friction.

**Education issue:** Maybe users don’t realize this violates terms. Maybe they think ‘AI generated’ means it’s okay.

**Deliberate misuse:** Some users know exactly what they’re doing. Running fake review farms. They found our tool works great and scaled it.

Root cause is all four. So we need solutions for all four.”

#### Layered Defense

“Multiple layers of defense:

**Layer 1: Detection**

Build classifier that detects product review attempts. Look for patterns: product name + rating request, multiple reviews for same product, no personal experience mentioned.

When detected, show warning: ‘This looks like a product review. Our terms prohibit reviews of products you haven’t used.’

Require confirmation: ‘I’ve personally used this product and I’m writing an authentic review.’

Takes est 3 engineers, 5 days.

Problem: False positives. Legitimate review gets warned. But that’s acceptable friction for crisis mode.

**Layer 2: Rate limiting**

If someone generates more than 10 product reviews in a day, flag it. First time: warning. Second time: require verification. Third time: suspend account.

Takes est 1 engineer, 3 days.

Problem: Sophisticated actors create multiple accounts. But raises cost of misuse.

**Layer 3: Watermarking**

Add invisible watermarks platforms can detect. Work with Amazon and Yelp to share the spec. They automatically flag our content for extra scrutiny.

Takes est 2 engineers, 7 days.

Problem: Users might feel we’re ‘snitching.’ But legitimate content survives scrutiny.

**Layer 4: Human review**

Hire content moderators to review flagged cases. Start with 5 moderators handling est 100 cases/day.

Problem: Doesn’t scale perfectly but catches edge cases.”

Check in: “I’ve outlined 4 layers. Should I keep going or dive deeper?”

Interviewer: “How do you decide what’s a false positive vs actual misuse?”

“That’s the key question. Let me think about the trade-off.”

#### Balance Friction

“The core trade-off:

**False positives:** Legitimate users get blocked. Example: Someone bought a blender, wants help writing a review because English isn’t their first language. We flag them. Impact: User frustration. Might stop using our tool.

**False negatives:** Fake reviews get through. Example: Review farm uses careful wording to avoid detection. Generates hundreds of fakes. Impact: Platforms block us. Business dies.

**Where to optimize right now (crisis mode):**

Optimize for low false negatives. Better to over-block than let fakes through. Amazon is threatening us.

Accept higher false positives. A legitimate user getting warned is annoying. Amazon blocking us is existential.

If we’re 60% confident something is fake, flag it. That means 40% of flags might be wrong. But catches more bad actors.

Show reasoning: ‘This looks like a product review because it mentions a product name and star rating. If you’ve personally used this product, confirm below.’

Transparency reduces frustration.

**After the crisis (Month 2+):**

Optimize for user experience. Reduce false positives.

Refine detection using Week 1-2 data. Learn what patterns actually indicate fakes.

Add appeal process: ‘Was this flagged incorrectly?’ Use feedback to improve.

Balance shifts from safety-first to usability-first once immediate threat passes.”

#### Success Metrics

“How we know it’s working:

**Week 1-2:** Detection rate above 70%, false positive rate below 10%, Amazon reduces escalations.

**Month 1-3:** Flagged reviews down 80%, platform relationships restored, user satisfaction doesn’t drop more than 5 points.

**Counter metrics:** Legitimate review usage shouldn’t drop more than 15%, track NPS and support tickets about false flags.

The test: In 3 months, are platforms comfortable with our content? If yes, solved. If no, need stronger measures or exit the review use case.”

Check in: “Does this approach make sense?”

## Category 7: AI Metrics & Pricing

Metrics and pricing questions test whether you understand what success looks like AND whether the business model works.

The key is connecting user value to business sustainability. You can’t pick metrics without understanding costs. You can’t price without knowing what you’re measuring.

Here’s what kills candidates:

- **Treating metrics and pricing as separate.** They pick metrics, then separately do pricing. These should inform each other.
- **Ignoring unit economics.** They propose pricing without checking if it’s profitable at scale.
- **Picking metrics they can’t monetize.** The north star looks great but doesn’t tie to willingness to pay.
- **No sensitivity analysis.** They don’t test what happens if costs spike or usage differs from assumptions.
- **Forgetting AI-specific considerations.** Inference costs, model improvements, accuracy trade-offs.

### The Framework

The framework that works: **Clarify → User Value → Business Model → Connect Them**

This works because metrics should measure user value, and pricing should capture that value. You need to think about both together.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VIz5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3b933c33-5bf4-48d7-b54f-0c96ead48aba_990x300.png)

Check in throughout. This is a conversation.

### Example Walkthrough

**Question:** You’re launching an AI sales email writer. What metrics would you track and how would you price it?

#### Clarify

“Let me take 30 seconds to think about what I need to understand.”

<30 seconds>

“I have a few questions:

Who’s the user - individual sales reps or companies buying for their sales teams?

What’s the core value prop - does it write better emails, write them faster, or both?

Where are we in the lifecycle - already launched with users, or still designing?

Any sense of comparable products or willingness to pay?”

Assume: Companies buying for sales teams. Value prop is both quality and speed - writes personalized emails in seconds vs hours. Launching in 3 months. Comparable sales tools cost $50-100/rep/month.

Check in: “Got it. So we’re helping sales teams write better emails faster, need to figure out what to measure and how to price it. Let me structure how I’ll think through this.”

#### Framework Setup

“I want to approach this by first understanding what user value looks like - because that drives what we measure. Then I’ll think about our costs and pricing options. The key is making sure the metrics and pricing actually connect - we’re measuring things that drive willingness to pay.

Make sense?”

Draw two boxes on your board: “User Value” and “Business Model” with an arrow connecting them.

#### User Value & Metrics

“Let me think about what value means for sales reps using this product.”

<1 minute>

“The value is really about helping reps close more deals. So let me work backwards from that:

**Ultimate outcome:** More deals closed

**How AI helps:** Better emails get more responses, which lead to more meetings, which lead to more deals

**What we can measure:**

If I think about input metrics - things the AI directly affects:

- Emails sent per rep per day
- Time to write each email
- Email quality score (maybe based on personalization, clarity)

For output metrics - business outcomes:

- Email response rate
- Meetings booked from emails
- Deal velocity for reps using the tool

Now here’s the interesting question: which should be the north star?

I’m torn between ‘meetings booked’ and ‘email response rate.’ Let me think through both:

**Option 1: Email response rate**

Pro: Direct measure of email quality. If our AI writes better emails, responses go up. Easy to attribute.

Con: Reps might already have good response rates. Hard to show improvement. And response rate doesn’t directly tie to revenue.

**Option 2: Meetings booked per rep per week**

Pro: Closer to revenue. Shows clear ROI. Companies care about meetings.

Con: Lots of factors affect meetings beyond email quality. Hard to isolate our impact.

Actually, let me think about this from a pricing angle. What would a sales leader pay for?

They’d pay for more meetings because meetings lead to deals. Response rate is interesting but not the thing they’re trying to optimize.

So I’d pick **meetings booked per rep per week** as the north star.

But I’d track response rate as a key input metric because it’s more sensitive to our AI’s quality. If response rate goes up, meetings should follow.

Does that reasoning make sense?”

Check in: “Should I keep going on metrics or do you want to push on this choice?”

Interviewer: “What about tracking actual deals closed?”

“That’s a great question. Deals closed would be the ultimate validation. But there’s a huge lag - could be 3-6 months from email to closed deal in B2B sales. So I’d track it, but not as the north star for measuring the product. We need faster feedback loops.

If we tracked meetings booked as the north star, we could add deals closed as a long-term output metric. Does that make sense?”

Interviewer: “Makes sense. What about guardrails?”

“Good question. Let me think about what could go wrong.”

<30 seconds>

“Guardrails I’d watch:

**Quality guardrails:**

- Email sends that get marked as spam
- Unsubscribe rates
- Sales reps editing AI suggestions heavily (means AI isn’t good enough)

**Business guardrails:**

- Rep satisfaction with the tool
- Time spent on the tool vs time saved
- Cost per email generated

That last one - cost per email - is going to be critical for pricing. Let me move into business model?”

#### Business Model & Pricing

“Now let me think about costs and what we can charge.”

<1 minute>

“On the cost side, we have:

**Variable costs:**

- AI inference per email generated - probably $0.05-$0.10 per email depending on length
- If a rep writes 20 emails per day, that’s 400 per month
- So maybe $20-40 per rep per month in inference costs

**Fixed costs per customer:**

- Customer success and support - maybe $3K per year per company
- Sales cost to acquire - maybe $5K, amortized over 2-3 years

So rough math: for a 10-person sales team, we’re spending maybe $400/month in inference plus $250/month in fixed costs = $650/month in costs.

Now what can we charge?

Comparable sales tools are $50-100 per rep per month. But what’s our ROI story?

If our tool helps each rep book 2 extra meetings per month, and their close rate on meetings is 20%, and average deal size is $50K - that’s $20K in additional pipeline per rep per month.

Even at 1 extra meeting per month, that’s $10K in pipeline.

So pricing at $75/rep/month means we’re capturing less than 1% of the value we create. That’s defensible.

Let me think about pricing models:

**Option 1: Per rep per month**

$75/rep/month feels right based on market comps and value.

Pro: Predictable revenue. Standard for sales tools. Scales with team size.

Con: Small teams might find it expensive. What if they only have 3 reps?

**Option 2: Per email generated**

$0.50 per email.

Pro: Pure usage-based. Pay for what you use.

Con: Our costs are $0.05-$0.10 per email, so we have good margins. But does this pricing make sense for customers? If a rep sends 400 emails per month, that’s $200/month. Way higher than per-seat pricing. Probably not competitive.

**Option 3: Tiered based on meetings booked**

Starter tier: Up to 20 meetings/month, $50/rep Pro tier: Up to 50 meetings/month, $75/rep Enterprise: Unlimited, $100/rep

Pro: Aligns pricing with value delivered. Heavy users pay more.

Con: Complex to explain. Hard to predict revenue. Might discourage usage.

I’m leaning toward **Option 1: $75 per rep per month** with maybe volume discounts for larger teams.

Why? It’s simple, predictable, and market-standard. The ROI story is so strong that pricing feels like a rounding error compared to pipeline value.

But wait - let me check if this actually works.”

Check in: “Does this pricing reasoning make sense before I test the economics?”

Interviewer: “What if inference costs are higher than you think?”

“Good challenge. Let me think about sensitivity.”

<30 seconds>

“If inference costs are double - so $0.20 per email instead of $0.10 - that changes things:

400 emails per month × $0.20 = $80 per rep in inference costs

At $75/rep pricing, we’d actually lose money on variable costs alone.

So a few options:

One, optimize the AI - use a smaller model, cache common responses, reduce token usage.

Two, adjust pricing - maybe $100/rep instead of $75.

Three, usage limits - maybe 300 emails per month at $75, then $50 for additional 100.

This is why connecting metrics to pricing matters. If our north star is meetings booked, but our costs scale with emails generated, we need to watch that ratio carefully.”

#### Connect Metrics to Pricing

“Let me take a minute to connect these pieces.”

<1 minute>

“Here’s how metrics and pricing connect:

**The value story:**

- North star: Meetings booked per rep per week
- Each additional meeting = $10K in pipeline value
- At $75/month per rep, we need to generate 1 extra meeting per rep every 2 months to be ROI-positive

**The cost story:**

- We spend $20-40/month per rep in inference costs
- At $75/month pricing, we have $35-55/month in contribution margin per rep
- Fixed costs are $250/month for a 10-person team

**The connection:**

- As meetings booked goes up (our north star), customer gets more value
- As long as we keep inference costs under $40/rep/month, we stay profitable
- So we need to track: meetings per rep (value) and cost per email (profitability)

The metrics framework and pricing model work together if:

One, meetings booked actually goes up when customers use our tool. If it doesn’t, they won’t renew regardless of price.

Two, inference costs stay manageable. If they spike, pricing breaks.

Three, reps actually send enough emails to get ROI. If they only send 5 emails per month, they won’t see value at $75/month.

Does this connection make sense?”

Check in: “Should I stress test these assumptions?”

Interviewer: “Yes, what could break this?”

#### Stress Test

“Let me think about what could go wrong.”

<45 seconds>

“ **Assumption 1: Meetings actually increase**

If our tool doesn’t increase meetings booked, the whole value prop collapses. Even at $25/month, customers won’t pay if it’s not working.

How we’d test: Track meetings booked for users vs non-users in first 90 days. Need to see at least 20% lift to justify the pricing.

**Assumption 2: Inference costs stay under $40/rep**

If OpenAI raises prices or usage is higher than expected, margins disappear.

How we’d handle: Set up alerts if cost per email hits $0.15. Have a plan to optimize the model or adjust pricing.

**Assumption 3: Reps use it consistently**

If adoption is low - like reps only use it for 5 emails per month instead of 400 - they won’t see ROI.

How we’d address: Track active usage in first 30 days. If below 100 emails/month per rep, flag for CS team to drive adoption.

**Assumption 4: We can sell to 10+ rep teams**

Our economics work better for larger teams because fixed costs are spread. A 3-rep team would be profitable but barely.

How we’d handle: Maybe minimum team size of 5 reps, or higher per-seat pricing for small teams.

The biggest risk is #1 - if meetings don’t increase, nothing else matters. So our north star metric directly predicts whether the business model works.”

Check in: “Does this stress test make sense?”

Interviewer: “One more thing - how would you adjust metrics or pricing after launch based on what you learn?”

“Great question. Let me think about what we’d learn.”

<30 seconds>

“After 3-6 months, we’d have real data on:

**What we’d learn about metrics:**

- Is meetings booked the right north star, or should it be something else like response rate?
- What input metrics actually predict meetings? Maybe email personalization score matters more than volume.
- Are there leading indicators we’re missing?

**What we’d learn about pricing:**

- Are customers seeing the ROI we predicted?
- What usage patterns actually emerge - are reps sending 400 emails or 100?
- Which pricing tier is most popular?

**How we’d adapt:**

If meetings don’t increase but response rates do, maybe response rate becomes the north star and we adjust our value story.

If usage is way lower than expected - say 100 emails per month - we might lower pricing to $50/rep since our costs are lower.

If larger teams get more value (network effects, templates shared across team), we might add team-based pricing.

The key is the metrics tell us if we’re delivering value, and the economics tell us if we can capture it. They should inform each other.”

Check in: “Does that cover metrics and pricing, or is there something else you want to dive into?”
