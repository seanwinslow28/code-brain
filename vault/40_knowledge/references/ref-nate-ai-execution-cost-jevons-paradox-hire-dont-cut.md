Cutting your team because AI made them more productive is like firing your sales force because they started closing too many deals. It’s the wrong move applied to the right observation, and it will be the defining strategic error of 2026.

The observation is correct: AI collapses execution cost. Part 2 of this series showed that 60 to 70 percent of labor hours in a typical tech company are coordination overhead, and that overhead evaporates when agents go directly from insight to code. The companies running that math aren’t wrong about what’s disappearing. They’re wrong about what to do next. Because when execution cost drops by an order of magnitude, the correct response isn’t to do the same work with fewer people. It’s to do dramatically more work, to pursue every opportunity that was previously too expensive, too niche, too speculative to attempt. The companies that get this will be unrecognizable in three years. The companies that don’t will have beautiful margins and no growth, and their competitors will eat them alive.

A few days ago, Whoop announced it’s hiring more than 600 people, nearly doubling its 800-person workforce. Will Ahmed, the CEO: “Right now, companies are debating whether to hire more people or just invest in AI. We are doing both.”

Ahmed is making the most important strategic bet of 2026. And the boards and leadership teams I work with — the ones actually making these decisions, not the ones performing for analysts — increasingly agree with him. The doom narrative lives in the media. Inside the rooms where it matters, the smartest operators are asking a completely different question. Not “how many people can we cut?” but “what can we build now that we couldn’t before?”

This part answers that question. Six unlocks. None of them are about efficiency. And I want you to hold something in your head as you read them: these six aren’t the point. They’re what I can see from where I’m standing. They’re illustrative, not exhaustive. The real argument is that we’ve spent eighteen months telling ourselves a doom story so relentlessly that we’ve lost the ability to see what’s right in front of us. Every conversation about AI and work starts from the same place, how many jobs disappear, and that starting point blinds us to an opportunity set that is staggeringly large and almost completely unexamined. The companies that break out of the doom frame first don’t just get a head start. They get the whole race, because everyone else is still arguing about headcount while the market moves.

**Here’s what’s inside:**

- **Iteration physics change strategy itself.** When learning cycles compress from quarters to days, exploration stops being a luxury and becomes the default.
- **Domain experts become builders.** The 36 million developers in the world are about to be joined by hundreds of millions of people who know what software should exist.
- **Quality stops being the premium.** Testing, security, accessibility, documentation: all verifiable, all labor-intensive, all about to become standard.
- **The market for ambition expands.** Opportunities that were “too small to staff” become profitable when execution cost drops by ten times.
- **Organizations move at the speed of insight.** The lag between seeing a problem and testing a fix collapses from months to hours.
- **The real reframe.** Why the imagination failure, not the technology, is the expensive mistake.
- **The prompts that build the case.** Four working tools that surface your org’s self-censored opportunities, turn domain expertise into buildable specs, construct the board-ready expansion argument, and compress the lag between seeing a problem and testing a fix.

Quick context if you’re joining the series here: [Part 1](https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/cursors-coding-agents-solved-a-math) showed that multi-agent harnesses generalize across domains. Part 2 showed that 60 to 70 percent of knowledge-worker hours are coordination overhead that evaporates when agents go from insight to code. Both parts described what gets deleted. This article is about what gets ***created***.

## LINK: Grab the prompts

The biggest risk with this article isn’t that you disagree with it. It’s that you agree, walk into your next strategy meeting, and watch someone pull up a headcount reduction spreadsheet that nobody built a counterargument to. Cuts have a spreadsheet. Expansion, so far, doesn’t.

These four prompts build it. The Ambition Audit forces your team to name every opportunity you’ve shelved or self-censored in the last twelve months, then re-runs the economics at collapsed execution cost. Most teams I’ve worked with are genuinely startled by how long that list is. The Domain Expert → Builder Roadmap is for the people in your org who aren’t engineers but know exactly what software should exist for their workflows. It draws out their knowledge and produces a specification concrete enough to hand to an AI coding tool the same afternoon. The Expansion Economics Brief constructs the board-level argument: what the freed coordination overhead funds, which shelved bets flip to profitable at 10x lower cost, and what the competitive landscape looks like in eighteen months if you cut while your rivals expand. And the Insight-to-Action Compression Map takes your specific workflows and redesigns them so the person who sees the problem can test a solution by end of day, not end of quarter.

These aren’t inspirational exercises. If you can’t fill in the inputs honestly, that’s information too.

## The wrong question

The question getting all the airtime — how many fewer people do we need? — feels sophisticated. You can model it in a spreadsheet. It has the satisfying crispness of a problem with a numerical answer. It is the wrong question.

The right question: Given that execution cost just dropped by an order of magnitude, what can we do now that was previously impossible?

The cost-reduction frame assumes a fixed pie of value and optimizes for how efficiently you capture your current slice. The ambition frame assumes the pie was artificially constrained by the cost of execution, and that removing the constraint creates a larger opportunity than the savings.

The history of technology tells us which frame wins. When steel got cheap, the industry expanded into skyscrapers, railroads, automobiles. When computing got cheap, it created personal computing, the internet, mobile, cloud. When distribution got cheap, media companies that played defense got destroyed by companies that built new categories. The pattern has a name: Jevons Paradox. When efficiency increases, total consumption goes up, not down, because cheaper resources make new applications viable. AI is the most dramatic efficiency improvement in the history of knowledge work. If Jevons Paradox applies, and every structural indicator says it does, the total demand for insight, judgment, creativity, and domain expertise is about to explode.

The cutters are pocketing the savings. Ahmed is betting on the paradox. I know which bet I’d take.

## Iteration changes the physics of strategy

When you compress the iteration cycle from months to days, you don’t get a faster version of the old strategy process. You get a fundamentally different relationship between insight and action.

Today, a product bet takes three to six months from hypothesis to shipped feature to measurable signal. You get maybe four to eight real learning cycles per year. Every strategic decision carries enormous weight because the cost of being wrong is a quarter of execution time. This is why the dominant strategy in corporate America is “copy what’s working for someone else.” The iteration cost is so high that exploration is irrational.

Now compress the cycle to days. Idea to spec to code to deploy to measure, in a single working session. This isn’t theoretical. Cursor’s February 2026 cloud agents update lets developers spin up ten to twenty parallel agents on isolated cloud VMs simultaneously, each one working on a separate branch, testing its own changes, opening pull requests when done. Roughly 35% of Cursor’s own merged pull requests are now created by agents operating autonomously.

What happens when a company runs two hundred learning cycles a year instead of eight? You can afford to be wrong constantly. Ship Monday, see it doesn’t work Wednesday, try a different approach Friday. The cost of a failed experiment drops from “a quarter of roadmap capacity” to “a day of agent compute.” Risk goes from something you manage to something you seek.

And here is the part that doesn’t get discussed enough. The long-shot ideas that die in prioritization meetings don’t just get deprioritized. They never get proposed. The person with the insight self-censors because they understand organizational economics. They know the pitch meeting, the resourcing conversation, the roadmap negotiation isn’t worth it for a bet that might not work. So the idea stays in their head. Multiply that across every smart person in your organization and you begin to see the scale of what’s been lost. Not bad ideas that were correctly filtered — good ideas that were never surfaced because the cost of testing them exceeded the organizational willingness to try.

In the new model, the self-censorship evaporates. The cost of testing an idea drops below the threshold of organizational friction. Startups die because they exhaust their funding before they exhaust their hypotheses. Drop the cost of testing each hypothesis by ten to fifty times and the runway equation changes fundamentally.

The human role in this world isn’t smaller. It’s bigger, and it’s different. You need people who can generate good hypotheses, people with deep customer intuition, contrarian market insight, creative vision that goes beyond incrementalism. Today, those people spend 80% of their energy shepherding a single bet through an organization. Tomorrow they’re generating and evaluating ten bets a week. The bottleneck shifts from “can we build it” to “should we build it,” and “should we build it” is a human question.

This is what Ahmed understands. He’s not hiring 600 people for the same work. He’s hiring them because Whoop’s opportunity set just expanded: hardware innovation, health monitoring research, global markets, personalized health interventions. Every one of those 600 new hires is pointed at an opportunity that didn’t exist at human coordination speed.

## Domain experts become builders

This is the unlock that will reshape the economy more profoundly than anything else I’ll describe here — and the one the discourse is most radically underestimating.

There is a doctor who knows exactly what software her patient panel needs. She can describe every screen, every workflow, every decision tree with clinical precision. But the hospital IT department has a fourteen-month backlog and her request is prioritized behind the new billing integration and the EMR upgrade and three compliance projects. So she uses spreadsheets and sticky notes and her own memory, and patients get care that’s worse than it should be because the person with the deepest understanding of the problem can’t build the solution.

There is a logistics manager who could draw the warehouse routing algorithm on a whiteboard in ten minutes. He’s been doing this job for twenty years and he knows every bottleneck, every edge case, every failure mode. But turning that whiteboard sketch into software requires a dev team, a product manager, a specification process, a prioritization conversation with the CTO, and six months of waiting. So he does it manually, and the warehouse runs at 60% of the efficiency it could achieve, and nobody even knows the gap exists because nobody with the domain expertise has ever been able to show what “better” looks like as working software.

There is a teacher who knows exactly what adaptive learning her students need. She could describe every branching path, every misconception that needs addressing, every moment where a student needs encouragement versus challenge. Ed-tech companies build generic platforms that approximate her vision at maybe 30% fidelity. So she improvises, every day, in a classroom of thirty kids with different needs, armed with tools that weren’t built by someone who understands what she understands.

These domain experts have been locked out of building by the translation layer. The gap between “knowing what should exist” and “making it exist as software” has required an intermediary class of professionals (product managers, engineers, designers) to translate human expertise into code. That translation is lossy, slow, and expensive. And it means the vast majority of domain knowledge in the world never makes it into software at all.

The numbers tell the story. There are roughly 36 to 47 million software developers worldwide, depending on how you count. There are hundreds of millions of domain experts who know what software should exist but can’t build it: healthcare workers, educators, scientists, supply chain operators, financial advisors, legal professionals, manufacturing engineers.

When a doctor can describe what she needs and an agent builds it in an afternoon, you unlock an entirely new class of builder. The long tail of software explodes. Platforms like Lovable, Bolt, and Replit are already putting production-quality development in the hands of non-coders. Shopify’s internal data tells the story: when they deployed AI coding tools across the whole organization, the fastest-growing user groups weren’t engineers. They were support and revenue teams, domain experts who suddenly had the ability to build tools for their own workflows. Shopify’s initial 1,500 Cursor licenses were snapped up within weeks, so they ordered another 1,500. The fastest-growing adoption wasn’t from engineers. It was finance, sales, and support.

The domain expertise is the irreducible input. The agent can code, but it can’t know what the ER nurse needs at 3 AM during a shift change. It can’t know what the warehouse floor looks like when two conveyor lines jam simultaneously. It can’t know what a struggling seventh-grader’s face looks like when they finally understand fractions. Those are human perceptions born of experience that no training dataset captures. And for the first time in the history of computing, those insights can travel directly from the person who has them to working software, without translation, without intermediaries, without waiting fourteen months in an IT backlog.

The scale of this is not incremental — it’s civilizational. We are about to go from 36 million people who can build software to hundreds of millions. The total surface area of human problems addressed by custom tools is going to increase by an order of magnitude.

## Quality becomes the default, not the premium

Most software is mediocre, not because builders lack ability, but because quality is expensive and something has to give. Testing, documentation, security review, performance optimization, accessibility: none are hard problems. They’re all verifiable. They’re just labor-intensive.

When agent harnesses run testing, security review, accessibility audits, and documentation as default steps in the build loop (not expensive add-ons but standard procedure) the baseline quality of all software goes up dramatically. Today, a startup shipping a new feature makes implicit decisions about what to skip: we won’t write tests for that edge case, we won’t update the docs, we won’t check screen readers, we won’t test in Japanese. Every shortcut is rational given the cost structure. But the shortcuts accumulate, and two years later you have a product held together by duct tape and tribal knowledge. The engineers aren’t sloppy. The economics forced them to choose between quality and speed, and the market punishes you for choosing quality.

The gap between what the top 5% of engineering teams ship and what everyone else ships is almost entirely explained by how much labor a company can afford for QA and polish. Agents collapse that gap.

The human role shifts from “catch bugs” to “define excellence.” The quality team sets standards higher than anyone currently ships — every page loads in under 200 milliseconds, every flow is accessible at WCAG AAA, every API endpoint has current documentation. Those standards become achievable instead of aspirational. The people who define what “good” looks like become the most valuable members of the team, because the definition is now the binding constraint, not the labor to implement it.

## Every company becomes a platform

Building and maintaining integrations is so expensive today that only the biggest companies can afford to be real platforms. A 50-person startup can barely maintain integrations with three other tools. An enterprise software company with two thousand engineers still can’t keep up with the integration requests from its largest customers. The API economy was supposed to make everything interoperable; in practice, it created a world where integration is technically possible and economically prohibitive for anyone below a certain scale.

When the cost of building and maintaining code drops by an order of magnitude, every company can be deeply integrated with every other. Custom integrations get done overnight. The customer who says “I need your tool to talk to this other tool that nobody’s heard of” gets a working integration by end of week instead of a ticket in a backlog that goes nowhere. “Walled garden” moats weaken dramatically. Enterprise incumbents that protect their position through integration lock-in find that when bridge-building costs approach zero, competition shifts to the quality of the product itself rather than the stickiness of the ecosystem.

## The market for ambition expands

Today, a company evaluating a new opportunity asks: “Is this big enough to justify hiring a team?” That question eliminates thousands of real opportunities that are too small, too uncertain, or too niche. A $10 million market isn’t worth entering when it costs $3 million a year in engineering payroll. An R&D project with a 20% chance of success isn’t worth pursuing when failure costs two quarters of roadmap capacity.

Drop execution cost by ten times and all those calculations flip. The $10 million market becomes a profitable niche one person with domain expertise can serve. The speculative R&D project becomes a cheap experiment; run five, one hits, the return dwarfs the cost of the four failures. The geographic expansion that “can’t justify the investment” becomes a localization project that takes a week instead of a quarter. Every company’s addressable market grows. Not because the market changed, but because the company’s ability to serve it changed. This is Jevons Paradox applied to knowledge work itself.

Capturing those expanded opportunities requires people. Different people doing different work. People with vision and domain expertise and creative insight who can see which opportunities to pursue. Those 600 new hires at Whoop are being selected from a pool of 750 applicants per position, a 0.13% acceptance rate. This is a company that understands the bottleneck is shifting to insight and is stockpiling the scarcest resource on earth — people who can see opportunities and execute against them.

## The org moves at the speed of insight

The lag between “someone has an insight” and “the organization acts on it” is the single greatest source of wasted value in every company I’ve consulted with. The insight was always the valuable thing. Everything between the insight and the action — latency imposed by organizational structure.

Today: a customer success manager notices a churn pattern. She writes it up. Goes to her manager. Manager raises it at the leadership meeting. VP of product asks for data. An analyst pulls the data. It gets discussed at the next product review. A PM writes a brief. Engineering estimates three weeks. Goes into sprint planning. Maybe something ships in two to three months. If the pattern was real and the fix was right, the company saved some customers. If the pattern was noise or the fix was wrong, the company wasted a quarter and learned nothing actionable until the next cycle.

In the compressed org, the CSM who notices the churn pattern pulls the data herself with an agent. She explores the hypothesis. She prototypes a solution. She tests it against historical data. She has a working demo deployed to a test cohort by end of day. Not a deck. Not a brief. Not a Jira ticket. A working prototype in production, gathering real signal.

I live this every day. I sit at a command line and iterate directly on design, specification, and code in the same session. The spec and the implementation converge into the same artifact. There is no separate “spec phase” and “build phase” because the person with the insight and the system writing the code are operating in a continuous loop. This is not some exotic future state. This is how I build things now, and increasingly it’s how every team I advise is starting to work.

When the translation layers disappear, everyone gets closer to the product. Not closer to a description of the product. Closer to the product itself. The PM isn’t writing a document that an engineer will interpret. They’re shaping the actual artifact. The marketing leader isn’t writing a brief for someone else to execute. They’re building the landing page, seeing what it looks like, tweaking the copy, testing the conversion, all in the same session. Everyone is fingertips-on-the-product in a way that layers of organizational abstraction have made impossible for decades.

What I’m describing is more than a productivity gain. It’s a more dignified and creative relationship with work itself.

## The real reframe

None of the six unlocks I just described are speculative. None require a technological breakthrough. None depend on AGI or some future model capability. They’re all available right now, with tools that exist today, to any organization willing to look up from the layoff spreadsheet long enough to see them.

And yet almost nobody is talking about them. The entire public conversation about AI and work has collapsed into a single question (who gets replaced?) and that question is so loud, so emotionally charged, so irresistible to media, that it has drowned out the far more consequential question: what becomes possible?

This is a failure of imagination, and it’s going to be expensive. The six unlocks I described aren’t the full picture. They’re the ones I can articulate from my vantage point, the ones I see in my consulting work, my own building, the conversations I have with operators who are actually making decisions. But the nature of a Jevons Paradox moment is that the new applications are inherently unpredictable. Nobody in 1995 looked at cheap internet bandwidth and said “ride-sharing, social media, streaming video, the gig economy.” The applications that mattered most were the ones nobody could envision from inside the old frame. That’s where we are now. The six unlocks I laid out are the obvious ones, the low-hanging fruit of a world where execution cost collapsed. The ones that will actually define the next decade are the ones we can’t see yet, because we’re too busy staring at the layoff numbers to look.

Whoop isn’t special because Will Ahmed figured out all the applications. Whoop is special because Ahmed broke out of the doom frame. He looked at the same facts everyone else is looking at and instead of reaching for the headcount reduction playbook, he asked what Whoop could become. That’s the unlock beneath all the other unlocks: the willingness to treat this moment as an expansion, not a contraction. Most leadership teams haven’t gotten there yet. Not because they’re stupid, but because the narrative environment is so overwhelmingly oriented toward fear that ambition feels irresponsible. It isn’t. It’s the only rational response to the structural economics.

I am not claiming that nobody will be displaced. Part 2 laid this out unflinchingly: 40 to 60 percent of current headcount performs work that is either already automatable or will be within eighteen months. That’s a real number with real human consequences.

What I am claiming is that the “fixed pie” framing is empirically wrong. The pie was artificially constrained by execution cost. We were never limited by ideas, insight, or ambition. We were limited by the cost of turning ideas into products. Remove the execution bottleneck and the constraint shifts to good ideas, deep domain knowledge, customer empathy, creative vision. Those are all human capacities, and they are in drastically short supply relative to what the world actually needs built.

Think about what the world needs and doesn’t have. Personalized education for every learning style. Clinical decision support calibrated to specific patient populations. Climate adaptation software for farming communities. Financial planning for two billion adults with a bank account but no advisor. None of these are unsolved technical problems. They’re unsolved economic problems. The cost of building custom software has been too high for these “too small” markets. AI is the bridge. Right now. Today.

And here’s the cost of the imagination failure that keeps me up at night — if you can’t see the opportunity, you can’t begin to plan the path from here to there. The hardest work ahead isn’t technical. It’s figuring out what upskilling actually looks like when the job isn’t “do the same thing faster” but “do something you’ve never been asked to do before.” That’s a different curriculum, different hiring profiles, different performance metrics, different leadership muscle entirely. None of that planning can start until you break out of the doom frame, because the doom frame doesn’t have a “build new capabilities” line item. It only has a “reduce headcount” line item. The companies that can’t imagine the expansion can’t prepare their people for it, and that’s how you end up with beautiful margins and a workforce that has no idea how to capture the opportunity that just opened up.

## The series, tied together

The harness generalizes (Part 1). The coordination tax is most of the org (Part 2). What emerges isn’t a smaller version of the old company. It’s a more ambitious version (Part 3). The winners will not be the companies that pocket the savings. They’ll be the companies that raise their ambitions to match, that use the freed capacity to pursue opportunities that were previously impossible, and end up hiring more people, not fewer, because the constraint shifted from “we can’t build it” to “we can’t see it.”

Whoop is hiring 600 people because Will Ahmed sees a market that expands by an order of magnitude when execution cost drops. The companies announcing AI-driven layoffs see the same work getting done by fewer people. One camp is reading the moment correctly.

The humans aren’t getting deleted. They’re getting unshackled from coordination overhead and freed to operate at the speed of insight. The companies building things in 2028 that we can’t imagine today won’t be the ones that ran the leanest. They’ll be the ones that aimed the highest — because for the first time in the history of technology, the constraint on what’s possible is ambition, not execution.

Act accordingly.
