Last month, a senior product manager at a company I work with resigned. She’d been there six years, consistently rated “exceeds expectations.” She was the person you wanted in the room when something was broken, the one whose instincts about customer problems were almost always right. She was also, by her own accounting, spending about 75% of her working hours on alignment meetings, cross-functional syncs, stakeholder management, and the slow process of translating her judgment into something a team of eight could execute across three time zones.

She left. She picked up Claude Code and Cursor. In her first month solo, she shipped a working product that addressed a market gap her former employer had been roadmapping for Q3. Not a prototype. A product, with paying customers.

I’ve been thinking about why that story keeps repeating. And the more I look at the data, the more I think almost everyone is asking the wrong question about AI-era talent.

The question everyone asks: “How do we find extraordinary people?”

The question they should be asking: “How did we spend the last thirty years building organizations that make extraordinary people look ordinary? And now that AI lets those people route around us entirely, what exactly is our plan?”

The solo founder explosion is not a story about a new kind of capability emerging. It is a story about an old kind of capability being uncapped. Ben Broca (@bencera\_ on X) crossed $1M ARR with Polsia in his first month, zero employees. Maor Shlomo built Base44 to 300,000 users and sold to Wix for $80 million in cash, six months from start to exit. Pieter Levels runs a $3M-plus ARR portfolio across multiple products as one person. Sarah Gwilliam, a grief coach who does not “speak AI” by her own description, launched Solace, an AI-powered platform for navigating loss, through an AI-native incubator. No founding team. No technical co-founder.

The narrative is set: AI lowers barriers, taste matters, solo founders are the future. Dario Amodei puts 70-80% odds on a one-person billion-dollar company by 2026. Carta reports the share of solo-founded startups rose from 23.7% in 2019 to 36.3% by mid-2025.

But the narrative is pointing at the founders. It should be pointing at the thousands of people inside your organization who have exactly the same caliber of judgment, and whose output looks nothing like theirs, because your organizational structure has been suppressing it for their entire careers.

**This briefing covers:**

- **The conviction problem.** Why “taste” is the wrong word for what makes these founders extraordinary, and what the real variable is that your performance reviews don’t measure.
- **Speed of control.** A framework for the actual bottleneck in AI-era talent, and why span of control is the wrong metaphor.
- **Three theses that should unsettle you.** The evidence that extraordinary talent is being capped by overhead, that AI is compressing the learning curve itself, and that your organization’s compromise tax is about to be exposed.
- **The variable everyone gets wrong.** Why correctness, not volume, is what’s scarce now, and why that distinction changes every talent decision you make.
- **The diagnostic.** Five questions you can run this week to find out how much talent your organization is actually suppressing.

I’m going to walk through why the prevailing framework for AI-era talent is wrong in a way that matters, what should replace it, and what you can do about it this quarter.

## Forget “taste.” The word is conviction.

The bumper sticker of the solo founder era is Ben Broca’s: “80% AI, 20% taste.” It’s catchy. It’s viral. It’s pointing at the wrong 20%.

Taste is aesthetic judgment. It’s knowing what’s good. Ben did not demonstrate taste when he designed Polsia’s interface after a minimalist clicker game called Universal Paperclips, or when he put a Daft Punk track on his about page that autoplays, or when he rejected the dark-mode sci-fi gradient aesthetic that every other AI product uses in favor of a stark black-and-white design that looks like nothing else in the space. Nobody asked for that. No design committee would have approved it. No A/B test would have suggested it.

That was conviction. And the distinction matters more than it sounds.

Taste evaluates. Conviction ships. The difference is the gap between the person who can recognize a good design and the person who builds one before anyone else agrees it’s good. Conviction is acting on your own vision when nobody has told you it’s right. And crucially, taste and conviction are not separate traits. They’re a feedback loop. Taste is the substrate that makes conviction reliable. Conviction without taste is just confidence, and a twenty-three-year-old with boundless confidence and no pattern recognition is going to be wrong most of the time. We all know that person. They move fast and break things, and the things stay broken, because they don’t yet have the accumulated judgment to know which direction is worth sprinting in.

What makes Ben’s conviction valuable is that fifteen years of cross-functional experience (engineering, product, management, operations under Travis Kalanick) gave him the taste to be right often enough that acting without consensus became a net positive. Same with Pieter Levels: forty-plus shipped projects, most failures, each one calibrating his sense of what works. By the time he’s building at full speed with AI, his conviction is backed by a decade of compounded pattern recognition. He’s not guessing fast. He’s recognizing fast.

I keep coming back to Ben’s co-founder story because it illustrates the mechanism so precisely. When he had a partner, they respected each other. Both talented. But their visions diverged, and every output was compromise, what he calls “a mish mush average thing.” Quality went up the moment he went solo with AI execution, and the reason had nothing to do with his co-founder’s ability. Every compromise slowed the cycle between “I believe this is right” and “here’s what actually happened.”

The mechanism is time. Consensus checkpoints consume clock cycles, and every “let me get buy-in” is a decision delayed. In a world where AI handles execution at near-zero marginal cost, the bottleneck on output is no longer the speed of building. It’s the speed of deciding. And the speed of deciding is gated by how much external validation a person requires before they’ll act.

McKinsey’s research on organizational decision-making confirms this at enterprise scale. Their survey of more than 1,200 managers found that faster decisions tend to be higher quality, not lower, directly contradicting the widespread assumption that speed and quality trade off. Companies they classified as decision-making “winners” (the top 20%) were nearly twice as likely to make decisions that delivered 20%-plus financial returns. The single strongest correlate wasn’t more analysis or better data. It was empowering people closest to the work to make decisions without escalation. Respondents at these companies were 3.2 times more likely to say employees were empowered to decide and receive coaching rather than await approval.

A separate McKinsey study found that executives spend almost 40% of their time on decisions, and believe most of that time is poorly used. Sixty-one percent of managers say at least half the time spent on decisions is ineffective.

That’s not a process failure. It is a structural consequence of organizations that require consensus before action, organizations that have built a tax on conviction into every layer of their operating model. The solo founders producing extraordinary results right now have the same caliber of judgment as your best people. The difference is that the conviction tax has been removed.

## It’s not span of control. It’s speed of control.

The phrase everyone reaches for when discussing AI-era leverage is “span of control,” how many things, people, or agents can one person oversee? The framing suggests a spatial metaphor: your reach got wider.

But that’s not what’s happening. What’s happening is temporal, and attentional.

Ben directs dozens of AI agents across multiple businesses. He isn’t “managing” them the way a VP manages a department through layers of context-setting and alignment docs. Every morning, his AI CEO agent emails him a status report, a compressed summary of what happened across all workstreams overnight. He reads it, makes a rapid series of binary judgment calls (yes/no, this direction/that direction, ship it/hold it), and the agents execute until the next decision point. The constraint on his output is not how many things he can hold simultaneously. It’s how fast he can cycle through high-quality decisions.

But that daily status report is doing something subtle that’s easy to miss. It’s solving the attention allocation problem. When you have dozens of workstreams running in parallel, the question isn’t just “how fast can I decide?” It’s “how do I know which decision needs me right now?” Ben doesn’t review every agent’s output with equal care every morning. The status report surfaces what matters: what’s stalled, what’s diverging, what needs a human call. He triages. He allocates his judgment to the two or three things that actually need it, and lets the rest keep running.

This is closer to an editorial function than a management function. A great editor doesn’t read every word of every draft with the same intensity. They develop a sense for where the problems are likely to be, and they allocate their attention disproportionately to those spots. Ben’s workflow is the same pattern: a wide surface area of activity, a filtering mechanism that surfaces what matters, and sequential high-quality decisions applied where they’ll have the most impact. The skill isn’t multitasking. It’s prioritization at speed, the ability to scan broadly and instantly recognize where your judgment adds the most value.

This reframe, from span to speed and attention, explains things the old model cannot.

It explains why conviction matters mechanically: every pause for validation slows the cycle. It explains why experienced operators sometimes underperform new builders with AI tools. Fastly’s July 2025 survey of 791 developers found a telling paradox: senior developers ship 2.5 times more AI-generated code than juniors, but 28% of those seniors spend so much time reviewing and correcting AI output that it offsets most of the time savings, nearly double the 17% rate among juniors. Their experience creates a quality instinct that is valuable in production but expensive in velocity.

The METR randomized controlled trial is even sharper. Experienced open-source developers working on their own familiar repositories were 19% slower when using AI tools. Afterward, they believed AI had sped them up by 20%. That 39-point gap between perception and reality, believing you’re faster when you’re measurably slower, is perhaps the most unsettling finding in the entire AI productivity literature. Their hard-won habits (careful review, thorough testing, defensive coding practices) became a drag on the judgment cycle when AI changed the cost structure of building. The senior developer’s instinct to review every line before shipping is prudent when humans write the code. When AI writes it at near-zero cost and you can ship, test, and iterate in hours, that same instinct is a speed penalty.

And it explains Pieter Levels. His $1M ARR in seventeen days wasn’t overnight success. It was ten years of compounded judgment, forty-plus shipped projects, most failures, applied at extremely high decision velocity. By 2025, his taste-conviction loop was so well-calibrated that he could scan output, identify what mattered, decide, and move on. He wasn’t reviewing everything. He knew where to look.

## Three theses that should keep you awake

**A massive population of extraordinary people were never blocked by ability. They were blocked by overhead.**

Think about the senior PM I mentioned at the top. She had instincts honed by a decade of customer contact. She was extraordinary at her job. Her actual throughput was gated by sprint planning, cross-functional alignment, design review cycles, and the slow process of translating her judgment into team execution. Give her the right tools and a long weekend. She ships something in seventy-two hours that would have taken her team a quarter. The coordination cost of translating her judgment into team execution was enormous, and that cost has now collapsed toward zero.

Sarah Gwilliam is a particularly clean example. She wasn’t blocked by ideas or domain expertise; she’d been doing grief work for years. She knew exactly what bereaved people needed at 2 AM when the world felt impossible. She was blocked by the fact that building a technology product required technical skills she didn’t have and couldn’t afford to hire. AI didn’t give her judgment. She already had it. It removed the blocker that stood between her judgment and a working product. The Economist cited her case as a leading example of AI enabling the first one-person unicorn.

The Carta data quantifies the trend: solo-founded startups rose from 23.7% to 36.3% of all new U.S. ventures between 2019 and mid-2025. The share of startups founded without VC backing climbed from 22% to 38% over roughly the same period. These are not all young hackers. Many are experienced operators who realized they no longer needed a team, or anyone’s permission, to build.

The implication is direct: you have people right now who could produce extraordinary outcomes if you removed the coordination overhead that’s consuming most of their working hours. AI doesn’t replace them. It uncaps them. What you need to decide is whether they’ll get uncapped inside your organization or outside it.

**AI is growing talent, not just unleashing it. And the implications are uncomfortable.**

The “unleashing” thesis is true but incomplete. Something else is happening that’s harder to see and harder to accept.

When you compress the time between “I think this will work” and “here’s what actually happened,” you compress the time it takes to acquire judgment. This is the taste-conviction feedback loop running at higher RPM. IBM research found that junior developers gain more productivity and learning velocity from AI tools than senior developers do. GitHub’s internal Copilot data shows developers using AI assistants complete tasks up to 56% faster, with juniors seeing the most dramatic gains. One engineering manager described a new hire who scaffolded a feature and solved a tricky validation bug on day one using Claude, a contribution that would have required weeks of onboarding in the previous era.

Ben describes this dynamic almost accidentally. He talks about building hundreds of AI companies simultaneously through a Polsia fund, not primarily for returns, but because it compresses the feedback loop on what works. If you can go from hypothesis to measurable result in hours instead of quarters, you develop instincts at a fundamentally different rate.

Here’s where it gets uncomfortable. The person with two years of AI-native building experience may have more relevant pattern recognition than someone with eight years of traditional execution. The reason isn’t raw intelligence. It’s that their judgment is calibrated to current conditions, while the experienced person’s judgment is calibrated to conditions that no longer exist. The senior operator’s ingrained reflex to “run a design review” or “build a six-month roadmap” isn’t just slower. In a world where those steps are no longer load-bearing, those reflexes are miscalibrated.

I should be careful not to overstate this. Deep domain expertise still matters enormously, and there’s a real risk of discounting hard-won institutional knowledge. But the METR data is striking: experienced developers on their own codebases, the most favorable possible setup, were measurably slower with AI than without it. And after the study, those same developers believed AI had sped them up by 20%.

Pieter Levels illustrates both sides. His judgment density is extraordinary, ten years of building in public, forty-plus shipped products. But he also recently spent time learning TypeScript through AI tools. He didn’t protect his PHP expertise as an identity. He treated it as a tool that was becoming less useful and adapted. His judgment stayed current because he kept iterating against reality instead of defending patterns learned in a previous era.

The implication: “years of experience” is becoming a less reliable proxy for capability. The question is not how long someone has been working. It’s how recently their judgment has been calibrated against real-world feedback, and how fast that calibration cycle is running.

**The compromise tax on talent is the biggest hidden cost in your organization, and it’s about to be exposed.**

Every alignment meeting, every design-by-committee, every consensus-driven prioritization exercise takes a sharp insight and regresses it toward the mean. The more people involved in a decision, the more the output converges on what nobody objects to, which is almost never the same as what’s actually right. Organizations know this. It’s why they invest in “empowered teams” and “decision rights frameworks.” But the fundamental incentive structure hasn’t changed: the enterprise talent model is optimized for reliable mediocrity. Consistent output. Predictable quality. Nothing too sharp, nothing too wrong, nothing too weird. Average by design.

AI-empowered individuals optimize for something structurally different: peak output from uncompromised judgment. Those two models are not on a spectrum. They are fundamentally incompatible operating systems.

Elad Gil illustrates this outside the startup context entirely. He backs forty-plus unicorns as a solo GP, with no investment committee, no partner votes, no consensus-driven deal memos. He takes high-conviction positions without waiting for market consensus. His returns consistently compete with institutional firms that have been around for decades. Same dynamic: undiluted judgment, moving at the speed of conviction, beating organizations built for consensus.

The transition is going to be brutal for organizations that can’t let go of the consensus model, because the people with the sharpest judgment, the ones who feel the compromise tax most acutely, are the ones most likely to leave.

## The framework: speed of control

If those theses are directionally right, we need a different framework for evaluating talent. “Years of experience” is decoupling from capability. “Taste” is too vague to diagnose anything. “AI skills” mistakes the tool for the trait.

The key variable is speed of control: the rate at which a person can cycle through high-quality decisions and see them executed. It’s a function of three components.

Judgment density: how much relevant pattern recognition does this person carry, calibrated to current conditions? Judgment density is perishable. It decays when the world changes and you don’t. It compounds when you iterate rapidly against real feedback. Ben Broca has high judgment density: fifteen years of cross-functional experience recently recalibrated through months of AI-native building. Pieter Levels has high judgment density: ten years of building in public, forty-plus shipped products, instincts pressure-tested by hundreds of small failures. Sarah Gwilliam has high judgment density in a different domain: years of direct work with grieving people, pattern recognition for what bereaved humans need that no amount of market research could replicate.

Conviction velocity: how quickly does this person move from insight to action without requiring external validation? This is not recklessness. It’s the earned confidence that comes from having been right enough times that you trust your own pattern matching. Ben built a Daft Punk about page because he wanted to, shipped it, and moved on. Gwilliam didn’t need a focus group to know that grief hits hardest at 2 AM when no therapist is available. Levels doesn’t run customer surveys; he builds, ships, and reads the revenue data.

Execution bandwidth: how much execution capacity can this person effectively direct? This is where AI enters. A solo founder with Claude Code, a fleet of task-specific agents, and infrastructure-as-code has execution bandwidth that previously required a team of twenty. A senior operator inside an enterprise with the same tools has the same potential, if the organization lets them use it. Execution bandwidth without judgment density produces fast garbage. Judgment density without execution bandwidth produces frustrated talent.

Speed of control = judgment density x conviction velocity x execution bandwidth.

Every solo founder I named maps cleanly to this formula. High on all three. Extraordinary output. The formula also explains why pure “vibe coding,” people with high execution bandwidth but low judgment density, produces a lot of professional-looking products that don’t work and don’t sell. Speed of control requires all three components. Any one missing collapses the whole product.

## The variable everyone gets wrong

Every conversation I hear about AI-era talent is about output volume. More code, more content, more products, faster. The implicit assumption is that what was previously scarce was raw productive capacity, and now AI has made it abundant.

This framing leads to disastrously wrong talent decisions. Volume is not the scarce resource. AI made volume cheap. What’s scarce is correctness: whether the thing you shipped is actually right. Not cosmetically right. Architecturally, strategically, fundamentally right.

A Harvard Business School field experiment tested this directly. Researchers studied 776 professionals at Procter & Gamble (an $84 billion CPG company, not a startup) working on real product innovation challenges. Individuals and two-person teams, some with AI and some without, all evaluated by independent experts blind to condition. AI-equipped individuals improved solution quality by 0.37 standard deviations, matching the performance of two-person teams without AI, and the strongest AI-augmented teams were significantly more likely to produce ideas in the top 10% of quality. Not more output. More likely to be correct at the highest level. The researchers called AI a “cybernetic teammate.”

And the mechanism matters for the talent question: without AI, R&D people produced technical solutions and marketing people produced commercial solutions, functional silos reflected in functional output. With AI, both groups produced more balanced, integrated ideas. AI extended each person’s judgment into adjacent domains. It didn’t add volume. It added correctness across a wider surface area.

This is what makes extraordinary individual talent so much more valuable now. A person with deep judgment density, operating at high conviction velocity, with AI extending their correctness into adjacent domains, is not just more productive than a committee. They are more correct, because their judgment is coherent across a single mental model, while a committee’s judgment is the average of multiple models that don’t fully align.

The dark side is real and worth naming plainly. AI makes it trivially easy to build the wrong thing fast, and the failure mode is harder to detect. A confident but miscalibrated person with high execution bandwidth ships professional-looking products that generate metrics resembling traction. The hockey stick chart looks the same whether the underlying business has staying power or not. Speed without correctness is just faster mistakes. And that means the ability to distinguish correct judgment from confident judgment becomes the highest-value evaluation skill in the entire organization.

## The ambition failure

Here is what I keep coming back to.

Everyone frames the talent question as a search problem. “How do we find people with taste?” “How do we identify extraordinary talent?” As if extraordinary talent were a rare mineral that must be mined from the earth through better recruiting.

This framing is a staggering failure of imagination.

You already have extraordinary talent. Probably a lot of it. You hired smart, experienced, capable people. Then you put them inside an organizational structure that eats 75% of their output in coordination overhead, rounds their sharpest instincts into committee-approved averages, punishes conviction by requiring consensus, and measures their performance on the reliable production of mediocre output rather than the intermittent production of brilliant output. You didn’t fail to find extraordinary people. You built a system that makes extraordinary people look ordinary.

Your performance management system measures people’s output within that structure, which is like measuring a Ferrari’s top speed in a parking garage and concluding it’s a slow car.

Maor Shlomo didn’t get smarter in the six months between “person nobody has heard of” and “$80M exit.” He had the same judgment the whole time. What changed was that the cost of translating his judgment into a working product dropped to near zero. Sarah Gwilliam didn’t suddenly develop taste when AI tools became available. She’d been accumulating domain judgment for years. What changed was that the blocker between her judgment and a functioning business disappeared.

How many Sarah Gwilliams do you have in your organization right now? How many people with deep domain judgment, strong conviction, and proven instincts who are operating at a fraction of their capacity because they’re trapped in an approval chain designed for a world where execution required teams?

You don’t know. And the reason you don’t know is that you’ve never measured what they can do when the garage walls come down. The way you find out is dead simple.

## How to find out what you’ve got

Give someone a scout mission. A real one. Not a training exercise. Not a hackathon. Pick a problem your company has been ignoring, something that matters, sitting in the backlog because nobody could staff it. Give one person full AI tooling, a week, and a clear objective: come back with a working prototype, a strategic recommendation, or a first draft that a strike team could execute against. No committee. No check-ins. No approvals. Just a person, the tools, and the problem.

What you’re testing for is the three components of speed of control.

Can they define the problem without being told how? This is what Shopify’s Tobi Lutke calls “context engineering,” the ability to specify a problem so completely that it’s solvable without follow-up clarification. The person who needs a product requirements document, a Jira ticket, and a meeting to understand what they’re building is not the same person who can look at a vague business problem and decompose it into something tight enough that AI agents can execute against it.

Do they know what “right” looks like? When AI produces output, can this person distinguish correct from merely plausible? Not at the syntax level, at the architecture level, the strategy level, the “does this actually solve the problem or does it just look like it does” level. People with judgment density reject subtly wrong AI output before it compounds into production failures.

Do they default to action or permission? In an AI-augmented model, you need people who see a problem and start solving it, rather than see a problem and schedule a meeting about it. The scout mission tests this directly: did they build something, or did they build a plan to build something?

Here’s the uncomfortable finding: the results will not track your current performance reviews. Some of your highest-rated people, the ones who navigate the existing org structure beautifully, run excellent meetings, write clear status updates, manage stakeholders with precision, will struggle. Those are coordination skills. They are extremely valuable in a large-team, consensus-driven structure. In an AI-augmented model optimized for speed of control, they are overhead.

Meanwhile, some of your most frustrating people, the ones who chafe at process, skip meetings, build things without asking, and occasionally ship something brilliant that nobody requested, may turn out to be exactly who you need. Those people have been fighting your organizational structure for their entire careers. The speed-of-control model is the structure they were built for.

Run ten scout missions. Give ten different people a week each. You’ll learn more about your organization’s actual talent capacity in ten weeks than any consulting engagement will tell you in ten months.

## Five questions to diagnose your organization

I’ll leave you with five questions. They are uncomfortable. They are also probably the most useful strategic exercise most leaders will do this year on the topic of talent.

One. Pick any person on your team. If you removed all organizational process and gave them AI execution capacity and complete autonomy, how much of their current output would increase, and how much of their current activity would simply disappear? The gap between those two numbers is the coordination tax your organization charges for the privilege of employing them. What, exactly, is that tax buying you?

Two. When was the last time a single person at your company built something real (not a deck, not a proposal, but a working prototype or shipped product) from scratch, in less than a week, that previously would have required a team and a quarter? If this hasn’t happened, either you don’t have the right people or you haven’t given them the tools and the permission. Both are fixable. Both are urgent. Because the people who can do this are increasingly choosing to do it for themselves.

Three. How many of your current performance criteria measure coordination skills (stakeholder management, consensus building, cross-functional alignment, status reporting) versus judgment quality and decision velocity? If the ratio skews toward coordination, you are systematically promoting people who are optimized for the old model and filtering out people who are optimized for the new one.

Four. If you gave your best person full AI execution capacity, removed all approval requirements, and pointed them at your biggest unsolved problem, what would they produce in thirty days? Now compare that to what they actually produced in the last thirty days within your existing structure. The gap between those two answers is the talent you’re suppressing. And every month you don’t close it, someone like that person, with the same judgment but without your organizational overhead, is building the thing that eats your market.

Five. This is the big one. How many of the people you’d describe as “solid performers” are actually extraordinary performers trapped in a structure that constrains them to solid performance? How would you know? What would you have to change to find out? And if the answer to that last question is “fundamentally restructure how we work,” are you willing to do it before your best people restructure themselves out of your org chart?

## The core thesis

The extraordinary talent you’re looking for is not rare. It is abundant. It is sitting in your organization right now, producing output that looks ordinary because your organizational structure is optimized to produce ordinary output.

The framework for identifying and developing extraordinary talent in this era is speed of control: judgment density times conviction velocity times execution bandwidth. Stop measuring years of experience; measure calibration recency. Stop rewarding consensus navigation; reward decision quality and speed. Stop hiring for execution skills that AI provides; hire for the ability to specify what “right” looks like and the conviction to ship without waiting for permission.

Every leader now faces the same fork: remove the overhead for your people, or watch your people remove it themselves by walking out the door, picking up an AI toolkit, and building in six months what your organization was going to build in three years.

The answer, for too many companies, will arrive as a resignation letter from someone they thought was merely good. That person will go solo, or join a five-person strike team, or build something from scratch with AI. And they will produce, in weeks, output that rivals what your fifty-person department planned to ship next year.

When that happens, the problem was never the talent. It was always the structure.
