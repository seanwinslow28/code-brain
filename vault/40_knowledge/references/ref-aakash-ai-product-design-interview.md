## 3\. The Companies Asking These Questions (And Their Specific Formats)

Before we go any further... Not all AI product design interviews are created equal.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8gIB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5a682f81-ec4b-43e1-b8a7-bed4e3930cc1_1080x1350.png)

*Let me explain the variations in question type - as well as how to adopt your answer given their varying missions.*

#### OpenAI

OpenAI’s loop typically includes a 30-minute recruiter screen, 45-minute hiring manager call, take-home assignment (often roadmap optimization), then a virtual onsite with 4-6 interviews covering product design, metrics, culture fit, and an engineering deep dive.

The product design round runs 45 minutes. One candidate was asked to design an AI product that helps people communicate with their pets.

A candidate I recently advised said it was very much like Meta.

> *“They have a structured interview process and rubric for AI product design interviews.”*

Every design question has an **implicit safety component**. If you propose a feature that could generate harmful content, they expect you to address it unprompted.

OpenAI’s stated mission is ensuring AGI benefits humanity, but what they actually optimize for is progress toward AGI through consumer products that fund research.

So come up with great consumer product ideas that show that muscle.

#### Anthropic

Anthropic’s 3-4 week process includes a recruiter screen, hiring manager deep dive, product sense and analytical rounds, then a values and culture fit conversation.

The product design round **blends design with ethical reasoning** and runs 45-60 minutes. One candidate was asked to design a Claude feature that helps journalists fact-check articles before publication.

A candidate I helped said it felt more like a conversation than an interview.

> *“They genuinely wanted to understand how I think about tradeoffs, not just whether I had the right answer.”*

Anthropic sits furthest toward safety-first on the spectrum. They call it a “race to the top” on safety.

So treat safety as a design constraint, not a checkbox. When you prioritize solutions, include “potential for misuse” as a real factor.

#### Google (Gemini / DeepMind)

Google adds an ML component to their standard 5-7 round PM process. If you’re interviewing for Gemini, expect a session with an ML engineer or applied scientist. DeepMind runs even longer.

The AI product design round runs 45 minutes. One candidate was asked to design an AI tutor that helps high school students learn calculus.

A PM I know who interviewed there said it was **exhausting**.

> *“Lots of 30-minute interviews back to back, each with pre-prepared questions. Very thorough.”*

The round also tests “Googleyness” in AI contexts. They’ll ask you to explain your AI design to a grandmother.

Google’s AI products serve billions of users across wildly different technical literacy levels. So show you can design for experts and make it comprehensible for everyone else. They also think in 10-year horizons, so connect your design to long-term ecosystem plays.

#### Microsoft

Microsoft’s AI PM interviews are 52% behavioral, more than any other major company. The AI product design round runs 45 minutes within the onsite loop.

One candidate was asked to design an AI assistant that helps small business owners manage their finances through Excel.

A candidate I advised said they **really pushed on storytelling**.

> *“Could I explain why this mattered, not just what I’d build.”*

Their AI products embed into existing workflows. Copilot lives inside Word, Excel, Teams. So emphasize integration over standalone experiences. They want enterprise productivity and defensibility, not flashy demos.

#### Meta

Meta’s AI PM interviews maintain the standard three-interview onsite: Product Sense, Analytical Thinking, Leadership & Drive. The AI product design round runs 35-40 minutes within Product Sense.

One candidate was asked to design an AI feature for WhatsApp that helps small businesses respond to customer inquiries.

A candidate I recently advised said **execution was where they really pushed**.

> *“They wanted specific metrics for every feature I proposed, not hand-wavy answers.”*

The AI product design round gets evaluated through a metrics lens. So always know how you’d measure success. When you propose a feature, immediately address hallucination rates, calibration, trust indicators. Designing the measurement matters as much as designing the product.

#### Amazon

Amazon layers AI questions onto their Leadership Principles framework. For PM-T roles on Alexa or Bedrock, expect AI/ML basics, system design, and the LP gauntlet.

You’ll write a PR/FAQ document 48 hours before your onsite. The AI product design round runs 45-60 minutes within the onsite loop.

One Alexa candidate was asked to design an AI shopping assistant that helps users find products based on vague descriptions like “something cozy for my living room.”

One candidate said **they wanted him to write the press release first**.

> *“What’s the headline? What customer problem does this solve? Only then could I get into the AI capabilities.”*

So start from customer need, not AI capability. Design the announcement before you design the product.

#### Adobe

Adobe’s process includes a presentation: 20 minutes on a past project, then 45 minutes of Q&A with senior PMs. The AI product design round is often embedded in the Q&A or as a separate 45-minute case.

One Firefly candidate was asked to design an AI feature for Premiere Pro that helps video editors automatically cut footage to match the beat of a song.

A candidate I recently advised said they kept asking how his feature would make designers feel.

> *“Not just what it does, but whether creatives would trust it.”*

Creative professionals want control. They don’t want AI that automates their job. They want AI that removes friction while keeping them in the driver’s seat. So center the tension between capability and creative control.

#### Figma

Figma’s interview includes a panel presentation to 5+ people and up to six 1:1s. The AI product design round runs 45-60 minutes.

One candidate was asked to design an AI feature that helps non-designers create professional-looking presentations.

A candidate I advised said **they didn’t want a final answer**.

> *“They wanted to watch me think through the problem live, change my mind, consider alternatives.”*

Their CPO Yuhki Yamashita on Lenny’s Podcast said they evaluate: “exploring solutions and moving up/down branches of exploration quickly” and “command of different perspectives.”

So don’t deliver a polished answer. Think out loud, explore branches, hold multiple perspectives. They want to see you grapple with AI-human collaboration tension live.

## 4\. Types of Product Design Questions + Example Question Bank

AI Product Design questions fall into five distinct categories.

#### How These Questions Differ From Other AI PM Rounds

Companies also test these skills in separate rounds. Don’t confuse them with AI Product Design:

- **Metrics Questions**: *“How would you measure success for Claude Projects?”*
- **Safety Questions**: *“Imagine you’ve trained a model with unusually strong memorization of sensitive data. What steps would you take next?”*
- **Technical Communication**: *“Explain embeddings to a product designer on your team.”*
- **Execution/Prioritization**: *“How would you prioritize between model improvements and application-layer UX changes?”*
- **Competitive Landscape**: *“How would you ensure product defensibility in an AI-saturated market?”*

The AI Product Design round specifically tests whether you can go from a problem statement to a coherent product design that accounts for AI’s unique characteristics.

*And there are 5 types.*

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zaRO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F06c0a6de-cf71-4a8f-b098-c24a9027abcc_1230x1448.png)

#### Type 1 - Product Improvement Questions

These give you an existing AI product with a specific problem to solve:

- *“Claude’s code generation has 40% thumbs down rate from non-technical users. You have two months. How do you fix it?”*
- *“How would you increase weekly active users of ChatGPT image creation from 175M to 350M in 3 months with only 3 engineers?”*

What they’re testing is: Can you diagnose an existing AI product’s problems, identify the right user segments affected, and propose solutions within realistic constraints? The constraint element matters. They want to see you prioritize.

#### Type 2 - New Product Design Questions

These ask you to design an AI product from scratch for a specific use case or market:

- *“Design an AI product to help communicate with pets.”*
- *“Design an AI-first travel agent for someone who has never planned a trip before.”*

They’re testing if you can identify the right users, understand their problems deeply, and propose creative AI-powered solutions that leverage current capabilities? This is where most candidates struggle. They either go too narrow (single feature) or too broad (vague platform).

In my experience, new product design questions trip up more candidates than any other type. People underestimate how deeply you need to explore the user segments and their specific cognitive tasks before jumping to solutions.

#### Type 3 - Platform/API Questions

These go deeper into the technical architecture of AI systems:

- *“Design an API for developers to build safe AI applications.”*
- *“How would you design the model selection interface for a multi-model AI platform?”*

They’re testing if you understand how AI platforms work? Can you think about developer experience, model routing, safety guardrails, and enterprise requirements? These require more technical depth than consumer product questions.

#### Type 4 - Constraint-Based Questions

These add specific constraints that force difficult trade-offs:

- *“Design an AI product for healthcare that can operate in low-connectivity environments.”*
- *“Build an AI assistant that complies with GDPR, CCPA, and India’s data localization requirements, all while maintaining consistent performance.”*

They’re seeing if you can design within real-world constraints. Legal requirements, infrastructure limitations, and regulatory environments all shape what’s actually shippable. Candidates who ignore constraints get dinged.

#### Type 5 - UX/UI for AI Questions

These focus specifically on interface design for AI-powered features:

- *“Design a better interface for our super agent.”*
- *“How would you redesign the image generation flow in ChatGPT?”*

They want to know if you understand how to surface AI capabilities in ways users can actually understand and control? Transparency, confidence indicators, undo mechanisms, and graceful degradation all matter here.

## 5\. The Framework That Actually Works (From Real Interviews)

So we’ve covered the 5 types. What framework do you use to conquer all 5? Well, it’s not CIRCLES (from [Lewis Lin](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jkTPnrqs1zw)).

*Here’s the 10-step framework I recommend:*

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X7ZS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F11445727-e3d6-454f-b7d2-66eb5b1f7349_800x2000.png)

#### Step 1: Clarifying Questions

Before even developing your framework, spend 2 minutes on clarifications.

Cover off on topics like:

- What’s the goal
- Are there specific focus areas
- How much time to spend on this question

In the mock, you see me even have a back-and-forth discussion with Bart. He said revenue but I also related it back to the *company mission*.

Why was I doing that? I wanted to use this time productively to **influence what you do later**. And you saw: it did affect what I did later.

Don’t skip clarifications!

#### Step 2: User Segmentation

It sounds tacky, I know. But you want to identify *everyone* in the ecosystem.

In the case, you saw that I initially missed segmenting by pet type. I just focused on buyer segments.

The goal you’re setting for yourself IS to **catch yourself** like I did. Set aside some time to think about the relevant vectors of segmentation for the case. Identify them. Catch yourself.

Maybe you’re doing a case at Doordash, eg. You might focus a lot on Dashers. But what about customers? And Doordash themselves? Use the segmentation section to think about *all players* in an ecosystem.

It matters for product design.

#### Step 3: Problem Identification

For your chosen segment, brainstorm the problems they face.

The key question: what *cognitive work* are they doing poorly? What decisions are hard? What information is missing? Where do they make expensive mistakes they can’t recover from?

In the mock, I rattled off seven problems in about 60 seconds. That’s the right pace. Don’t filter yet. Don’t prioritize yet. Just get them out.

You’ll be tempted to jump to solutions here. **Resist.** The best candidates sit in the problem space longer than feels comfortable.

#### Step 4: Problem Prioritization

Not all problems are equal. Your job is to create a **hierarchy**.

I think of it as a pyramid. Some problems are *foundational*. Others depend on solving the foundation first. If users can’t capture the basic inputs, your advice layer is useless.

In the mock, Bart reminded me mid-interview that I hadn’t tied back to AGI. He was right. So I reframed my pyramid to include it: understanding animal behavior creates data for emotional intelligence. Simpler organisms first, then humans. That became a *prioritization input*, not just a closing flourish.

If your interviewer emphasizes a goal, use it to prioritize. Don’t just acknowledge it and wander off.

#### Step 5: Creative Solution Generation

Brainstorm 5-8 solutions. Don’t self-edit yet.

The goal is **range**. Hardware and software. Active and passive. Consumer and expert. Real-time and asynchronous. Push yourself to cover the solution space.

Why does range matter? Your best idea often comes from *combining* two mediocre ones. You won’t see that combination if you only generated three ideas that all look the same.

In the mock, I generated seven solutions. Some were boring. Some were wild. That’s fine. You need the raw material before you can sculpt.

### Step 6: Prioritization with Clear Criteria

Create a table. Actually *score* each solution.

Pick 3-4 criteria that matter for this specific case: user impact, technical feasibility, differentiation, engagement potential. Whatever fits. Then rate each solution against them.

Here’s what kills candidates: they say “I’d prioritize based on impact and effort” and then just pick their favorite. Interviewers see through this immediately. They want to **watch you make decisions**, not hear that you’re capable of making them.

Do the prioritization visibly. Show your math.

#### Step 7: Combine and Design

Your top solutions might combine into something stronger.

In the mock, real-time behavior coach ranked first. Conversation simulator ranked second. But together? The coach provides ongoing utility. The conversation mode creates the *magic moment*. Neither alone is as strong as both together.

Look for this pattern. One solution handles practical value. Another creates emotional stickiness or differentiation. The **combination** often exceeds both.

#### Step 8: Core Flows

Design the actual product. This is where most candidates fail.

They describe a vague feature. “It would have a dashboard.” “Users could see insights.” That’s not design. Design means: what screens exist, what state is the system in, what triggers the AI, what does the user see, what data are you collecting, what happens when it fails.

Map out things like the **setup phase**, the **primary usage modes**, and the **magic moment**. Get specific enough that an engineer could start asking clarifying questions.

#### Step 9: Mission Connection + Risks

Connect back to company mission. Address risks head-on.

Every AI product fails sometimes. Your design must account for this. What happens when it gives bad advice? How do you calibrate trust? What are the privacy implications? How do you handle edge cases?

Candidates who ignore risks look *naive*. Candidates who dwell on risks look *scared*. The right move: **acknowledge, mitigate, move on**.

#### Step 10: The Compelling Narrative

Close with a user story.

Name a specific person. Describe their pain vividly. Walk through how they discover and use the product. Show the transformation. End on the emotional payoff.

Why does this matter? Interviewers *remember stories*. They’ll retell your story in the debrief. “She had this great example about a user named Sarah who...” Give them something to retell.

Make yours concrete.

*The key with all of this? Allocate your time well!*

## 6\. Anti-Patterns That Kill Candidates

In [coaching people](https://www.landpmjob.com/) on this interview… I see the same mistakes pop up again and again:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KfKE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F179f81f5-119e-49c6-bcc8-9faeb2e82188_932x1362.png)

*Lend me one more minute to cover each one in depth…*

#### Anti-Pattern 1 - Starting With Problems Instead of Users

Some candidates lead with “here are the problems to solve” and then retrofit users who might have those problems. This seems logical. Problems feel concrete. Users feel squishy.

But for novel AI products, starting with users reveals cognitive tasks you’d never discover otherwise. Consider pet communication AI. “New pet owners learning to interpret their first dog’s bark” are doing fundamentally different mental work than “professional trainers optimizing behavior patterns.” The new owner needs translation and reassurance. The trainer needs precision and measurement. These require *completely different products*. You only see that distinction when you start with users.

Always segment users first. Then explore their problems. **The order matters.**

#### Anti-Pattern 2 - Focusing on the Wrong User

For pet communication AI, some candidates focus on what’s good for the pets.

Pets don’t buy products. Pet owners do.

It’s like designing children’s toys to appeal to toddlers instead of parents. The toddler doesn’t make the purchase decision. Even though you’re building an AI to understand animals, your user is the human who will pay for and use the product. Ask yourself: **who pays?** Who uses daily? Who has the problem severe enough to seek a solution? That’s your user.

#### Anti-Pattern 3 - Solutions Without Prioritization Math

Candidates generate five or six solutions, then say “I’d prioritize these based on impact and effort” without actually doing the math.

Interviewers want to see your decision-making *process*, not just hear that you have one. Generic prioritization frameworks without application show you know the theory but can’t execute.

Create a visible table with criteria. Score each solution. **Show which ones rise to the top and why.** Even better: show how multiple solutions combine to exceed a target.

#### Anti-Pattern 4 - Forgetting Company Mission

Candidates design products that make sense generically but don’t connect to why *this* company would build it.

OpenAI isn’t building pet communication AI because pets are cute. They’re building it because animal behavior understanding creates data for emotional intelligence that advances AGI. If you miss that connection, you’ve missed the strategic rationale.

Early in the interview, clarify success metrics. If they say “engagement,” push back: “I know OpenAI’s mission is progress toward AGI. Should I be thinking about how this advances that goal?” Then **connect your solution back to that mission** at the end.

#### Anti-Pattern 5 - Missing the Design Section Entirely

Some candidates spend 40 minutes on users and problems and solutions, then realize they have five minutes left and never actually *design* the product.

It’s an AI Product **DESIGN** interview.

The interviewer wants to see flows, screens, modes of interaction. Not just strategy.

After prioritizing solutions, explicitly say “let me design the core flows.”

Map out the setup phase, primary usage modes, and the magic moment that creates differentiation. Get specific:

- What does the user see?
- What happens in each state?

#### Anti-Pattern 6 - Ignoring the Interviewer’s Signals

The interviewer asks “should you add the AGI goal to your prioritization table?” and the candidate says “no, let’s not do that” without addressing the underlying concern.

Interviewers give hints when you’re going off track. Dismissing them without engaging signals arrogance or inflexibility. When an interviewer suggests something, **take it seriously**. Even if you disagree, engage with the reasoning.

Recall from my mock: I responded, “That’s a good point. Here’s how I’m thinking about the AGI connection...”

A response like that shows you’re collaborative. Shutting it down shows you’re not.

#### Anti-Pattern 7 - Never Drawing Anything

Candidates who only talk score lower on execution. The visual structure helps both you and the interviewer. You can focus on one block at a time while processing the next. The interviewer can see *exactly* where you are in your thinking. Without it, even great ideas get lost.

**Draw your framework at the start.** Label sections. Work through each visually. Even if you’re remote on a video call, **use screen share with Miro or FigJam**.

*And that covers it for my advice on the interview!*

## 7\. Worked Examples: Applying the Framework

We’re reaching the end of the deep dive.

Below are three worked examples across different AI Product Design categories.

Here’s how to use these:

DON’T JUST READ THEM!

Practice each section yourself and then progress to compare your answer to the example.

These are practice canvases. Not things to read!

### Example 1: Product Improvement Question

**Question**: “Claude’s code generation has 40% thumbs down rate from non-technical users. You have two months. How do you fix it?”

#### Clarifying Questions

Before jumping into analysis, nail down the constraints:

- What’s the baseline for technical users? (Let’s say 15% thumbs down.) This tells you the gap is specifically a non-technical user problem, not a model quality problem.
- What counts as “non-technical”? (Users who we categorize via LLM.) Now you know who to focus on.
- What resources do we have? (One product team of 5 engineers.) This constraint shapes what’s actually possible in two months.
- What’s the primary use case for these users? (Automating workflows, generating scripts for personal use, building simple tools.) This reveals the cognitive tasks they’re attempting.

#### User Segmentation

Even within “non-technical users,” there are meaningful distinctions:

- **Marketers trying to automate workflows** — want code that connects tools like Zapier, automates reports, or scrapes data
- **Business analysts generating SQL queries** — have some technical familiarity but hit walls on complex joins or optimization
- **Content creators building simple automations** — want personal productivity tools, often have no idea what’s possible
- **Students learning to code** — using Claude as a tutor, need explanation alongside output

The largest segment by volume is marketers and content creators. They represent about 60% of the thumbs-down feedback in this hypothetical scenario. Focus there.

#### Problem Identification

Through user interviews and feedback analysis, you find these core problems for marketers and content creators:

- **Code doesn’t work when copied** — the most common issue. They paste it into their environment and get errors they don’t understand.
- **Error messages are incomprehensible** — they see “TypeError: cannot read property ‘x’ of undefined” and have no idea what to do next.
- **No debugging pathway** — they’re stuck. They either abandon the task or ask Claude again without useful context.
- **Mismatch between expectation and output** — they asked for something and got something adjacent but not quite right.
- **Don’t know what they can ask for** — they don’t understand the boundaries of what’s possible.

#### Problem Prioritization

Create a hierarchy. “Code doesn’t work when copied” is the foundational problem. Everything else is downstream of that. If the code worked on the first try, they wouldn’t need to understand error messages or debug.

But “mismatch between expectation and output” often causes the first failure. They ask for “a script to pull my sales data” without specifying the source, format, or destination. Claude generates reasonable code that doesn’t match their actual setup.

So the prioritization is:

1. **Reduce mismatch through better elicitation** (prevent failures)
2. **Increase first-try success rate** (fix failures before they happen)
3. **Provide recovery pathways when code fails** (handle remaining failures)

#### Solution Generation

Generate five to eight solutions without self-editing:

1. **Clarifying question flow** — Before generating code, Claude asks 2-3 targeted questions about their environment, expected output, and constraints
2. **Auto-execute in sandbox** — Run the generated code in an isolated environment and show results before the user copies it
3. **One-click error fixing** — When code fails, a single button triggers Claude to diagnose and fix
4. **Progressive complexity** — Start with templates and pre-built solutions, unlock custom code as users demonstrate competence
5. **Plain English explanation layer** — Every code block comes with a natural language description of what it does
6. **Environment detection** — Ask users once about their setup (Mac/Windows, what tools they use), then tailor all outputs
7. **Video walkthrough generation** — For complex scripts, generate a brief visual guide showing where code goes
8. **Confidence scoring** — Show users how likely the code is to work in their environment: “High confidence for Google Sheets on Mac”

#### Solution Prioritization

Build a scoring table:

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sPxs!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd91e8ce2-bc0b-42ef-9a99-cdd2a7629347_1294x662.png)

Top two: **Auto-execute sandbox** (addresses root cause, high differentiation) + **one-click error fixing** (high feasibility, handles remaining failures). These combine into a coherent system.

#### Designing the Product

This is where most candidates rush. Take your time here. The design section demonstrates whether you actually know how to build products.

**Core Experience Flow**

The user journey breaks into three modes: Generation, Validation, and Recovery.

**Mode 1: Generation**

User asks: “Write me a script that pulls my email open rates from Mailchimp and puts them in a Google Sheet.”

Claude responds: “Before I generate this, let me confirm a few things:

- Do you have a Mailchimp API key set up, or should I include instructions for that?
- Which Google Sheet should this go to: an existing one or should the script create a new one?
- How often do you want this to run: once, or on a schedule?”

User answers, and Claude generates code with their specific parameters baked in.

**Mode 2: Validation**

Instead of just showing code, the interface displays:

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Your Script                                     │
│ [Code block - collapsed by default]             │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ What this does:                                 │
│ "Connects to your Mailchimp account, pulls open │
│  rates from the last 30 days, and adds them to  │
│  row 2 of your 'Email Metrics' sheet."          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Test Results: ✓ Passed                          │
│ "I ran this in a test environment. It           │
│  successfully connected to Mailchimp and wrote  │
│  sample data to a test sheet."                  │
│                                                 │
│ [Show test output]  [Copy to clipboard]         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The sandbox runs automatically. The user sees results before copying anything. The “Show test output” button reveals actual data that was pulled in the test.

If the sandbox test fails:

```markup
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│ "The Mailchimp connection worked, but I couldn't │
│  find a sheet called 'Email Metrics.' Do you     │
│  want me to create one, or use a different       │
│  sheet name?"                                    │
│                                                  │
│ [Create new sheet]  [Enter sheet name]           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The user never sees raw errors. Every failure is translated into a decision they can make.

**Mode 3: Recovery**

When the user runs code in their own environment and it fails, they come back to Claude. The interface now shows:

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Something went wrong?                           │
│                                                 │
│ [Paste error message]  or  [Describe what       │
│                             happened]           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Quick fixes:                                    │
│ ○ I got a permission error                      │
│ ○ The data looks wrong                          │
│ ○ Nothing happened                              │
│ ○ Something else                                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Selecting “I got a permission error” triggers Claude to ask: “Which service gave you the permission error, Mailchimp or Google Sheets?” Based on the answer, Claude provides step-by-step instructions for fixing permissions, then regenerates the code with better error handling.

**Key Interface Elements**

- **Collapsed code by default** — Non-technical users don’t need to see code unless they want to. The summary and test results are primary.
- **Action buttons, not instructions** — Instead of “Copy the code and paste it into Apps Script,” show a button that copies with one click and a tooltip explaining where to paste.
- **Confidence indicators** — “High confidence” / “Medium confidence—your setup may differ” / “Low confidence—let’s verify before running”
- **Progressive disclosure** — Simple view by default. “Show technical details” expands to full code and logs.

**Edge Cases to Design For**

- **User’s environment differs from sandbox** — Acknowledge this explicitly: “This ran successfully in our test environment. If your setup differs, let me know and I’ll adjust.”
- **Code requires authentication** — Guide users through API key setup with screenshots and validation (”Paste your key here to test the connection before copying the full script”).
- **Long-running scripts** — For scripts that take minutes to run, set expectations: “This might take 2-3 minutes. Here’s how you’ll know it’s working...”

#### Progress Toward Mission and Risks

Connect to Anthropic’s mission: “Improving code generation success rates for non-technical users advances Claude’s usefulness as a general assistant. These users represent the majority of humanity—most people aren’t engineers. Making Claude work for them extends the mission of beneficial AI.”

**Risks**:

- **False confidence from sandbox** — Users might think “it worked in test” guarantees it works everywhere. Mitigation: Clear messaging that test environments differ from production.
- **Over-reliance on Claude** — Users never learn to debug themselves. Mitigation: “Explain this error to me” option that teaches alongside fixing.
- **Scope creep** — Two months isn’t enough for the full vision. Ship sandbox + error flow first. Progressive complexity and video generation are v2.

#### Metrics

- Primary: Thumbs-down rate from non-technical users (40% → target 20%)
- Secondary: Time-to-working-code (measure current baseline, target 50% reduction)
- Secondary: Error recovery rate (what % of users who hit an error get to working code without starting over)
- Leading indicator: Sandbox test pass rate (proxy for code quality)

#### Compelling Narrative

“Picture Maya, a marketing manager at a D2C brand. The code looked right, but when she pasted it into Google Sheets, she got an error she didn’t understand. She gave up.

Now she tries again. She asks for an automation. Claude asks three quick questions about her setup. She answers. Claude generates the script, runs it in a sandbox, shows her: ‘Here’s what I pulled from your Mailchimp test data.’ It works. She copies with one click, follows the brief setup instructions, and ten minutes later she has an automation that will save her two hours every week.

That’s a 40% thumbs-down rate becoming a bookmark, a share with her team, a reason to trust Claude with bigger problems.”

### Example 2: New Product Design Question

**Question**: “Design an AI-first travel agent for someone who has never planned a trip before.”

#### Clarifying Questions

- **Domestic or international?** (Start with domestic to reduce complexity: passports, visas, foreign currency add friction. Expand later.)
- **Are we standalone or within an existing product?** (Standalone product. This means we own the full experience but also need to handle booking integrations.)
- **Success metric?** (Completed bookings. Revenue is downstream, start with conversion.)
- **What’s the timeline?** (MVP in 6 months, not relevant to design but shapes scope.)

#### User Segmentation

“Never planned a trip before” is a broad category. Break it down:

- **Young adults planning first solo trip** (18-24, high anxiety, often budget-constrained, scared of making expensive mistakes)
- **Couples planning first vacation together** (new relationship milestone, different preferences to merge, budget conversations)
- **Parents planning first family trip** (logistics nightmare, kid-friendly concerns, different needs by age of children)
- **Recent retirees with new free time** (budget flexibility, physical accessibility concerns, first time traveling without work constraints)
- **International students traveling in a new country** (unfamiliar with local norms, language considerations, documentation stress)

Focus: **Young adults planning first solo trip**. Why? Highest anxiety means highest willingness to delegate decisions. Most decision paralysis means most value from AI assistance. Budget constraints mean they can’t afford mistakes: they need this to work. Plus, this segment represents early adopters who will evangelize if the product is good.

#### Problem Identification

Interview and research this segment. Their problems:

- **Don’t know where to start** — The blank page problem. “I want to go somewhere” but the infinity of options freezes them.
- **Fear of making expensive mistakes** — A bad flight, a sketchy hotel, a missed connection. They don’t know what they don’t know.
- **Hidden costs and logistics** — They budget for the flight and hotel, then realize they need airport transfers, travel insurance, activity tickets, tipping customs.
- **Analysis paralysis** — They read 47 TripAdvisor reviews and are more confused than when they started.
- **No one to validate decisions** — Their friends haven’t been there either. They have no trusted advisor.
- **Don’t understand the tradeoffs** — Is a longer layover bad? Is this hotel neighborhood safe? Is this airline reliable?

#### Problem Prioritization

Map these to the cognitive work:

1. **Foundation**: “Don’t know where to start” and “don’t understand tradeoffs” — they lack the mental model
2. **Middle layer**: “Analysis paralysis” and “hidden costs” — they have too much information, not enough synthesis
3. **Surface layer**: “Fear of mistakes” and “no validation” — emotional reassurance needs

Solve foundation first. If they have a clear mental model of what kind of trip they want, the paralysis and fear reduce. Start with destination selection and trip shaping.

#### Solution Generation

- **Vibe-based discovery** — “What do you want to feel?” → AI matches feeling to destinations. No overwhelming destination lists.
- **Budget guardrails** — Set total budget once. AI prevents overspending, flags hidden costs before they surprise.
- **Decision validation** — “87% of first-time travelers to Austin loved it. Here’s what they said.” Social proof from similar users.
- **One-decision-at-a-time flow** — Never show the full complexity. One screen per major decision.
- **Trip preview** — Before committing, see your daily itinerary. Visualize what each day looks like.
- **Tradeoff explainers** — When presenting options, explain what you’re trading. “The cheaper flight has a 4-hour layover. That’s long but you could explore the airport or rest.”
- **Emergency backup** — “If anything goes wrong, here’s what to do.” Pre-written contingency plans for common issues.
- **Local insider tips** — For each destination, the non-obvious stuff: best neighborhoods, tourist traps to avoid, what to pack.

### Example 3: UX/UI for AI Question

**Question**: “Design a better interface for AI-assisted code review.”

#### Clarifying Questions

- **What’s wrong with the current interface?** (Developers ignore suggestions; the noise-to-signal ratio is too high. Current acceptance rate is 12%.)
- **What platform?** (GitHub PR flow. Most of the engineering world lives here.)
- **Who’s the primary user?** (Engineers reviewing their own code before merge, and tech leads reviewing others’ PRs.)
- **Success metric?** (Suggestion acceptance rate. Secondary: bugs caught before merge.)

#### User Segmentation

- **Senior engineers (skeptical)** — High standards, have seen bad tools before, hate anything that wastes their time
- **Junior engineers (eager)** — Want to learn, might over-rely on AI suggestions, need guidance on which suggestions matter
- **Tech leads reviewing others’ code** — Time-constrained, want to catch issues quickly, need to balance teaching with shipping

Focus: **Senior engineers**. If they adopt, juniors follow. If they dismiss the tool as noise, it’s dead. Winning senior engineers is the unlock.

#### Problem Identification

Why do senior engineers ignore AI suggestions?

- **Too many suggestions, most are style nitpicks** — “Add a blank line here” when they’re looking for logic bugs
- **Can’t tell which suggestions actually matter** — The critical security issue is buried among 47 formatting suggestions
- **Don’t trust AI understanding of their codebase** — AI suggests patterns that don’t match their team’s conventions
- **Accepting suggestions feels like admitting weakness** — “I need AI to write my code?” Ego plays a role.
- **Suggestions lack context** — “This is bad” without “Here’s why and here’s what your team usually does”

#### Problem Prioritization

The foundational problem is **severity blindness** —everything looks equally important. Solve that first. If critical issues surface clearly, engagement follows.

Second priority: **context proof**. If suggestions demonstrate understanding of the codebase, trust builds.

#### Solution Generation

- **Severity tiers** — Critical (will cause bugs) / Important (best practice) / Optional (style). Visual hierarchy by severity.
- **Context proof** — “Based on your team’s patterns in \[file\], this violates your convention.” Show the AI did its homework.
- **One-click apply with diff preview** — Don’t make them copy-paste. Show what changes, apply with one click.
- **“Teach me” mode** — Explain WHY this is better, not just WHAT to change. Optional for those who want to learn.
- **Suggestion stats** — “You accepted 80% of Critical suggestions, 30% of Optional last month.” Feedback on their own patterns.
- **Confidence indicators** — “High confidence” vs “Might be worth checking” vs “Suggestion—take it or leave it”
- **Batch actions** — “Accept all formatting suggestions” so nitpicks don’t require individual decisions
- **Snooze for repo** — “Stop suggesting this pattern in this repo” for legitimate exceptions

#### Solution Prioritization

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0X7w!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4312b283-6d45-4fd1-b821-687b742c204b_1276x668.png)

Top combination: **Severity tiers** + **context proof** + **confidence indicators**. This trio addresses the core problems: too much noise, lack of trust, and unclear prioritization.

**PR View: The New Default**

When a developer opens a PR with AI review enabled, they don’t see 47 inline comments. They see a summary:

The default view surfaces only what matters. Optional suggestions are hidden by default. Senior engineers can access them, but they’re not noise.

**Critical Issue Display**

Expanding “Show Critical”:

```markup
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ ⛔ CRITICAL: Possible null pointer              │
│                                                 │
│ Location: src/api/handler.js, line 42           │
│                                                 │
│ The Problem:                                    │
│ user.profile.name is accessed without a null    │
│ check. If user.profile is undefined, this       │
│ crashes the request handler.                    │
│                                                 │
│ Why I'm confident (High):                       │
│ In this repo, 6 other files use optional        │
│ chaining for user.profile. This file doesn't.   │
│ Crash reports show 3 incidents from this        │
│ pattern last month.                             │
│                                                 │
│ Suggested fix:                                  │
│ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │
│ │ - const name = user.profile.name;           │ │
```

Notice the structure:

1. **Severity badge** — immediate visual priority
2. **Plain English problem statement** — what’s wrong, not just “warning”
3. **Context proof** — why the AI is confident, with repo-specific evidence
4. **Inline diff** — see exactly what changes
5. **One-click actions** — apply without copy-paste

**Important Suggestion Display**


- Red background for Critical
- Yellow background for Important
- Gray background for Optional

**Confidence Calibration**

The confidence indicator isn’t just decoration. It’s calibrated:

- **High confidence**: >90% historical acceptance rate for this pattern
- **Medium confidence**: 60-90% acceptance rate, or AI is uncertain about context
- **Suggestion**: <60% acceptance rate, or explicitly style-based

Engineers learn to trust High confidence suggestions. The system earns credibility by being right when it says it’s confident.


This closes the loop. Engineers see their feedback matters. The tool improves visibly.

**Edge Cases**

- **What if AI is wrong about Critical?** Every dismiss for Critical triggers a review. False positives in Critical erode trust fastest, prioritize precision over recall for this tier.
- **What if engineers dismiss everything?** Track acceptance rates by engineer. If someone dismisses 95% of Critical, either the tool is broken for their use case or they’re not the right adopter to focus on.
- **What about suggestions that conflict with intentional patterns?** “Snooze for repo” option for legitimate exceptions. The AI learns: “This repo intentionally doesn’t use optional chaining.”
- **What about PRs with 100+ suggestions?** Auto-collapse to summary view. Show “47 formatting issues” not 47 individual comments.

#### Progress Toward Mission and Risks

For GitHub: “Code review is where quality happens or doesn’t. Current AI review has a 12% acceptance rate, 88% noise. If we improve acceptance to 40%, we’ve reduced engineering time wasted on dismissed suggestions and increased bugs caught. That’s a multiplier on every engineering team on GitHub.”

**Risks**:

- **Senior engineers dismiss it as “AI slop”** — Mitigation: Lead with Critical-only mode. Prove value before expanding.
- **Over-reliance by juniors** — Mitigation: “Teach me” mode explains reasoning, not just fixes. Learning happens.
- **Confidence indicators become gameable** — Mitigation: Calibrate on actual acceptance, not self-reported confidence. The math keeps it honest.

#### Metrics

- Primary: Suggestion acceptance rate by severity tier (Critical: target 70%, Important: target 50%, Optional: target 30%)
- Secondary: Time spent in review (should decrease as noise decreases)
- Secondary: Bugs caught before merge (leading indicator: Critical issues found per 1000 LOC)
- Retention: % of engineers who keep AI review enabled after 30 days

#### Compelling Narrative

“Picture a senior engineer, let’s call her Priya. She’s seen AI code review before. Last time, it flagged her for not adding comments to obvious code. She disabled it within a week.

This time, she opens a PR and sees: ‘1 Critical issue.’ That’s it. Not 50 suggestions. One. She clicks in. The AI shows her: ‘You’re accessing user.profile.name without a null check. Six other files in this repo use optional chaining here. Last month, three crashes came from this exact pattern.’

Priya’s eyebrows go up. The AI knows her repo. It cited her team’s patterns. It’s not generic advice. It’s specific to how they build.

She clicks ‘Apply fix.’ Done.

Over the next month, she catches two more Critical issues before they hit production. Her acceptance rate for Critical suggestions is 85%. She tells her team lead: ‘This thing actually works.’

That’s how you win over skeptics. Not by being loud. By being right when it matters.”

### The Pattern Across All Three Examples

Notice what’s consistent:

1. **Clarifying questions narrow the problem**  
	You can’t design a good product if you haven’t defined the constraints.
2. **User segmentation identifies who and why them**  
	The “who” determines everything downstream. Pick wrong and the whole design misfires.
3. **Problems map cognitive tasks**  
	What mental work are users struggling with? AI products offload cognition—you need to know which cognition.
4. **Solutions generate breadth before narrowing**  
	Five to eight ideas, then score against criteria. Don’t fall in love with your first idea.
5. **Prioritization uses explicit criteria**  
	A table with scores, not “I feel like this one is best.” Show your math.
6. **Design gets specific about flows, states, and the magic moment**  
	This is where 90% of candidates fail. They describe a vague feature. You need to describe screens, interactions, edge cases, and what success looks like.
7. **Risks address what happens when AI fails**  
	Every AI product fails sometimes. Your design must account for failure states, trust calibration, and recovery pathways.

This pattern works regardless of question type. Practice it until the structure becomes automatic, and you can spend your mental energy on the creative parts: the novel solutions and the compelling narrative.
