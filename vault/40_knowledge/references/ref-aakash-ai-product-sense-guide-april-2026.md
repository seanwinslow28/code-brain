## 1\. What Changed Since October 2025

When I wrote the [first guide](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-product-sense-interview), I said “ *this interview doesn’t exist yet, but it’s coming.*” Six months later, it’s here.

#### The round is no longer optional. It’s spreading everywhere.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!U7DF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F87e79fec-97de-4abe-ac70-fc22f1ef0036_2400x3008.png)

Ankit categorized the landscape into three tiers based on what he actually experienced during his search -

1. **Tier 1 - In Everything.** OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind. AI product sense is part of every interview in some way. At OpenAI, you get questions like “ *how would you double ChatGPT image creation with just three engineers?*” At Anthropic, “ *how would you increase Claude Code WAU 10x?*”
2. **Tier 2 - Added to existing loops explicitly.** Meta is the biggest shift here. They literally added a 4th interview called “ *Product Sense with AI* ” for IC6+/M1/M2 roles. This is specific to the Central Products org. Figma has also added a specific round called ‘AI Product Sense.’
3. **Tier 3 - Woven into 1-2 rounds.** For instance, LinkedIn asked Ankit about how OpenAI and Anthropic launching into their markets should reshape product strategy in another interview. This is the sneaky one. Your recruiter email won’t mention “ *AI product sense.*” But if you’re interviewing for an AI PM role, expect it.

Here’s the thing Ankit said that hit me hardest.

> *“Even at companies that don’t have a dedicated AI round, AI fluency is being evaluated inside the regular and traditional product sense round. The bar has very much shifted.”*

I’d triple-click on this for you. **If your recruiter doesn't list 'AI product sense,' that doesn't mean you won't be tested on it. It means it's embedded inside a round with a different name.** The candidates who prep for it specifically are the ones who walk out knowing they nailed it.

#### The nature of what’s being tested has changed.

In a traditional product sense interview, you’re designing features for a deterministic system. User clicks a button, something predictable happens. You can use CIRCLES. You can follow a template. You can honestly pattern-match your way through it.

That’s the fundamental shift. Traditional frameworks can’t solve for this. You can’t CIRCLES your way through “how would you increase Claude Code WAU 10x?” because the answer requires you to understand how agentic workflows work, what Opus 4.6 is capable of, how slash-loop infrastructure enables proactive behavior, and what Cowork is as a product surface.

#### The round now decides your level, not just your pass/fail.

This is the insight from our [Land PM Job cohort](https://www.landpmjob.com/) data that blew my mind.

Across 80 cohort candidates, the pattern I keep seeing is that AI product sense correlates more with level placement than behavioral. Candidates who came back saying 'I was expecting L5, I got L4' more often than not pointed to the AI round when we debriefed.

Ankit's framing on this was the clearest I've heard. 'Behavioral gets you through the door. AI product sense is the round that truly decides your offer. The level, the money, the negotiation leverage all flow from this round.

### 2b. The 5 Thinking Shifts That Separate 9s from 7s

This is the core of the updated guide. These aren’t the “3 layers” I covered in October (model considerations, safety, AI metrics). Those are baseline now. Everyone knows them.

These are the 5 thinking shifts I’ve identified from coaching 80+ candidates in the last 6 months. In my coaching debriefs, the candidates who scored 8+ consistently hit four or five of these. The 6s and 7s were usually missing two or three.

#### Thinking Shift 1 - Start with strategic context, not users

In traditional product sense, you jump to users. “Who are the users? What are their pain points?”

In AI product sense, the first 2-3 minutes should be about why this question matters to this company right now. Not in a generic “AI is growing” way. In a specific, opinionated, I-have-done-my-homework way.

Watch what Ankit did.

He opened with -

1. Claude Code represents a shift from AI-assisted typing to autonomous coding agents
2. $2.5B annualized revenue. Anthropic’s largest revenue driver.
3. OpenAI’s Codex CLI just launched and is gaining traction (competitive threat)
4. 70-80 features shipped in Q1 2026 (velocity signal)
5. Non-developers are emerging as an unexpected user segment
6. Growth connects directly to Anthropic’s safety research mission (more revenue → more alignment research)

I scored this 10/10. I told him it might be the best strategic context opening I’ve ever seen.

Now here’s what a 6/10 opening sounds like. I hear this every week in coaching -

> Claude Code is a really popular product. AI coding tools are growing fast. Let me jump into the users.

Same product. Same question. One candidate proved they understand the business. The other proved they can read a landing page.

**Your prep should include 2-3 minutes of company-specific strategic context for any company you’re interviewing at.** Revenue numbers, competitive dynamics, recent product launches, and why the growth question connects to the company’s broader strategy. If you can’t explain this with specifics, you’re starting from behind.

#### Thinking Shift 2 - Segment by relationship to the AI capability, not demographics

Traditional product sense segments by demographics, job titles, or use frequency (power users vs. casual). These are fine for Instagram or Uber.

For AI products, the segmentation heuristic that separates strong candidates is **how users relate to the core AI capability.**

Ankit segmented Claude Code users by “relationship with code” -

Segment Who they are Relationship with code Professional Coders Experienced devs in terminal/IDE They review AI output critically, push product limits Aspiring Builders PMs, founders, students who want to build They can’t code, terminal scares them, Cowork isn’t enough Knowledge Automators Finance, legal, ops automating repetitive work They don’t want to build anything new, just automate existing tasks

This segmentation is powerful because it directly determines -

1. Which surface area each group uses (terminal vs. web vs. Cowork)
2. What friction they hit (rate limits vs. intimidation vs. blank slate)
3. What growth lever matters (retention vs. activation vs. awareness)

A demographic segmentation like “junior devs vs. senior devs” tells you nothing about which product surface to invest in. An AI-capability relationship segmentation tells you everything.

The interviewer isn’t testing whether you can segment. Every PM can segment. They’re testing whether your segmentation reveals something specific about how users interact with the AI. **If your segments could apply to a non-AI product, you’ve done it wrong.**

#### Thinking Shift 3 - Specify what’s model-layer versus application-layer in every solution

This is the single most important technical distinction in AI product sense. And it’s the one most candidates miss entirely.

When I asked a Google candidate last month “is your solution a model change or an application-layer change?”, he froze. Interview was effectively over.

Here’s why it matters. In a traditional product company, when you propose a feature, the engineering team figures out implementation. You don’t need to specify which system does what.

In an AI product company, the PM needs to know whether a solution -

1. Requires the research/model team (expensive, 3-6 month timelines, cross-team dependency)
2. Can be shipped by the application/product team (faster, lower risk, you own the roadmap)
3. Needs both (and in what sequence)

Watch how Ankit did it for each of his three solutions -

**Solution 1 - Workflow Memory**

1. *Model layer* - Distills messy user sessions into structured, reusable workflow representations. Distinguishes one-time corrections from persistent preferences.
2. *Application layer* - Stores versioned workflow templates. Surfaces them at the right moment when the user returns.

**Solution 2 - Output Calibration**

1. *Model layer* - Learns implicit formatting and quality preferences from edit patterns over time.
2. *Application layer* - Tracks edits and deltas across sessions. Maintains the quality profile.

**Solution 3 - Proactive Agent**

1. *Application layer* - Extends the existing slash-loop/cron infrastructure to monitor folders for new files matching trigger conditions.
2. *Model layer* - Recognizes whether incoming files match expected patterns. Handles partial or unexpected inputs gracefully.

See the pattern? He didn’t just say “Claude should remember things.” He specified which part of the system does what. That’s the difference between a PM who has worked on AI products and a PM who has read about them.

Scripted line to memorize -

> “On the model layer, we’d need \[X\]. On the application layer, my team would ship \[Y\]. The model work is the longer dependency, so I’d sequence the application layer first.”

#### Thinking Shift 4 - Build safety into the design of each solution, not as a section at the end

I covered this in October. But what I’ve learned since then is that the bar has moved. In October, mentioning safety at all was differentiated. Now, where and how you mention it is what’s scored.

The mistake pattern I see every week -

> And for safety considerations, we would add content filters, have human review for edge cases, and make sure we comply with regulations.

This is a paragraph at the end of the solutions section. It’s generic. It could apply to any AI product. The interviewer marks “checked the safety box, no depth.”

What Ankit did instead. Safety was woven into each individual solution -

1. Workflow Memory - “Workflow memories are user-owned and editable. Claude shows what it remembers before executing. Stephanie can approve the plan and the agent handles execution.”
2. Output Calibration - “This only affects formatting and style, not analytical substance. Low-confidence preferences trigger questions rather than assumptions.”
3. Proactive Agent - “Every proactive action requires explicit user approval, at least initially. Claude never modifies files unprompted. Users control which folders are monitored.”

Three solutions. Three specific safety designs. Each tailored to the actual risk of that specific feature.

That’s what Anthropic means when they say they evaluate safety. They don’t want a generic paragraph. They want evidence that you think about safety the way their engineers think about it, as a design constraint specific to each feature.

#### Thinking Shift 5 - Defend your growth math with specific levers, not hand-waving

Traditional product sense lets you get away with “this will drive significant growth.” AI product sense does not.

When I asked Ankit “how are we going to be sure this gets us to 10x?”, he didn’t panic. He said “Give me 30 seconds.” Then he came back with three specific growth levers -

1. **Activation** - Millions of pro and max subscribers already using Claude Chat have never tried Cowork. Workflow memory changes the value proposition. Without it, Cowork’s value resets to zero every session. With it, the first session teaches Claude and every subsequent session is better.
2. **Retention** - The blank slate problem is a key churn driver. Users try Cowork, find the reteaching cost too high, revert to chat or manual work. Workflow memory turns a high-setup-cost, variable-output experience into a zero-setup, consistent-output experience.
3. **Word of mouth** - Claude Code spiked because of developer word of mouth. For the same thing to happen with Cowork for knowledge workers, you need the “zero-setup” experience. Stephanie tells her finance team “Claude does my quarterly reports in 15 minutes.” That story only happens when the output is reliable and consistent, with persistent workflows.

Each lever is specific. Each connects to the product solution. Each is grounded in how AI products actually grow.

**The candidates who get 9s can explain why their solution creates a flywheel.** The candidates who get 7s say “this will increase engagement.” That’s the difference.

### 2c. Ankit’s Own Words - Traditional vs AI Product Sense

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ij-b!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb5e4c697-f49b-4e20-a20f-487b9ba46ed8_2400x3000.png)

After the mock, I asked Ankit to summarize what he did differently. He named three things that I think every candidate should internalize.

**“Use model capabilities as a constraint, not just features during a brainstorm.”**

In a regular product sense question like “improve Gmail,” you start with users, pain points, solutions. With the Claude Code question, Ankit had to think through what Claude can and cannot do today and how it’s evolving. When I threw the Cowork curveball, it became even more layered. Now he had to think about Claude Code’s shipping velocity (120 features in 90 days), how Opus 4.6 is evolving, and how Cowork is already built for knowledge workers.

Traditional product sense doesn’t require this. AI product sense demands it. The model’s capabilities are not a feature list. They are the constraint space your entire answer lives within.

**Safety was in my solution design, not in the appendix.**

Ankit said it best. “My answer wasn’t just to add a specific filter. Safety has to be a strategic decision while thinking through the product outcome.”

When he proposed Workflow Memory, the safety consideration wasn’t “add a review step.” It was “workflow memories are user-owned and editable. Claude shows what it remembers before executing. Stephanie approves the plan. The agent handles execution.” That’s specific. That’s structural.

**I evaluated every solution against a potential model improvement trajectory.**

The subtlest point. Ankit’s solutions weren’t just application features bolted onto Claude. Each had clear implications for the model team. Workflow memory requires the model to distinguish one-time corrections from persistent preferences. Output calibration requires the model to learn implicit preferences from edit patterns.

These aren’t requests you throw over the wall. They’re informed proposals that show you understand how model improvement actually works.

### 2d. The Mock Walk-Through - Phase by Phase

I want to show you what the actual answer looked like, because the flow matters as much as the content.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!B2et!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F31ed7a91-2be3-441a-8ba8-46bfe7245249_2400x3004.png)

**The Clarifying Questions (2 minutes)**

Before answering, Ankit established three things -

1. I’ll assume I’m a PM at Anthropic on Claude Code. This matters because Anthropic has a safety-first mission, and that shapes how I think about growth.
2. I’ll assume global market, since Claude Code is available internationally.
3. I’ll define WAU as unique users who run at least one session across any surface and send at least one message beyond turning it on.

I pushed back and added a constraint. “If they just type Claude into their terminal, they’re not a WAU. They have to send at least one message.” This kind of metric negotiation happens in real interviews. Ankit handled it cleanly. Most candidates skip it.

**The Strategic Context (3 minutes) - Scored 10/10**

He didn’t just say “Claude Code is growing.” He laid out -

1. The shift from AI-assisted typing (autocomplete) to autonomous coding agents
2. $2.5B annualized revenue, Anthropic’s largest revenue driver
3. Growing 10x strengthens the flywheel (more users → more usage data → more safety research funding → better model → more users)
4. OpenAI’s Codex CLI gaining traction, Reddit frustration about Claude token consumption
5. 70-80 features shipped in Q1 2026
6. Non-developers emerging as an unexpected user segment
7. Connection to Anthropic’s safety research mission

I told him this might be the best strategic context opening I’ve ever seen.

**The Cowork Pivot (the most important teaching moment)**

After the strategic context, I threw the curveball. I told him Claude Cowork is actually built on top of Claude Code. The Code team maintains it. And Cowork isn’t about building software. It’s about completing enterprise-grade workflows.

I even added pressure. “We don’t talk about this publicly. We’re worried internally because the latest model can do everything a junior employee does.”

Ankit didn’t panic. He said “That’s a great point. I had assumed Cowork was an independent product. But based on your feedback, I understand Cowork is built on top of Claude Code as the machinery.”

Then he asked a clarifying question. “Are you suggesting we should also think about growing Cowork in addition to Claude Code?”

I told him Cowork isn’t a separate product team. It’s a surface area his team maintains.

He took a minute. Restructured. Came back with a completely adjusted segmentation incorporating Cowork.

**This is the single most important moment in the mock.** In real interviews, the curveball IS the test. The interviewer isn’t trying to trip you up. They’re testing whether you can absorb new information and adapt. The candidates who get 9s treat the curveball as a gift. The candidates who get 6s treat it as an attack.

**The Segmentation (5 minutes)**

Ankit segmented by “relationship with code” -

**Professional Coders** - Experienced devs in terminal/IDE. Review AI output critically. Bottleneck is rate limits, not discovery. Medium reach, medium underserved.

**Aspiring Builders** - Non-technical people who want to build software. Terminal scares them. Cowork is too document-focused. High reach, extremely high underserved.

**Knowledge Automators** - Finance analysts, legal, ops automating repetitive work. Cowork is designed for them but adoption is early. Hundreds of millions. High reach, medium-high underserved.

He prioritized knowledge automators because Cowork already exists as the right surface, the market is 10-50x larger than developers, many already have pro subscriptions, and the 10x math is most compelling.

I pushed back. “I actually thought you were going to prioritize aspiring builders.” He defended his choice well, but this led to the prioritization framework contradiction I flagged in scoring. His own dimensions (reach + underserved) pointed more strongly to aspiring builders.

**The lesson** - always sanity-check that your scoring actually supports your final choice. If the numbers don’t align, address the contradiction before the interviewer catches it.

**The Persona and Pain Points (5 minutes)**

“Let’s call her Stephanie. Senior financial analyst at a healthcare company.”

Every quarter she manually pulls data from 20-50 PDFs, normalizes into spreadsheets, creates comparison tables, drafts executive summaries. Takes a full week. She knows Claude as a chatbot for emails. Has no idea Cowork exists.

Three pain points with frequency/severity rubric -

Pain Point Frequency Severity Blank slate - Claude doesn’t remember workflows, re-explains every time Extremely High High Multi-doc hallucination - reads 12 of 15 PDFs correctly, silently invents numbers from the other 3 High High Reactive - waits for instructions instead of proactively identifying work Medium Medium

He went deep on each one. For the hallucination pain point, he described exactly what goes wrong. “Cloud might read 80% of the sources correctly. But because it’s misreading a table in the remaining 20%, it’s going to silently leave that out or invent a number. Stephanie might not catch this. But her VP will flag it. And at that point, trust breaks permanently.”

That level of specificity is what makes an answer memorable. “The AI hallucinates” is a 6/10 pain point description. “Claude reads 12 of 15 PDFs correctly but silently invents numbers from the misread tables, and Stephanie’s VP catches it, permanently breaking her trust in the tool” is a 9/10.

He prioritized the blank slate problem because it’s deeply AI-native (persistent memory, workflow learning, personalized calibration) and it’s where Opus as a model truly excels.

**The Solutions (10 minutes)**

**Solution 1 - Workflow Memory (Prioritized for V1)**

After Stephanie completes a task, Claude offers to remember it. “This looks like a recurring workflow. Do you want me to remember how you did this?” Once she agrees, Claude distills the session into persistent memory, capturing steps, formatting preferences, metric extraction logic, even files referenced. Next quarter, Claude executes from memory, only confirming what changed.

1. *Model layer* - Distilling messy sessions into structured reusable workflows. Distinguishing one-time corrections from persistent preferences.
2. *Application layer* - Storing versioned templates. Surfacing them at the right moment.
3. *Safety* - User-owned, editable, reviewable. Claude shows what it remembers before executing. Stephanie approves, agent handles execution.
4. *Impact* - High. *Effort* - High.

**Solution 2 - Output Calibration**

Every time Stephanie edits Claude’s output (reformatting tables, fixing labels, adjusting tone), Claude observes the delta and builds a quality profile. Over multiple sessions, Claude’s first drafts converge on what Stephanie would have edited them into.

1. *Model layer* - Learning implicit preferences from edit patterns. Individual-level personalization through context engineering.
2. *Application layer* - Tracking edits and deltas across sessions.
3. *Safety* - Only affects formatting and style, not analytical substance. Low-confidence preferences trigger questions, not assumptions.
4. *Impact* - Medium-High. *Effort* - Medium.

**Solution 3 - Proactive Agent**

Stephanie sets a trigger. “When a new report appears in my quarterly folder, start the competitive analysis.” Claude monitors the folder via slash-loop infra (already exists for Claude Code). When it detects matching files, sends a Mac OS notification. “12 new quarterly reports detected based on your saved workflow. Want me to kick this off?” Stephanie approves from her phone. Draft is waiting when she opens her laptop.

1. *Application layer* - Extends slash-loop/cron architecture to Cowork folder monitoring.
2. *Model layer* - Recognizes whether new files match expected patterns. Handles partial inputs gracefully.
3. *Safety* - Every proactive action requires explicit approval. Claude never modifies files unprompted. Users control which folders are monitored.
4. *Impact* - Medium. *Effort* - Medium.

He prioritized Workflow Memory because it attacks the highest-friction moment (the reteaching tax), creates an AI-native flywheel (usage → learning → better output → more usage), and builds organic switching cost (once Claude knows your workflows, moving to a competitor means starting from scratch).

He also noted these three solutions form a roadmap that builds iteratively. They’re not contradictory.

**The 10x Math Defense (3 minutes)**

When I asked “how do we know this gets us to 10x?”, he asked for 30 seconds to think. Then came back with three levers -

1. **Activation** - Millions of pro/max subscribers using Claude Chat have never tried Cowork. Without workflow memory, Cowork’s value resets to zero every session. With it, the first session teaches Claude and every session after is better. This alone could be a massive WAU multiplier.
2. **Retention** - The blank slate problem is a key churn driver. Users try Cowork, reteaching cost is too high, they revert to chat or manual work. Workflow memory turns high-setup + variable-output into zero-setup + consistent-output.
3. **Word of mouth** - Claude Code spiked from developer word of mouth. For Cowork to do the same with knowledge workers, you need the zero-setup experience. “Stephanie tells her finance team ‘Claude does my quarterly reports in 15 minutes.’ That story only happens when output is reliable and consistent with persistent workflows.”

Then the elevator pitch. “If Dario walked by, the key insight is this. Claude Code’s WAU bottleneck is a developer-centric base. The knowledge automator segment is 10-50x larger. Many already have pro subscriptions. The product exists, the pricing works. What doesn’t work is retention. They hit a blank slate every time.”

### 2e. Why People Fail - From Ankit and From My Coaching

At the end of the mock, I summarized why people fail at AI product sense. This isn’t the companies’ actual rubric, but it’s what I’ve seen kill candidates -

1. **Not having a custom framework.** Boring them to death with CIRCLES or an old framework is the fastest way to a 5/10. Your framework should seem specific to the question asked.
2. **Not covering the model and application layer.** If every solution is just “make AI better” without specifying which team ships what, the interviewer writes “no technical depth.”
3. **Not being collaborative.** AI product sense cases run 35-45 minutes. If you monologue for 15, you’ve missed the curveballs that would have made your answer stronger.
4. **Not covering safety and ethics proactively.** If the interviewer has to prompt you for safety, you’ve already lost points.
5. **Not ruthlessly prioritizing with math.** “High reach” means nothing. “Hundreds of millions vs. tens of millions” means everything.

And the roadmap I give every candidate -

1. Build your AI foundations. Use the products daily.
2. Study AI product patterns. Understand architectures.
3. Practice the interview. Use the tools above.
4. Sharpen and calibrate with people who are actually calibrated. That’s what the [Land PM Job cohort](https://www.landpmjob.com/) is for.

### 3c. Questions You Should Practice

I covered the [7 question types with full framework implementations](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-product-sense-interview) in the October guide. Here are the specific questions I’m hearing most frequently in April 2026 that you should practice with -

**Growth questions (the most common type right now)**

1. How would you increase Claude Code WAU 10x?
2. Build a strategy for Gemini to pass ChatGPT in active users.
3. How would you grow Claude’s enterprise adoption 5x in 12 months?
4. Your AI product has 1M signups but only 50K WAU. Diagnose and fix.
5. Expand ChatGPT into developing markets where data costs are high.

These test whether you can think about AI-specific growth levers. Traditional growth (SEO, referrals, paid) still applies. But AI products have a unique lever. The model output IS the growth engine. When Claude Code makes a developer 3x more productive, that developer tells their team. The product output is the marketing.

**Product design questions with AI constraints**

1. Design the interaction flow for ChatGPT for pets.
2. Design an AI copilot for product managers.
3. How would you design an AI-powered interface for elderly users?
4. Design an AI code review tool that engineers actually trust.

These test whether you can design for probabilistic outputs. Traditional product design assumes deterministic systems. AI design has to handle wrong answers, feedback loops, user corrections, and trust-building. The best answers show the interaction model between human and AI, not just the human interface.

**Strategy questions that are uniquely AI**

1. When should you use an LLM vs. a traditional ML model for a feature?
2. How do you build a moat for an AI-native product?
3. Your CEO wants to “add AI to everything.” How would you prioritize?
4. How do you balance model improvement investments vs. application-layer investments?

These test whether you understand the AI industry landscape. Build vs buy. Data flywheels. Distribution advantages. Inference cost economics. The candidates who fail use generic strategy frameworks. The candidates who pass use specific AI examples. Cursor vs Copilot. Claude Code vs Codex. Perplexity vs Google Search.

**Safety and ethics questions**

1. Your AI chatbot generated harmful medical advice. Immediate response and long-term fix?
2. How would you prioritize between a capability improvement and a safety improvement?
3. Your AI generates biased outputs against certain demographics. Diagnose and fix.

At Anthropic, safety has its own dedicated round. At every other company, it’s woven throughout. The strong answer describes a system, not a philosophy. Specific incident, specific response, specific prevention mechanism built after.
