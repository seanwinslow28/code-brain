Taika Waititi's Screenplay Craft:
Actionable Technique Patterns
for 2D Animated Comedy Shorts
Executive Summary
This report reverse-engineers Taika Waititi's screenplay techniques
into transferable, mechanical patterns for writing 3–5 minute 2D
animated comedy shorts with emotional depth. It draws on Waititi's
own descriptions of his process, close analysis of his screenplays
(particularly Jojo Rabbit, What We Do in the Shadows, Hunt for the
Wilderpeople, Boy, and his short films Two Cars, One Night and Tama
Tū ), and structural breakdowns of specific scenes. Each section
isolates the construction principle behind the effect.
1. Dialogue Mechanics
The "Scripted Improv" Construction Technique
Waititi's dialogue sounds improvised because he builds in the
rhythmic properties of natural speech — false starts, mid-thought
redirections, and asymmetric exchanges — but every beat serves a
dramatic function. His method has three layers:
Layer 1: Write loose, then strip to bone. Waititi writes one or two
drafts at 110–120 pages, reads the script once, then deletes it from his
computer and rewrites entirely from memory. The result is a 70-page
skeleton — "the bare bones of the story and the most resonant bits...
not funny, no real heart, but it's the structure." He then adds humor
and texture back in. This process means every line in the flnal script
has survived a memory filter: if you couldn't remember it, it wasn't
essential. The dialogue that remains has been unconsciously
pressure-tested for resonance.[1]

Layer 2: Write dialogue by hand first. Waititi writes all dialogue
longhand before typing, which he describes as a method to avoid
getting stuck on blank pages. Handwriting forces a conversational
cadence — you can't type faster than you think, so lines naturally
adopt the rhythm of someone actually speaking. This is why his
dialogue has breath marks, mid-sentence subject changes, and
incomplete thoughts. These aren't afterthoughts; they're baked in
during initial composition.[2]
Layer 3: Build the scene goal, leave the path open. On What We Do
in the Shadows, Waititi and Clement scripted the full film structure
and knew "how every scene would start and end," but the dialogue
was almost entirely improvised. They would tell actors: "Here's the
goal — from the beginning of the scene to the end, you have to get this
achieved. Here's your mission: convince this person to do this". This
technique creates dialogue that wanders naturally toward its target
rather than driving straight at it — producing that mid-thought,
slightly lost quality.[3][4]
Deadpan Setup: What's On the Page
Waititi's deadpan is constructed through a specific page-level
technique: the script creates the absurd situation; the script
directs the character to play it straight; the comedy lives in the
gap. He rarely uses parentheticals to indicate "deadpan" — instead,
the action lines describe matter-of-fact behavior in an absurd context.
In the Jojo Rabbit screenplay, when Jojo and imaginary Hitler discuss
strategy, the action lines are written in a flat, operational tone — "Jojo
and Hitler again strategize in his room" — while the dialogue itself
treats an insane premise (a child consulting imaginary Hitler about a
Jewish girl in his attic) as a mundane tactical conversation. The
deadpan isn't in a parenthetical "(deadpan)" — it's structural. The
script treats the absurd as normal, and that flatness IS the comedy.[5]
His direction to actors reinforces this: "I'll give them a line reading. I'll
say, 'Just say it exactly like this.' Sometimes I'll just say, 'Count to three
and then say that'". The script builds deadpan by writing dialogue
that a character would say in complete earnest — the humor emerges
because the audience recognizes the absurdity the character doesn't.
[1]

Interruption and Trail-Off Patterns
Waititi's dialogue interruptions follow a structural pattern:
characters interrupt when they've stopped listening, not when
they disagree. This is distinct from Aaron Sorkin-style interruptions
(which signal intellectual engagement) or Tarantino-style overlaps
(which signal power dynamics). In Waititi's scripts, characters
interrupt because they're inside their own heads.
From the Jojo Rabbit script, when Jojo tries to explain himself to Elsa,
she doesn't wait for him to finish because she's already categorized
him — she trails off into her own thought, picks up a different thread,
or physically does something that signals she's moved past what he's
saying. The pattern: Character A makes a statement → Character B
responds to an earlier part of that statement or to something
entirely internal → Character A adjusts course mid-sentence. This
creates the sensation of two people each having slightly different
conversations simultaneously.[6]

The structural principle: in any given scene, Waititi picks ONE
primary humor channel and commits to it. He doesn't stack
mechanisms within a single joke — the dish-meeting scene in

Character Voice Differentiation
In ensemble casts, Waititi differentiates characters through their
relationship to self-awareness, not through verbal tics or
catchphrases:
Viago (Shadows): Knows he's embarrassing, tries to manage it —
humor from the gap between his self-awareness and his
incompetence[4]
Vladislav (Shadows): Believes he's still impressive, despite
centuries of evidence otherwise — humor from delusion that the
character half-recognizes[4]
Deacon (Shadows): Genuinely doesn't understand why things
aren't working — humor from pure obliviousness[7]
Yorki (Jojo Rabbit): Sees the world clearly but has zero alarm
about it — humor from calm observation of horror[6]
Klenzendorf (Jojo Rabbit): Knows exactly what's happening and
has given up caring — humor from exhausted capitulation[6]
The technique: assign each character a position on the awareness
spectrum, then write every line from that position. A character
who is oblivious will produce different humor from the same
situation than a character who is self-aware but impotent.
2. Scene Construction
The Comedy-Drama Seam: Simultaneous, Not
Alternating
Waititi's signature move is scenes that are funny AND emotionally
resonant in the same moment. The mechanical structure has three
components:
Component 1: Ground the comedy in a real emotional need. In Jojo
Rabbit, Jojo writes fake letters from Nathan to Elsa. The scene is
funny — a ten-year-old boy writing a breakup letter in a man's voice,
then immediately writing a reconciliation letter. But the underlying

action is a child trying to manipulate a girl he's developing feelings
for, and Elsa knows exactly what he's doing. She cries at the first
letter and is "a little amused" by the second. The comedy and the
emotion aren't alternating — they're the same gesture viewed from
two angles.[6]
Component 2: Let the character's emotional truth BE the joke.
Waititi told The Creative Independent: "I like when something tragic is
happening and then it's undercut with a laugh, or something's funny
and then the carpet's pulled out and it just nose dives into a tragedy. I
like a bit of see-saw going on". But the most effective version isn't the
see-saw — it's when both exist simultaneously. The Bella funeral
scene in Wilderpeople works because the priest's incompetence isn't a
comedic digression from grief — it IS the grief. The scene shows what
happens when death arrives and the ritual meant to honor it is
inadequate. The laughter and the sadness have the same source.[8]
[1]
Component 3: Never let the audience decide which register
they're in. Waititi specifically shot a "normal sad funeral" for the
Wilderpeople scene and rejected it because "it had no real impact".
The principle: if the audience can categorize a scene as "the sad
part," they armor up. If they're laughing when the devastation
arrives, the armor is down.[1]
Scene Openings
Waititi's scenes typically open with a character already mid-action or
mid-conversation. The Jojo Rabbit script rarely has establishing beats
— characters enter scenes already engaged. The Elsa confrontation
scenes begin with Jojo already in the room, not entering it. Hunt for
the Wilderpeople uses chapter title cards as scene openers, which
function as cold-open resets — each title card gives the audience
permission to laugh at the absurdity of the title before the emotional
content of the chapter arrives.[10][5]

Scripting Awkward Silence
Waititi scripts pauses through action lines rather than the word
"beat." In the Jojo Rabbit script, the silence after Elsa reads Jojo's fake
letter isn't written as "(beat)" — it's written as physical description:
Elsa's reaction, then Jojo's observation of that reaction. The
technique: replace "beat" with a micro-action — a character
looking away, adjusting something, breathing. This gives the pause
a visual life that translates directly to animation, where a "beat" is
meaningless but a character slowly lowering their eyes is a drawable
moment.[6]
Physical Comedy in Action Lines
Waititi writes physical comedy with the precision of choreography
notation. The grenade scene in Jojo Rabbit is scripted as: "He hurls the
grenade like a war hero. It flies through the air, bounces off a tree,
hits another one, deflects off a nearby branch and ricochets
backwards, landing only 6-feet away from Jojo". Note the
construction: each impact is a separate clause, building rhythm. The
humor is in the accumulation — the grenade's journey gets worse
with each conjunction. The structural pattern for writing visual gags
in action lines: each beat of the physical joke gets its own clause,
creating a rhythm the reader can "see."[5]
Scene Length and Rhythm
Waititi's scenes are short by drama standards, long by comedy
standards. A video essay analysis of his work notes that "the whole
movie has an eccentric rhythm because this director is confident
enough to let scenes sit and breathe before accelerating his narrative
with peppy deadpan montages". In Hunt for the Wilderpeople, the
Leonard Cohen tracking-shot montage compresses what could be ten
scenes into a single continuous movement — time and space merge.
The pattern: short scenes when things are happening TO
characters; longer, breathing scenes when characters are
happening to each other. Action and plot compress; relationship and
observation expand.[11]

3. Structural Patterns
Emotional Arcs Within Comedy Frameworks
Waititi builds devastating emotional moments using a specific
structural technique: he earns the audience's trust through
comedy, then uses that trust as a delivery system for emotion. A
video essay explains the principle: "If a character makes us laugh
then usually we like the character, which means we care about what
happens to them, which means the dramatic story elements then
have more weight".[12]
In Boy, the structure is: Boy's fantasy version of his father (funny) →
gradual revelation that the father is a pathetic criminal (still funny,
but with growing unease) → the birthday scene where the fantasy
fully collapses (devastating). The audience has been laughing at Boy's
inventions about his dad — "master carver, deep sea treasure diver,
captain of the rugby team" — and each laugh is an investment in
Boy's emotional world. When that world collapses, the audience isn't
just watching a character be sad; they're losing something they
participated in building.[9]
In Jojo Rabbit, the same principle operates at a larger scale. The entire
first half trains the audience to laugh at Nazi imagery by making it
absurd. Every Hitler joke, every ridiculous Hitler Youth exercise,
every bumbling officer is teaching the audience that this world is safe
to laugh at. That training is the setup. Rosie's hanging is the
punchline.[12][6]
The "Turn" — Executing the Shift Mid-Laugh
The structural signature of Waititi's emotional turns is: the
devastating moment arrives during or immediately after a comic
scene, with zero tonal preparation. The principle is that the
comedy IS the preparation — it lowers the audience's emotional
defenses.
In Jojo Rabbit: Jojo has just had a comic encounter with Yorki (pages
74–76), who is wearing a paper uniform and claims it's "impervious
to damage." This is pure comedy. Then, within a few pages (pages 89–
90), Jojo follows a butterfly to the gallows and discovers his mother's

body. There is no transitional scene. No ominous music cue written
into the script. No darkening tone. The butterfly — a symbol of
lightness — literally leads him to death.[6]
In Hunt for the Wilderpeople: Bella's death occurs abruptly, off-screen,
between scenes. The very next scene is the absurd funeral where
Waititi himself plays an incompetent priest. The comedy doesn't
follow the grief as "comic relief" — it arrives simultaneously with the
grief, producing a scene where laughter and tears occupy the same
physical space.[8]
The setup-to-turn ratio in Waititi's features is approximately 75/25:
roughly three-quarters of the runtime is spent building the comic
world and the audience's investment in it, with the major emotional
turn arriving in the flnal quarter. In Boy, the reveal of Alamein's true
character arrives in the third act. In Jojo Rabbit, Rosie's death arrives
at approximately the 75% mark.[13][6]
Pacing Signatures
Waititi accelerates during external action and decelerates during
interpersonal exchange. His pacing triggers follow a pattern:
Accelerate when: society intrudes on personal relationships
(the Gestapo visit in Jojo Rabbit, the manhunt escalation in
Wilderpeople), physical comedy unfolds (the grenade sequence),
or montage compresses time[11]
Decelerate when: two characters are negotiating emotional
territory (Jojo/Elsa conversations, Ricky/Hec in the bush), a
character confronts reality (Boy seeing his father clearly for the
first time), or silence says more than dialogue[14]
Hunt for the Wilderpeople uses chapter titles as pacing gear-shifts —
each new chapter resets the audience's rhythmic expectations.[10]
Ensemble Management
Waititi manages ensembles by giving each character a narrative
function that doubles as a comedy function. In Jojo Rabbit, Yorki is
the comic relief character AND the reality check character — he's the
one who tells Jojo the Jews "seemed normal" and later delivers the

news that Hitler killed himself. Klenzendorf is the bureaucratic
obstacle AND the secret protector — his scenes serve plot mechanics
while simultaneously being funny. The structural approach: every
ensemble character must justify their existence on both the
comedy axis and the plot axis simultaneously. Characters who are
only funny get cut; characters who only serve the plot don't get
written.[6]
4. Animation / Visual Storytelling Relevance
Techniques That Transfer Directly to 2D Animation
Several of Waititi's live-action techniques are not just transferable to
2D animation — they're arguably more powerful in that medium:
Reaction shots as comedy infrastructure. Waititi's comedy
frequently depends on a character's non-reaction to an absurd event.
In What We Do in the Shadows, the documentary format means the
camera catches characters' faces during other characters' absurd
monologues. In 2D animation, you control the reaction shot
absolutely — you can hold a character's expression for exactly as
many frames as the joke needs. The animation equivalent: design
"reaction holds" — specific expressions that get held for 8–12
frames longer than feels natural, creating comedic tension
through stillness.[7]
Accumulating physical comedy. The grenade scene's clause-by-
clause construction ("bounces off a tree, hits another one, deflects off
a nearby branch...") translates directly to animation timing where
each impact can be given distinct staging. In animation, you can push
this further — objects can defy physics in ways that amplify the
accumulation.[5]
The deadpan character in an absurd world. Waititi's "straight face
in absurd situation" is the foundational principle of classic animation
comedy (Buster Keaton's heirs include every character from Daria to
early Squidward). For expression sheets: design a "neutral resting
face" that is the character's most-used expression. The comedy
comes from how rarely they deviate from it, and how small those
deviations are.

Visual Tone / Emotional Content Contrast
Jojo Rabbit uses deliberately bright, saturated colors during its
depiction of Nazi Germany. The visual design choice is scripted: the
world is seen through Jojo's eyes, and to a ten-year-old believer, this
world is exciting and beautiful. The color palette is the subjective lens
of a child's perception.[15][12]
For animated shorts, this translates to: design the visual world to
match the protagonist's emotional state, not the scene's objective
reality. If your character is oblivious to danger, the color palette
should be warm and inviting even as the situation deteriorates. The
audience will feel the dissonance — the gap between what they see
and what they know — and that dissonance produces both comedy
and dread simultaneously.
Waititi's approach to framing Rosie's shoes — repeated low-angle
shots of her feet throughout the film, so that when Jojo discovers her
hanging body, the audience recognizes her shoes before
understanding the scene — is a technique built for animation. Visual
motifs can be planted more precisely in animation because every
frame is intentional. Design a recurring visual element (a hat, a
gesture, a color) that accumulates meaning through repetition, then
weaponize it in the emotional climax.[16][15]
Deadpan Expression Sheets
For translating Waititi's deadpan to character design: the key is
asymmetry of expression investment. In his films, the funniest
characters move the least facial muscles. Design your deadpan
character with:
A default expression that reads as "mildly processing" — not
blank, not engaged, somewhere between
Maximum three subtle expression variations (slight eyebrow
raise, slight mouth downturn, slight eye-widening) that signal
internal reaction without breaking the deadpan surface
Reserve full expression changes for the emotional turn — when
the deadpan character finally reacts fully, the audience feels it as
a seismic event precisely because the character has been
expressively restrained for the entire runtime

5. Transferable Techniques for Short-Form
Two Cars, One Night (11 minutes): Compression
Mechanics
Waititi's Oscar-nominated short demonstrates how his feature
techniques compress. The film places three children in a pub carpark
at night and lets a relationship develop through cross-car dialogue.
What survives from his feature technique:[17][18]
A single contained location — the carpark functions like a
stage. Compression requires spatial constraint.
Dialogue-driven character revelation — the children reveal
their worlds through how they talk, not through plot events.
Romeo insults Polly, then pivots to curiosity. The pattern:
aggression → interest → vulnerability unfolds in under twelve
minutes.[19]
Comedy from childhood mimicry of adult behavior — the
children flirt the way they've seen adults flirt, badly. This is the
same innocence-as-lens technique Waititi uses in features.[12]
What doesn't survive: extended montage, subplot, ensemble
management, and the slow accumulation of motifs (the shoes in Jojo
Rabbit require 60+ minutes to build meaning).
Tama Tū : Comedy Under Pressure
Tama Tū places Mā ori Battalion soldiers in a ruined building, hiding
from enemy forces. They joke, tease, and laugh — until a crow flies in,
symbolizing death, and they go silent. The short's technique: comedy
as coping mechanism, interrupted by reality. The humor doesn't
alternate with the danger; it coexists with it. The soldiers' jokes ARE
the tension, because the audience knows that any sound could kill
them.[20]
For 3–5 minute animated shorts: this is the most directly transferable
structure. Place characters in a situation where humor is their
only tool for managing fear or discomfort, then let reality
intrude. The comedy and the emotional weight share the same
engine.

Comedy-Drama Balance in 3–5 Minutes
In features, Waititi can spend 75% of runtime in comedy mode before
executing the emotional turn. In shorts, the ratio must invert: you
need to establish the emotional stakes within the first 30 seconds,
then let comedy operate in service of those stakes throughout.
The reason: audiences need a reason to care before they'll accept the
tonal shift, and in 3 minutes, you can't earn that caring through
comedy alone — you must signal it immediately.
The effective short-form ratio is approximately: 15% emotional
grounding → 60% comedy that reveals character → 25%
emotional payoff that recontextualizes the comedy. Compare this
to Waititi's feature ratio of 75% comedy / 25% emotional payoff.[6]
Mockumentary/Confessional in Short Animated Format
Waititi's mockumentary technique — the talking-head confessional
intercut with "documentary footage" — compresses well into
animated shorts for a specific structural reason: the confessional
creates instant interiority. A character looking at camera and
speaking directly tells you who they think they are; the
"documentary" footage shows who they actually are. The gap is the
comedy.[21][7]
In the What We Do in the Shadows TV series, each episode does
scripted takes first, then "funsies" (improvised takes), giving the show
its naturalistic texture. For animated shorts using this structure, you
don't need true improv — you need the appearance of mid-thought
speech patterns: sentence fragments, self-corrections, and glances at
camera that feel spontaneous. Write these by hand first, as Waititi
does, to capture conversational rhythm.[22][2]
The confessional format works particularly well for shorts because it
eliminates exposition scenes — a character can tell you their
situation directly, freeing screen time for comic situation and
emotional payoff.

Scripted "Improv" Density in 3 Minutes
In features, Waititi's naturalistic dialogue includes many lines that
serve character texture but not plot function — the wandering, the
false starts, the non-sequiturs. In a 3-minute short, you can afford
approximately two to three "texture" lines — lines that exist purely
to make dialogue feel real. More than that, and the short feels
aimless. The technique: write the scene with full naturalistic
dialogue (5+ minutes), then cut to the bone, preserving only
texture lines that also serve character revelation. This mirrors
Waititi's own delete-and-rewrite process, applied at scene scale.[1]
6. Process
Writing Method
Waititi's writing process has a distinctive sequence:[23][1]
1. Conceive the opening and closing images first — "This will be
a cool way to open a film... this will be a cool way to end it"[23]
2. Write scattered scenes and sequences — "I'll put in some
scenes throughout. I think this would be a great scene." He does
not write linearly[23]
3. Glue sequences together — "When I've got enough of these
scenes or sequences, then lazily just glue them all together with
some sort of connecting scenes"[23]
4. Write 1–2 full drafts (110–120 pages)
5. Read once, then delete — Puts the file on an external drive and
rewrites from memory, producing a ~70-page structural
skeleton[1]
6. Add humor, heart, and texture back in — Writes "the longest
version of the scene knowing that I can cut it down"[1]
7. Write all dialogue by hand before typing[2]
What's On the Page vs. Discovered on Set
The ratio varies dramatically by project:
Boy: Primarily scripted, with Waititi performing as Alamein
based on his own father[4]

What We Do in the Shadows (film): Structure fully scripted,
dialogue almost entirely improvised. 125 hours of footage cut to
86 minutes[24][4]
Jojo Rabbit: Fully scripted screenplay (adapted from a novel),
with the naturalistic quality built into the writing itself[5]
Thor: Ragnarok: Waititi claimed 80% of dialogue was
improvised[25]
The on-set technique across all projects: Waititi gives actors a clear
scene goal, allows exploration within that goal, and edits to find the
take with the best timing. He frequently gives line readings — "Say it
exactly like this" — and uses physical constraints to alter
performance: "This entire scene, you're not allowed to move your
hands".[3][1]
Comedy Training Roots
Waititi was a member of the five-man comedy troupe So You're a Man
at Victoria University of Wellington in the mid-1990s, alongside
Jemaine Clement and Bret McKenzie. He and Clement formed the
comedy duo The Humourbeasts, which won New Zealand's highest
comedy accolade, the Billy T Award, in 1999. Their act included
vampire characters as early as 2000, which eventually became What
We Do in the Shadows.[26][27]
This background manifests in his screenplay structure in three ways:
(1) Scene-as-bit construction — many Waititi scenes have the
architecture of a comedy sketch (premise, escalation, button), (2)
Comfort with audience discomfort — improv and stand-up train
you to hold a silence, to let awkwardness do the work, (3) Character-
first comedy — improv trains you to build humor from character
behavior rather than from joke construction. Waititi has said he
doesn't know any comedy "rules" — "I just know what I think is
funny. It usually comes from the taste I've developed over the years".
[1]

Cultural Specificity as Universality
Waititi achieves universality through specificity, not despite it. His
approach: tell "normal stories about general human conditions, but
tell them in a very unique place from indigenous authentic people
who know the specific culture". In Boy, the Mā ori community setting,
the Michael Jackson obsession, the specific slang — none of this is
explained or translated. Yet the film became New Zealand's highest-
grossing film because the emotional core (a child's fantasy about an
absent parent collapsing upon contact with reality) is universally
recognizable.[28][13][2]
The technique is not "add cultural detail for flavor" — it's "make the
cultural specificity structurally necessary." In Boy, Boy's fantasy
about his father isn't just any fantasy — it's inflicted by Mā ori
masculine ideals and by the specific pop culture of 1984 New Zealand.
In Hunt for the Wilderpeople, Ricky Baker's dialogue and worldview
are shaped by growing up Mā ori in the foster system. The specificity
doesn't decorate the story; it generates the story.[29][9]
Waititi told CBC Radio: "I like it when our experience is presented in a
way that feels normal... putting Indigenous actors in spaces that other
people are in, and not boxing them into telling stories specific to their
culture or their trauma". The principle for any writer: don't exoticize
your characters' cultural context — make it the water they swim
in, unremarked upon, and the audience will recognize the human
behavior even if they don't recognize the cultural container.[29]
Application Framework: From Waititi
Technique to Animated Short
For a 3–5 minute animated comedy short with emotional depth, distill
Waititi's techniques into this construction sequence:
1. Start with the emotional endpoint. What truth does the
character confront? Build backward from that moment.
2. Design the comedy as a delivery system for that truth. Every
joke should be a brick in the wall that the emotional moment
will break.

3. Ground comedy in a single contained situation (carpark,
hiding place, waiting room) — spatial constraint forces
character-driven humor.
4. Assign each character a position on the awareness spectrum
— who knows what's really happening, who's oblivious, who's
pretending?
5. Write dialogue by hand. Write the long version. Cut to the
bone. Preserve only texture lines that also reveal character.
6. Script pauses as micro-actions, not beats. A character looking
at their hands is a drawable moment; "beat" is not.
7. Execute the emotional turn mid-laugh — never signal it, never
prepare for it, never let the audience categorize the scene before
it shifts.
8. Design the visual world to match the character's subjective
experience, not objective reality. Let the audience feel the gap.
