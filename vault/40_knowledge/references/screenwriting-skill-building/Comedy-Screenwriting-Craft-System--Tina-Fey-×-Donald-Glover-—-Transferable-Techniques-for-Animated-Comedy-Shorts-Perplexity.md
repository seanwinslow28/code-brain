Comedy Screenwriting Craft
System: Tina Fey × Donald
Glover
Transferable Technique Patterns for 2D
Animated Comedy Shorts with Emotional
Depth
This analysis treats Fey and Glover as a complementary pair — a
machine-gun satirist and a surrealist poet — whose techniques,
mapped structurally, can be combined into a unified craft system for
short-form animated comedy. Every technique below is reverse-
engineered from specific work, not summarized from biography.
1. Dialogue Mechanics
Fey: Machine-Gun Construction
Fey's dialogue operates on a principle of joke layering — multiple
comedic payloads compressed into single lines. The most cited
example is the 30 Rock moment where Jenna responds to Liz's Sling
Blade impression with "No, you don't, Oprah." Dan Harmon has called
this the best joke in 30 Rock, and analysis reveals at least five
simultaneous layers: (1) Jenna misidentifies the impression, (2) her
"impression" is just her normal voice, (3) she violates the cardinal
improv rule of "Yes, And" by saying "no," (4) the casting inversion is
absurd, and (5) it deepens Jenna's character as confidently
incompetent. This density isn't accidental — it's engineered through
collaborative ping-ponging. Robert Carlock describes their writing
process at 30 Rock as "pitching that joke was a real back-and-forth
with Tina, like every other word," where they dare each other to push
a joke further with each pass.[1][2][3]

The measurable result: 30 Rock achieved 7.44 jokes per minute, the
highest rate among measured sitcoms — more than one joke every
eight seconds, surpassing New Girl (7.11), Parks and Recreation
(6.97), and The Office (6.65). This density is structurally supported by
character-voice distinction. Each character occupies a different
reference pool:[4][5]

When these characters collide in a scene, comedy emerges from
register collision — the structural intersection of incompatible
vocabularies occupying the same conversational space. Jack delivers
a corporate strategy monologue while Liz fixates on food; the comedy
isn't in either register alone but in their rhythmic interplay.

The throwaway technique: 30 Rock scripts background gags, visual
jokes, and one-off lines that don't get narrative emphasis. Frank
Rossitano's trucker hats bear a different cryptic slogan every episode
("Shark Cop," "Half Centaur," "Space Gravy"). These aren't improvised
— they're written into wardrobe notes, designed to reward
rewatching. Similarly, fake movie titles (Tracy's fllmography: A
Blaffair to Rememblack, Who Dat Ninja?, Honky Grandma Be Trippin')
appear in backgrounds and throwaway dialogue, each functioning as
a compressed satire of Hollywood racial politics.[7]
Callback architecture: 30 Rock plants joke seeds that pay off across
episodes and seasons. Jack mentions that Kenneth and Clay Aiken are
cousins — a detail never revisited by Kenneth, creating humor from
the asymmetry of information. Kenneth's implied immortality begins
as background oddities and escalates across seven seasons into a full
mythology. The technique: introduce a detail casually (low emphasis),
let it recur at irregular intervals (building recognition), then escalate
it to absurdity (payoff through accumulation).[8][7]
Glover: Naturalistic Layering
Glover's dialogue operates on a fundamentally different principle:
conversations that sound unscripted but carry structural weight
through subtext and tonal shifts. Atlanta's dialogue is written, not
improvised — the show is shot on film, meticulously scripted. But it's
designed to sound meandering, casual, as if overheard rather than
performed.[9]
The key technique is register-shifting within a single conversation.
In a single scene, Glover's characters can move from mundane small
talk to philosophy to social commentary to surreal non-sequitur
without any signal to the audience that the register has changed. In
the "B.A.N." episode (S1E7), Paper Boi appears on a fake talk show
where the host and an academic overanalyze his rap lyrics as
transphobic. Paper Boi responds to their elaborate academic framing
with "Nope" and "Please, please. Tell me about myself" — lines that
function simultaneously as comedy (the deflation of pretension),
social commentary (who gets to define Black expression), and
character work (Paper Boi's authentic frustration with being spoken
for).[10][11]

Silence as dialogue tool: Where Fey fllls every second with a joke,
Glover uses silence as a comedic and dramatic instrument. Director
Hiro Murai has described their approach as deliberately leaving
"empty frames" — a technique their editor initially warned against
because it "completely kills the rhythm," which turned out to be
exactly the effect they wanted. When characters in Atlanta don't
respond, or respond incorrectly, or change the subject, the silence
becomes the text. The audience fllls the gap with discomfort,
recognition, or dread. This is scripted as deliberate beat directions and
scene-ending holds rather than dialogue.[14]
Murai has articulated the philosophy behind this restraint: "It's uh,
it's like someone who talks way too much. You're going to be more
interested in the story of someone who is deliberate with their words
and takes their time than someone who's trying to talk you to death".
[14]

Where They Overlap
Both Fey and Glover use incongruity as the engine of dialogue
comedy — they just calibrate it differently. Fey creates incongruity
through simultaneous register collision (two vocabularies in the same
scene, happening at once). Glover creates it through sequential
register shifting (one conversation drifting through multiple registers
over time). Both techniques produce the same audience effect: the gap
between what's expected and what's delivered generates the laugh or
the emotional resonance.
For a 3-5 minute animated short, the combinable technique is: open
with Glover-style naturalistic conversation (grounding), then
accelerate into Fey-style density as the scene escalates. The opening
establishes character reality; the acceleration rewards attention and
creates climactic energy. Silence can function as a punctuation mark
between Fey-density bursts — a structural rest that makes the next
burst hit harder.
2. Scene Construction
Fey: The Joke-Dense Sitcom Scene
A typical 30 Rock scene follows a compression architecture:
establish the situation in 2-3 lines, introduce the comic engine
(usually a collision between two characters' agendas), escalate
through rapid joke exchanges, and button with a punchline that
either caps the scene or pivots to the next story thread. The entire
sequence typically runs 60-90 seconds in a show that averages 7+
scenes per 21-minute episode.[15][16]
The cold open as craft form: 30 Rock's cold opens are self-contained
mini-sketches that typically run 30-90 seconds before the title card.
They function as tone-setters and often feature standalone comedic
situations that may or may not connect to the episode's A-story. The
structure is almost always: normal situation → absurd revelation →
button. For example, a cold open might show Liz arriving at work
normally, then reveal an escalating absurdity that's been happening
off-screen, buttoned by a character's deadpan reaction.[17][18]

A/B/C story weaving: 30 Rock runs three concurrent storylines per
episode, each occupying roughly: A-story (45-50% of screen time), B-
story (30-35%), C-story (15-20%). The weave pattern is not random —
storylines intersect at specific structural beats, usually around the
midpoint and the climax. Storylines are thematically linked even
when they're narratively separate: if the A-story is about Liz's need
for control, the B-story might feature Jack losing control, and the C-
story might show Tracy exercising chaotic freedom.[19][20]
Ensemble scene management: When 4+ characters share a scene,
Fey keeps everyone active through distributed joke assignment —
each character gets at least one line that serves their voice. No
character is reduced to a reactor. The technique is: assign each
character a distinct want within the scene (even if minor), then let
those wants collide. Tracy wants attention, Jenna wants validation,
Jack wants resolution, Liz wants everyone to stop — these competing
agendas generate comic friction without any character being passive.
[20][6]
Glover: The Drift Mechanic
Atlanta's scene construction operates on what can be called a drift
mechanic — scenes begin in a grounded, recognizable reality and
gradually drift toward the surreal, the horrific, or the philosophically
disorienting without any clear transition point.
"Teddy Perkins" (S2E6) is the masterclass. Darius answers a
mysterious message board posting about a piano for sale. He arrives
at a mansion. The scene begins as a straightforward transaction.
Then details accumulate: Teddy Perkins's unsettling appearance
(Glover in whiteface prosthetics), the house's oppressive atmosphere,
references to abusive father figures. The episode was shot with no
commercial breaks, runs 34 minutes (longer than standard), and
Glover stayed in character as Teddy for the entire production — crew
referred to him as "Teddy" on set, and co-star LaKeith Stanfield didn't
know it was Glover. Director Murai and cinematographer Christian
Sprenger drew on Kubrick's The Shining for the visual language, and
Murai described looking at Glover in makeup as "like looking into a
doll's face — uncanny and unsettling".[21][22]

The structural mechanic of the drift: each scene beat slightly raises
the temperature — not through joke escalation (as Fey would) but
through accumulating wrongness. Normal details become slightly
off, then more off, then clearly wrong, then terrifying. The audience
can never identify the exact moment the comedy became horror
because there was no single pivot — just continuous drift.
Tonal ambiguity is constructed in the screenplay through dual-
register staging: situations are simultaneously funny and
disturbing, and the script never resolves the ambiguity. In "Helen"
(S2E4), Van brings Earn to a German Oktoberfest in rural Georgia.
The Get Out parallels are deliberate — a Black couple driving into
White County, nearly hitting a boar on the road. But the episode plays
simultaneously as relationship drama (Van and Earn's
incompatibility), cultural comedy (Earn bewildered by German
festival games), and racial tension (coded hostility from white
townspeople). Writer Taofik Kolade, in his TV writing debut,
structured this ambiguity by never letting any single register
dominate — the audience never settles into "this is a comedy scene"
or "this is a horror scene".[23][24][25]
Bottle episode technique: "Helen" also demonstrates Glover's
approach to confined settings. By trapping characters in a single
location with clear social rules (the festival), the script amplifies
tension through claustrophobia. Earn cannot leave, cannot code-
switch effectively, cannot find comfortable footing — and the confined
setting makes every micro-interaction a potential escalation. The
barbershop scene Stephen Glover cited as a favorite operates
similarly: Alfred's frustration with barber Bibby's distractions plays
out in a single chair, with no escape.[26]

For a 3-5 minute short, the structural choice between these
approaches depends on the emotional target. If the short aims for
cathartic energy (audience leaves laughing), use Fey's ensemble
architecture. If it aims for lingering resonance (audience leaves
thinking), use Glover's. The hybrid: open with Fey-style comic friction

3. Structural Patterns
Fey: Reliable Architecture
30 Rock's episode structure is a well-oiled machine: cold open → A/B/C
story introduction (Act 1) → complication and intersection (Act 2) →
resolution and button (Act 3), all within 21 minutes. The audience
always knows what kind of show they're watching. This reliability is
a feature, not a limitation — it creates a contract with the viewer that
allows Fey to pack extreme density and absurdity into every scene
because the audience trusts the structural container.[27][15]

Season-level structure: 30 Rock uses a reset model — most episodes
return characters to baseline, with slow-burn seasonal arcs (Jack's
career trajectory, Liz's romantic life) providing forward momentum.
Individual episodes can be watched out of order without confusion,
but sequential viewing rewards callback recognition.[27]
Mean Girls feature structure: Fey's film writing adheres closely to
classical three-act structure. The first act runs almost exactly 30
minutes, establishing characters and world. The inciting incident
(Cady deciding to infiltrate the Plastics) occurs at 29:30. The midpoint
(Cady becoming what she set out to destroy) hits at the one-hour
mark. The third act resolves through a public reckoning (the
assembly scene) that changes not just the protagonist but the entire
school ecosystem. Fey's feature technique: side characters are more
active than the protagonist — the Plastics and Janis drive the plot
while Cady reacts, which is structurally unusual and creates the
impression of a world that operates independently of the main
character.[28][29][30]
Setup and payoff mastery: Mean Girls is a case study in planting
details early that pay off later — Fey's comedy background trained
her to think in terms of setups and callbacks, and she applies this at
feature length with remarkable discipline, where nearly 85% of
dialogue transferred unchanged from screenplay to screen.[31]
Glover: Unpredictable Architecture
Atlanta's structure is deliberately unstable. Each episode can be a
different genre — comedy, horror, mockumentary, sketch anthology,
social thriller — and the audience has no structural contract. Glover
and Murai have described the show's world as "malleable," where
"each episode expands the narrative sandbox a little more" and this
evolution "wasn't planned from the outset; it just naturally unfolded".
[32][33]
Anthology coherence: Seasons 3 and 4 of Atlanta include standalone
episodes with entirely new casts and no main characters. These
maintain series coherence through thematic threading rather than
narrative connection. Season 3's anthology episodes all explore
variations on a "curse of whiteness" theme — racial reckoning,
cultural appropriation, white savior complexes — while the main cast

episodes explore the disorientation of Black Americans in Europe.
The standalone episodes function as dream logic: disconnected in plot
but connected in subconscious preoccupation.[34][35]
"B.A.N." (S1E7) structural experiment: The entire episode
abandons the show's format to present a fake talk show with fake
commercials. Paper Boi appears as a frustrated guest; the fake
commercials (including an animated cereal commercial where a cop
brutalizes a Black version of the Trix rabbit) carry more social
commentary than the talk show segments. The structure mirrors
actual TV viewing — commercial breaks interrupt narrative flow —
and the commentary is embedded in the structure itself, not just the
content. Glover doesn't need characters to state a thesis because the
form is the argument.[36][10]
Signaling rule changes: Atlanta signals tonal and structural shifts
through visual and pacing cues rather than dialogue exposition.
Murai's approach begins by identifying the "emotional moves" of a
scene rather than plotting shots, building outward from an instinctive
"guttural reaction". When an episode opens differently than expected
(no opening credits in "Helen," no main characters in "Three Slaps"),
the audience registers the structural break as a signal that the rules
have changed.[14]

Social commentary through structure: Where Fey displaces
commentary into absurd metaphor, Glover embeds it into the
structural choices themselves. The B.A.N. episode doesn't have a
character explain that Black media is commercialized — instead, fake
commercials structurally interrupt the fake talk show, and the form
itself demonstrates the argument. The "Teddy Perkins" episode
doesn't lecture about how racism destroys families — it puts the
audience in a horror movie where a man's face has literally been
bleached into something inhuman. Glover's structural choice that
enables going darker: he doesn't resolve the tension. Where Fey's
scenes button with a punchline (releasing pressure), Glover's scenes
often end with the pressure still building, carried into the next scene
or left unresolved entirely.[21][10]
Combinable Techniques for a Single Short
The techniques that coexist most naturally:
Fey's character-voice distinction + Glover's register-shifting:
Give each character a clear verbal identity (Fey), then let
conversations drift through multiple tonal registers without
announcement (Glover). The distinct voices make the shifts
legible; the shifts create emotional complexity.
Fey's absurd escalation + Glover's refusal to resolve: Build a
scene using Fey-style escalation (each beat more absurd than the
last), but don't button it with a clean punchline. Instead, let the
escalation reach a point where it stops being funny and becomes
something else — uncomfortable, poignant, strange.
Fey's throwaway jokes + Glover's thematic coherence: Pack
background details with Fey-style visual gags, but ensure each
background gag thematically echoes the scene's emotional
content (Glover). The audience laughs at the surface joke;
rewatching reveals the deeper pattern.
Fey's joke density for the first two-thirds + Glover's silence
for the flnal third: Use the rapid-fire approach to establish
comedic energy and character, then drop into deliberate pacing
for the emotional climax. The shift in rhythm signals to the
audience that something has changed.

5. Transferable Techniques for Short-Form
Sitcom Structure Compressed
A 21-minute sitcom like 30 Rock contains ~7 scenes and runs 3
storylines. A 3-5 minute short contains 3-5 scenes and can sustain
one story with one tonal counterpoint. The A/B/C weave collapses to
an A-story with a thematic B-element (a recurring visual gag, a
background subplot expressed in 2-3 quick beats, or a secondary
character's parallel reaction).
The compressed scene template:
1. Scene 1 (0:00-1:00): Establish character, world, and comic
engine. Fey's technique: the first scene should contain a joke that
reveals character. Glover's technique: the first scene should feel
like you're walking into a conversation already in progress.
2. Scene 2 (1:00-2:30): Escalate. This is where Fey's density
mechanics earn their keep — pack this scene with the highest
joke-per-minute rate. Alternatively, use Glover's drift mechanic
to let the situation grow stranger.
3. Scene 3 (2:30-4:00): Resolve or refuse to resolve. Fey buttons
with a punchline; Glover exits with ambiguity. For emotional
depth, the hybrid: resolve the comedic premise but leave the
emotional question open.
4. Optional Scene 4 (4:00-5:00): Tag/coda. Both Fey and Glover use
end-tags effectively (30 Rock's end-of-episode tags, Community's
iconic "Troy and Abed" tags that Glover performed). A 5-second
to 30-second coda after the emotional climax can reframe the
entire short.[40]

The rhythm change: in a sitcom, the joke rhythm is relatively
constant. In a short, the rhythm should decelerate — starting faster

emotional deepening.
Sketch Techniques That Transfer
Both Fey and Glover have sketch comedy DNA. Fey's SNL training
established the fundamental sketch structure: premise → game →
escalation → blackout/button. Glover's Derrick Comedy work at NYU
built on commitment to absurd premises played straight — his "Mad
Hatter" sketch shows gradual descent into madness delivered with
complete sincerity.[41][42][43]
Transferable sketch mechanics for animated narrative shorts:
The Game: Every sketch has a "game" — the single funny thing
the sketch explores. In a narrative short, identify the game (the
core comic mechanism) and make sure every scene plays it.
"Character tries to be cool but keeps revealing they're not" is a
game. "Two people having the same conversation but about
completely different things" is a game.

Heightening: Sketch comedy escalates the game in each beat.
Transfer this to animated shorts by making each scene a more
extreme version of the previous one.
The Straight Man: Sketch requires at least one character who
recognizes the absurdity. In animated shorts, this character is
the audience's proxy and is essential for emotional grounding.
The Blackout: Sketch ends on the biggest version of the game or
an inversion. Animated shorts can use this as the climax, then
add a brief emotional coda afterward (something sketch doesn't
do, but narrative requires).
Dan Harmon's Community scripts would literally include the stage
direction "And then Donald says something funny" — a placeholder
for Glover to riff — and Glover would consistently deliver lines
funnier than the writers' room could produce. This isn't useful for a
solo creator, but the principle is: leave room in your script for the
performance to elevate the writing, especially in animation where
vocal delivery is everything.[44]
Minimum Viable Ensemble
A 3-minute animated short can support 2-3 distinct characters at
most. The minimum viable ensemble for comedy requires:
The Driver: The character with the want (Fey's Liz, Glover's
Earn)
The Disruptor: The character who prevents the Driver from
getting what they want (Fey's Jack/Tracy, Glover's Darius/Paper
Boi)
Optional: The Observer: A character who comments on or
reacts to the collision (Fey's Kenneth, Glover-style silent witness)
At 3 minutes, give each character a maximum of one defining trait
expressed through their voice. Fey's character-distinction technique
scales down well: assign each character a single reference pool (one
talks in food metaphors, one talks in gaming metaphors, one talks in
weather metaphors) and let the reference pools collide.

Writing Room Techniques for Solo Creators
Fey's room runs on collaborative punch-up — scripts go through
multiple passes where every line is examined for whether it can be
funnier, sharper, or more character-specific. Carlock describes the
core discipline as "trying not only to amuse each other, but to ask the
most annoying questions — does that track, could this be better?".[45]
[2][3]
For a solo creator, simulate this with:
1. First pass: Write the story. Don't worry about jokes.
2. Second pass (Fey pass): Go line by line and ask: can this line
contain a joke? Can this joke contain a second joke? Can this
second joke also reveal character? Apply the layered-joke
principle — look for places where a single line can do multiple
jobs.
3. Third pass (Glover pass): Go scene by scene and ask: is there a
moment where silence would be funnier or more powerful than
a joke? Is there a place where the tone could productively shift?
Where does the audience expect a punchline that you could
withhold?
4. Fourth pass (table read): Read the script aloud, timing it. 30
Rock conducted table reads approximately one week before
shooting, then rewrote based on the room's reactions — laughs,
silences, and confused looks all provided data. As a solo creator,
record yourself reading it and play it back. The places where
your energy drops are the places the audience will tune out.[45]
6. Process
Fey's Writing Room Methodology
The 30 Rock writers' room operated on a high-volume, high-iteration
model inherited from Fey's SNL experience. At SNL, writers submit
dozens of sketches weekly, and only a handful make it to air — a
process Fey has described as both brutal and liberating because "the
biggest gift they give you is the constant failure and the feeling of like,
okay, I failed so deeply and I survived".[2]

At 30 Rock, the process ran: writers pitch story ideas → stories are
selected and broken in the room → individual writers (or pairs) draft
scripts → table read with cast and ~25 people → rewrite based on
table read feedback → rehearsal and shooting on a 12-14 hour day
schedule. Fey and Carlock functioned as the flnal filter, with Carlock
bringing "erudite references and absurd joke constructions" and Fey
bringing structural sharpness and character instinct.[3][45]
The collaboration model was built on creative daring. Fey and
Carlock's most celebrated jokes emerged from one pushing the other
further: "So much of collaboration is trying not only to amuse each
other, but to ask the most annoying questions — does that track,
could this be better? It's torture in the process, but it does ultimately
lead to a better product".[2]
Glover's Creative Process
Atlanta's writers' room was deliberately small and unconventional.
Donald Glover hired an all-Black writing staff, most of whom had
never written for television before — a choice driven by authenticity
rather than credentials. As Stephen Glover explained: "We used to
talk about how this show shouldn't be a show about rappers; it
should be a show for rappers". Donald Glover stated the intent
clearly: "I wanted to show white people, you don't know everything
about Black culture".[46][12]
The room grew across seasons but remained intimate. The creative
process centered on emotional truth over structural convention.
Murai has described their approach: "whenever we break down a
script, we usually schedule the shot list thing but what we actually do
really is just kind of break down the kind of emotional sort of moves
of a scene... so we talk about what's interesting to us personally about
each scene".[14]
The guiding creative principle evolved with the show. By season 4,
Glover's directive was simply "Have more fun. Act like you're going to
get canceled" — a return to the risk-taking mentality of season 1,
where the attitude was essentially that the show wasn't "supposed to
be on TV" so they might as well take every creative risk available.
Murai confirmed that Glover gave the team "free reign" with the
instruction: "I want this show to get canceled — I don't think this

show is supposed to be on TV but they let us have it so we should"
make the most extreme version possible.[47][14]
The Fey-Glover Connection
These processes aren't as distant as they appear. Glover was trained
in Fey's room. He was hired at 23 to write for 30 Rock after Fey
spotted his Derrick Comedy work on YouTube, and he stayed for
three seasons. Fey writes in Bossypants that "because he came from a
large family in Georgia, he was very helpful in writing for the
character Kenneth the Page". Glover has described the experience as
formative: "I decided I wanted to write for television because of Tina.
She was always so happy, and I was like, I want to be happy like that
too".[48][49][50]
Glover absorbed Fey's structural discipline — how to construct a joke,
how to run a room, how to manage ensemble voices — and then
deliberately inverted many of those principles in Atlanta. Where 30
Rock filled every second, Atlanta left space. Where 30 Rock's structure
was reliable, Atlanta's was volatile. Where 30 Rock's comedy was
dense and verbal, Atlanta's was sparse and behavioral. This isn't
rebellion — it's mastery of the rules followed by intentional
subversion. Understanding both systems gives a writer the full
spectrum of comedy craft.
Rewriting and Punch-Up
Both Fey and Glover treat rewriting as essential rather than optional,
but their approaches differ:
Fey's punch-up: Line-by-line examination. Can this joke be funnier?
Can it do double duty (funny AND character-revealing)? The 30 Rock
room would identify "joke deserts" — stretches of more than 8-10
seconds without a laugh — and target them for additional comedy.
Carlock's rule: "Jokes are just surprises" — if a line is predictable, it
needs work.[2]
Glover's refinement: Scene-level examination. Does this scene feel
true? Does the tone do what we intended? Is there a place where
we're over-explaining? The Atlanta room's instinct was toward
reduction rather than addition — cutting dialogue, extending silences,

trusting the audience to flll gaps. Stephen Glover's favorite dialogue
came from the barbershop's opening scene because it captured
authentic voice without any joke construction.[26]
For solo animated short creators: Run both passes. The Fey pass
adds jokes and density. The Glover pass strips away anything that
feels explained rather than experienced. The flnal product should feel
dense but effortless — every joke earned, every silence intentional.
