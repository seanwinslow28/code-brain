As a Comedy Architecture Analyst, I approach screenwriting not as a mystical art, but as structural engineering. To fulfill your request, we must examine two of the most mechanically distinct, yet highly complementary, architects of modern television: **Tina Fey and Donald Glover**.  
Fey is a structural traditionalist who builds ironclad, high-velocity joke machines. Glover is a surrealist architect who constructs grounded realities specifically to erode their foundations. Where they overlap is foundational comedy craft; where they diverge reveals distinct mechanical solutions to pacing, tone, and audience psychology 1, 2\.  
**⚠️ CRITICAL ANTI-PATTERNS TO AVOID:**

* **The Fey Risk:** Joke density so high it prevents emotional landing. If every line is a punchline, the audience has no space to process stakes, rendering the climax emotionally hollow 3, 4\.  
* **The Glover Risk:** Tonal ambiguity so extreme the audience doesn't know how to engage. If the structural "drift" into surrealism lacks a grounded anchor, the audience detaches completely 3, 5\.

Here is the Screenplay Mechanics Profile synthesizing their methodologies for 2D animated comedy shorts.

### 1\. DIALOGUE MECHANICS

**Verbal Differentiation:**

* **Fey (Lexical Fingerprinting):** Characters pull from mutually exclusive ontological reference pools. In *30 Rock*, Jack Donaghy constructs dialogue from weaponized corporate jargon ("vertical integration," "Six Sigma"), while Liz Lemon relies on food obsessions and pop-culture minutiae 6-8. The comedy is generated by the structural collision of these distinct registers occupying the same scene 6, 8\.  
* **Glover (Processing Speeds & Worldview):** Characters share similar vocabularies but operate on different philosophical wavelengths. Earn speaks with hesitant, exhausted, "deadbeat intellectual" hedging; Paper Boi uses direct, guarded pragmatism; Darius floats on serene, esoteric non-sequiturs 9-11.

**Subtext Construction:**

* **Fey:** Avoids subtext. The text *is* the joke. She uses a "layered joke" mechanic to compress time, ensuring a single line delivers 3-4 comedic payloads 12, 13\.  
* **Glover:** Subtext lives in "dead air." Silence, false starts, and incorrect responses are aggressive dialogue components. What characters *fail* to say builds profound emotional tension and signals a shift into the uncanny 9, 14, 15\.

**Sentence Construction Patterns:**

* **Fey:** Machine-gun rhythm, engineered to hit an industry-leading 7.44 jokes per minute (JPM) 16, 17\. Interruptive volleys and rapid-fire cutaways 18\.  
* **Glover:** The Void. Register shifting within a single conversation, drifting from mundane small talk to existential dread without any formal demarcation 19, 20\.

**Example Exchanges:**

* **Fey's Layered Volley (*30 Rock*):** Liz attempts an obscure *Sling Blade* impression. Jenna responds: *"No you don't, Oprah."* **Architecture:** 4 layers of jokes in 4 words. (1) Jenna misidentifies the impression, (2) her counter-impression is just her normal voice, (3) it violates the improv rule of "Yes, And," (4) it perfectly solidifies her narcissistic oblivion 12, 21\.  
* **Glover's Register Shift (*Atlanta* \- "B.A.N."):** On a talk show, an academic delivers an elaborate, multi-paragraph thesis breaking down Paper Boi's rap lyrics as inherently transphobic. Paper Boi simply responds: *"Nope."* **Architecture:** Deflates pretension instantly, shifts the register from intellectual to grounded street reality, and uses silence as the ultimate punchline 10, 20\.

### 2\. SCENE CONSTRUCTION SIGNATURES

**Openings (First 15-30 seconds):**

* **Fey:** The "Cold Open" as a 2.5-page micro-thesis. Opens with a highly recognizable sitcom trope, introduces a comedic misunderstanding, and subverts it with extreme absurdity 22, 23\.  
* **Glover:** The *In Medias Res* Drift. Drops the audience into a completely mundane, grounded conversation (e.g., trying to order a kid's meal with no money) 9, 24\.

**Building Tension / Tonal Pivots / Closings:**

| Structural Element | Tina Fey’s Engine | Donald Glover’s Engine |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Escalation** | """Serve and volley."" Every character has an active, competing want in the scene, generating escalating comic friction 18, 25." | "The ""Drift Mechanic."" The scene begins in stark reality, then slowly erodes the laws of physics or social logic 26, 27." |
| **Tonal Pivot** | "Lateral pivot. A bizarre non-sequitur is deployed for a laugh, then the scene snaps back immediately to the A-story 28." | "Trapdoor pivot. A non-sequitur plunges the entire scene into a new genre (e.g., from urban comedy to gothic horror) 28, 29." |
| **The Button (Close)** | "High-impact punchline that neatly caps the scene and slingshots momentum forward 18, 30." | "Ambiguous hold. The scene cuts away while the tension is still building, leaving the audience unsettled 31, 32." |
| **Pacing Rhythm** | "60-90 seconds. Rapid, frantic, zero dead air 30, 31." | "Deliberate, drawn-out. Willing to trap characters in a ""bottle"" location for agonizing lengths of time 29, 31, 33." |

### 3\. STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

**Macro Organization:**

* **Fey (The Reliable Weave):** Adapts traditional sitcom structure into a hyper-paced, single-camera A-B-C story weave. The A, B, and C plots are mathematically engineered to physically collide in the third act, creating a singular catastrophic/triumphant set-piece 34-36.  
* **Glover (The Unpredictable Anthology):** Abandons the A-B-C weave. Uses a genre-fluid anthology approach where episodes might abandon the main cast entirely, switch into a fake documentary, or become a psychological thriller 37-39.

**Setup and Payoff:**

* **Fey:** Immediate setup/payoff within scenes, supplemented by massive, long-term "background callbacks" (e.g., Kenneth's immortality starting as a throwaway joke and ending as canon) 40\.  
* **Glover:** Delayed, slow-burn payoff. A surreal element is introduced casually as background noise (e.g., Marcus Miles' "invisible car"), ignored for 20 minutes, and then pays off violently at the climax 26, 41\.

### 4\. VISUAL / ANIMATION-RELEVANT TECHNIQUES

**Scripting Visual Storytelling:**

* **Fey (Throwaway Universe):** Fey scripts an immense amount of visual data that doesn't demand narrative emphasis. Frank's trucker hats changing every scene ("Shark Cop," "Space Gravy") or fake movie posters (*Who Dat Ninja?*) 40, 42\. *Translation to 2D:* Fill the background with dense, referential sight gags that reward pausing and rewatching, without slowing down the primary dialogue 43, 44\.  
* **Glover (The Impossible Object):** Glover introduces impossible, surreal imagery (a man in heavy whiteface eating a soft-boiled ostrich egg, a Black Justin Bieber) and forces the characters to treat it with deadpan naturalism 29, 45\. *Translation to 2D:* Exploit animation's boundless visual limits by drawing something utterly bizarre, but animate the characters treating it as a mundane annoyance 26, 46\.

### 5\. THE EMOTIONAL ENGINE

**Engineering Emotional Responses:**

* **Fey (Satirical Displacement):** Fey keeps social commentary funny by displacing it through "benign grandiosity" and hyperbole 47\. To critique capitalism, she makes the corporate executive so cartoonishly sociopathic that the audience laughs at the audacity of the ideology rather than feeling lectured 47, 48\. Emotion is protected by an armor of relentless jokes.  
* **Glover (Experiential Dread):** Glover embeds commentary directly into the structure. By removing the "laugh track mentality," he allows scenes dealing with poverty or racism to breathe as actual distressing drama 49, 50\. The comedy doesn't protect the audience; it curdles into dread, forcing them to experience the systemic absurdity firsthand 51, 52\.

**Endings:**

* **Fey:** Resets the ecosystem. The characters learn a minor lesson, order is restored, and the comedic status quo returns 35, 53\.  
* **Glover:** Refuses to resolve. The comedic premise may conclude, but the emotional or psychological question is left open, bleeding into the credits 32, 50\.

### 6\. TRANSFERABLE TECHNIQUES FOR 3-5 MINUTE COMEDY SHORTS

*Note: Fey's A-B-C story weave DOES NOT compress to short form. 3 minutes cannot support the exposition required for three intersecting plots 54, 55\. Instead, shorts require a focused A-story, utilizing these synthesized mechanics:*  
**Technique 1: The "Bait-and-Drift" Opening**

* **Fey:** Rapidly establishes the "normal world" and conflict with maximum joke density 24, 56\.  
* **Glover:** Abruptly shifts the tone from light comedy into surreal dread 56\.  
* **Why it works:** The Fey setup lulls the audience into a false sense of sitcom security within 30 seconds, maximizing the emotional whiplash of the Glover structural rug-pull 54\.  
* **Animated Short Application:** *Here's the Fey version of this scene:* Two baristas argue in rapid-fire, pop-culture-dense dialogue about the best way to froth milk while dealing with an overly demanding customer. *Here's the Glover version:* Two baristas stare in silence as a customer slowly, methodically pours scalding coffee over their own hand. *Here's what happens if you COMBINE them:* The baristas engage in a rapid-fire, highly articulate Fey-style argument about milk frothing, completely ignoring the Glover-style surreal nightmare of the customer melting their own hand in the foreground 46\.

**Technique 2: Register Collision over an Impossible Object**

* **Fey:** Characters argue using completely incompatible vocabularies 6, 8\.  
* **Glover:** The argument is centered around an impossible, magical-realist event 26, 45\.  
* **Why it works:** It grounds visual absurdity in character-driven neurosis.  
* **Animated Short Application:** *Here's the Fey version:* A grounded protagonist tries to file taxes while their roommate dramatically recounts a bad Tinder date. *Here's the Glover version:* A protagonist tries to file taxes while a literal, animated demon emerges from the floorboards to collect a blood debt. *Here's what happens if you COMBINE them:* The demon emerges from the floorboards, but the roommate treats the demon entirely as a bad Tinder date, using dating-app jargon ("He's totally ghosting me, right?") while the protagonist tries to negotiate the blood debt using IRS tax-code vocabulary 6, 45\.

**Technique 3: The "Joke Desert" Climax**

* **Fey:** Fills every second with a punchline to ensure the audience is always laughing 3, 57\.  
* **Glover:** Uses silence as a tension-building device 14, 15\.  
* **Why it works:** In short form, rhythmic deceleration signals emotional weight.  
* **Animated Short Application:** *Here's the Fey version:* The climax features a chaotic physical brawl where everyone shouts their catchphrases. *Here's the Glover version:* The climax features characters staring at the aftermath of a disaster in uncomfortable silence. *Here's what happens if you COMBINE them:* The first two minutes operate at 8 jokes-per-minute (Fey). At minute 2:30, the animation drops the JPM to zero. The frantic music stops. The characters sit in 15 seconds of protracted, beautifully animated, ambient-sound silence as they realize the emotional gravity of what they just did (Glover) 4, 58\.

**Technique 4: The Background Throwaway Universe**

* **Fey:** Plants highly specific, absurd visual gags in the background that aren't acknowledged by the characters 42, 43\.  
* **Glover:** Uses background details to build thematic, psychological dread 44\.  
* **Why it works:** It creates a massive, lived-in world without burning precious screenplay page-time on exposition 59\.  
* **Animated Short Application:** *Here's the Fey version:* Background posters feature fake, highly satirical movies starring Tracy Jordan. *Here's the Glover version:* The background features a slow-moving, unsettling police brutality incident. *Here's what happens if you COMBINE them:* The background of the shot is packed with hilarious, hyper-specific visual gags (Fey), but every single gag points thematically to the exact emotional trauma the protagonist is desperately trying to ignore in the A-story (Glover) 44\.
