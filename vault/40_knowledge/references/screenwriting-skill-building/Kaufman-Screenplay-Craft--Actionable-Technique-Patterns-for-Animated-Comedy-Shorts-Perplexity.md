Charlie Kaufman Screenplay
Craft: Actionable Technique
Patterns for Animated Comedy
Shorts
Executive Summary
This analysis reverse-engineers Charlie Kaufman's screenplay
mechanics across six dimensions—dialogue, scene construction,
structure, animation relevance, short-form transferability, and
process—extracting specific, replicable technique patterns from Being
John Malkovich, Adaptation, Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind,
Anomalisa, and I'm Thinking of Ending Things. Each technique is
described as a mechanism with concrete examples and assessed for
applicability to 3–5 minute 2D animated comedy shorts with
emotional depth.
1. Dialogue Mechanics
The Stammering Precision Pattern
Kaufman's dialogue sounds naturalistic but is structurally
choreographed. His characters stammer, repeat, and circle—but
every hesitation carries information. The opening train scene of
Eternal Sunshine demonstrates this precisely: Joel says "I'm sorry?"
when Clementine calls "Hi!" across an empty car. She responds "Why
are you sorry? I just said hi." Joel: "No, I didn't know if you were
talking to me, so…" She looks around the empty car: "Really?". Each
line performs double duty—it's simultaneously realistic awkward
small talk and a structural encoding of Joel's defining character trait:
apologizing for his own existence. His "I'm sorry" is not about the
greeting; it's his existential posture compressed into two words.[1][2]

The pattern: Kaufman writes dialogue that is one conversation on
the surface and a different conversation underneath, where the
surface conversation is banal and the underneath conversation is
the emotional thesis of the character. Joel's subsequent "Anyway.
Sorry. Hi. Hello. Hi." is not improvised-sounding clutter—it's three
attempts to restart a human connection, each word a micro-failure.
[1]
Building Subtext Through Behavioral Contradiction
Subtext in Kaufman's work emerges from the gap between what a
character does and what their dialogue says about that doing. In
Anomalisa, Michael Stone sits on a plane reading an angry breakup
letter from his ex Bella—"Dear Michael. Fuck you. Just fuck you. You
just walk away?"—while banal passenger chatter about crossword
puzzles plays around him. The screenplay direction species that
Michael unfolds this letter from his back pocket as the flight
announcement plays, establishing that he carries this wound
physically on his person while performing normalcy. The subtext is
not in what anyone says—it's in the juxtaposition of the mundane
action (landing, seatbelt announcements) against the private
emotional document.[3][4]
Kaufman's subtext rule: Characters never articulate their actual
problem. Instead, the problem manifests as an inability to
perform the mundane task at hand. When Michael calls Bella later,
rehearsing "Hi Bella, it's Michael" multiple times before dialing,
hanging up, redialing—the dialogue is "Hi Bella" but the subtext is a
man who has destroyed every intimate connection he's made and
knows he will again.[3]

The mechanism: Each character has a sentence-length signature
and a relationship to directness. Maxine speaks in short

speaks in questions. Lester speaks in monologues. Floris speaks in

Repetition as Structural Device
Kaufman uses dialogue repetition in three distinct modes:
Mode 1: Escalating Loop (comedy). In Being John Malkovich, the
scene where Craig guesses Maxine's name
—"Buuuhhppaahhhnnnn… Muhhhahhhhh… Maxine?"—then
repeats her name obsessively: "Maxine! Maxine! Maxine! I will shout
it from the rooftops!" While Maxine flatly responds: "Somebody told
you." The repetition reveals Craig's desperation while Maxine's
single-note response reveals her impenetrability.[5]
Mode 2: Erosion Loop (devastation). In Anomalisa, the arguing
couple in the hotel hallway trades "Fuck you" back and forth seven
times. But it's voiced by Tom Noonan—the same voice for both
husband and wife—making it literally a person arguing with
themselves. The repetition isn't just realistic marital combat; it's a
structural mirror of Michael's worldview where everyone is
interchangeable, even in rage.[3]
Mode 3: Identity Dissolution Loop. In I'm Thinking of Ending Things,
the young woman's name changes throughout—Lucy, Louisa, Lucia,
Ames, Amy—and she occasionally notes "Hmmm, that doesn't seem
right? Is that my name?". This repetition-with-variation signals that
reality itself is unreliable, built from a lonely man's faltering memory.
In a Google Talks Q&A, Kaufman confirmed that "she constantly
changes. A lot of other things change, too… costuming and set design
and stuff like that is constantly sort of switching around".[7][8]

2. Scene Construction
Opening a Scene: The Mundane Action as Emotional
Crisis
Kaufman's scene openings follow a consistent pattern: a character
performs a mundane physical action while a separate channel
(voiceover, letter, offscreen dialogue) delivers the emotional
payload. The audience's attention splits between the visible task and
the invisible feeling.
Being John Malkovich, page 1: The screenplay opens not with Craig
himself but with a puppet version of Craig—"a finely sculpted puppet
version of Craig paces back and forth, wringing its hands with
incredible subtlety." The puppet dances, sweats (via a device Craig
controls), collapses, and weeps. Then Craig "hangs the puppet, and
comes down around the front of the stage. He is heaving." The
mundane action is puppeteering. The emotional crisis is that Craig's
only means of self-expression is through a proxy, and even that proxy
ends in collapse. Before we know anything about Craig, we know he
can only access his emotions through someone else's body—which is
the entire thematic engine of the film.[5]
Anomalisa, page 1: The screenplay opens on overlapping airplane
chatter—a boy saying "Hey Ma, look airplane," passengers debating a
crossword clue about Kojak—while Michael silently takes a pill and
unfolds Bella's hate letter. Kaufman's stage direction for the film even
species the intention: "IT IS THE AUTHOR'S INTENTION THAT
MICHAEL VIEWS AND HEARS EVERYONE IN THE WORLD (OTHER
THAN LISA) AS ONE PERSON". The mundane action (flight descent)
and the emotional crisis (a letter documenting destroyed love) exist
simultaneously, and the audience is forced to hold both.[3]
Eternal Sunshine, pages 1–3: Joel sits on a train, watches "slowly
passing desolate terrain." Clementine enters the opposite end of the
car. She "busies herself deciding where to sit." He "feels her watching
him." The mundane action is two strangers on a train. The emotional
crisis—which the audience won't understand until later—is that these
two people have had their memories of each other erased, and their
bodies are finding each other again before their minds can.[1]

Tension Escalation Within a Scene
Kaufman's escalation mechanic operates through accumulating
social discomfort until someone crosses a boundary that can't be
uncrossed. The Anomalisa bar scene with Bella demonstrates this
precisely: polite reunion → drinks → small talk → Michael asking if
she "changed" (repeating the word five times: "did I change you? Did
you change? Did anything change? Did a change occur?") → asking
her to his room → Bella: "We're not going to fuck, Michael" →
Michael: "I'm just really bloody lonely" → Bella: "Fuck you, Michael.
Fuck off." Each beat escalates by one degree of inappropriateness.
The scene doesn't build through external events but through
Michael's inability to stop pushing past the other person's boundaries.
[3]
The Tonal Pivot Mechanism
Kaufman's comedy-to-devastation pivots follow a structural formula:
establish comedic rhythm → introduce a specific sensory detail
that is emotionally loaded → let the character respond honestly
to that detail, breaking the comedic contract.
The Anomalisa hotel room scene is the clearest example. Lisa is
performing comedy—self-deprecating jokes, "Shut up, Lisa,"
comparing herself unfavorably to Emily. Michael asks her to sing. She
giggles, refuses, then sings Cyndi Lauper's "Girls Just Wanna Have
Fun." The screenplay notes: "MICHAEL: That was so beautiful. LISA:
No, it was—Oh, my God, are those tears?" The tonal pivot arrives
because Lisa's singing voice—the only voice that sounds different to
Michael in a world of sameness—is the first genuine human thing
he's experienced. The comedy of her awkwardness flips to
devastation when Michael weeps, because the audience now
understands that his entire existence is an undifferentiated hum, and
this one person's voice has broken through it. The sensory detail (her
singing voice) is what enables the pivot; without it, the turn would
feel arbitrary.[3]

Scene Closings: Buttons vs. Open Endings
Kaufman alternates between two modes. Buttoned scenes end with
a punchline or hard cut: Craig is asked why he was caught cross-
dressing at a girls' school. "Why, Craig, why?" "I… puppeteer." Cut. The
button is funny, but it also structurally summarizes Craig's entire
philosophy of existence in two words.[5]
Open scenes end with a character left in a state of unresolved want:
after Maxine dismisses Craig at the coffee machine, "Craig sits on a
box. He puts his head in his hands and sighs. Across the room he
notices a very small door". The scene doesn't resolve Craig's
humiliation—it converts it into discovery. The open ending propels
narrative momentum because the character's emotional state hasn't
been discharged.[5]
Kaufman's rule: comic scenes get buttons; emotionally vulnerable
scenes stay open. When both modes collide (the Anomalisa "fuck
you" couple, which is simultaneously funny and devastating), the
scene ends on the repetitive loop itself, unresolved.
3. Structural Patterns
Non-Linear and Meta-Narrative Architecture
Kaufman's structural innovations work because they are
thematically motivated, not decoratively experimental. The non-
linearity of Eternal Sunshine exists because memory itself is non-
linear—the erasure procedure works backwards through Joel's
relationship, forcing him to relive bad memories first and good ones
last. This means the audience experiences the relationship's arc in
reverse: hostility, then ambivalence, then warmth, then first love. The
structural effect is that the audience falls in love with Clementine at
exactly the rate Joel re-falls in love with her. The midpoint—when Joel
enters a positive memory and realizes he no longer wants the
procedure—transforms him from passive recipient to active
protagonist. Non-linearity here isn't a puzzle to decode; it's an
emotional architecture that makes you feel what memory erasure
would feel like.[9]

The Self-Referential Loop: How Meta-Narrative Becomes
Functional
Adaptation is a screenplay about the failure to write a screenplay,
which itself becomes the screenplay. The structural principle that
makes this work rather than merely clever is what one analysis calls
a "50/10/40 split"—the first 50% of the film is Charlie's paralysis
(owned by Resistance as the "villain"), the middle 10% is the shortest
conventional middle build in screenwriting, and the flnal 40% is the
formulaic thriller ending that Charlie explicitly said he didn't want to
write.[10]
Kaufman himself explained this structure in an interview: "People
are very critical of Adaptation's ending, they think of the end as a
failure. But one thing I've never seen in print is that that movie was
intended to be a failure… the character in that movie is the script,
that's the character you're following, not the people. And I guess
people can't feel an emotional connection to a screenplay. But I
thought it reflected a corruption of the man who was writing it".[11]
The meta-narrative works because it has stakes. Charlie-the-
character's artistic integrity is the thing at risk. When he finally
attends Robert McKee's seminar, McKee's dialogue—"Wow them in
the end and you've got a hit"—is both a real screenwriting maxim
and an in-story event that corrupts Charlie's principles. Then Charlie
does exactly what McKee prescribes: car chases, drug plots, character
death. The third act is simultaneously formulaic Hollywood
entertainment and a portrait of artistic capitulation. The audience is
entertained by the very thing they're watching the character be
destroyed by.[10][11]
The transferable principle: Meta-narrative becomes functional
when the act of creating the narrative is itself the source of dramatic
conflict, and the structural choices visibly mirror the protagonist's
internal state.

Tonal Shift Mechanics Across a Full Screenplay
Kaufman manages full-screenplay tonal shifts through what might be
called gradual thermal change: he doesn't jump from comedy to
drama; he introduces increasingly dissonant elements within a comic
framework until the comedy can no longer contain them.
In Being John Malkovich, the first 40 pages are pure absurdist comedy
—cross-dressing puppeteer gags, a secretary who mishears
everything, a 7½ floor. But the portal itself is the tonal pivot point:
once characters start entering Malkovich's body, questions of identity,
consent, and control gradually displace the comedy. The Malkovich-
Malkovich scene (where Malkovich enters his own portal and finds a
restaurant where everyone is him, saying only "Malkovich") is the
apex of comedy—but it's also the moment the film tips into body
horror, because Malkovich is now a vessel being used by others
without consent.[12][5]
Endings That Resist Resolution
Kaufman's endings provide emotional catharsis without narrative
closure. Eternal Sunshine ends with Joel and Clementine, having
listened to their own complaints about each other, choosing to try
again anyway. Clementine says "Okay." The film loops the flnal shot
three times before fading to white—suggesting both a fresh start and
an infinite cycle. The ending is satisfying because it resolves the
thematic question (is it better to have loved and lost or to have
erased?) while leaving the plot question (will they work out?)
deliberately unanswered.[13]
In his Reverse Shot interview, Kaufman described his approach: "I
find it very uninteresting going in with a clear idea of what I'm going
to do. For me, not knowing is the most important thing about writing;
the writing is the exploration". This philosophy extends to endings:
the audience is given enough to feel emotionally complete but not
enough to stop thinking.[11]

Pacing Signatures
Kaufman lingers on mundane process (checking into a hotel, ordering
room service, shower temperature adjustments) and cuts fast
through conventionally "important" events. Anomalisa dedicates its
first 15 screenplay pages to: landing, cabbie small talk, checking in,
bellhop tour, room service order, phone call home, Bella's letter,
finding the bar—a sequence of painstaking banality. The sex scene
later is extended and awkward in real-time. Meanwhile, Michael's
keynote speech—the ostensible reason for his trip—collapses in
seconds. The rhythm is: dwell on the private, compress the public.
This inverts conventional pacing where public events get emphasis
and private moments get montaged.[3]
4. Animation and Visual Storytelling
Relevance
How Anomalisa Uses Animation to Enhance Theme
The screenplay for Anomalisa opens with a production note that no
other Kaufman screenplay contains: a direct statement of authorial
intention regarding the Fregoli Delusion. This note exists because the
central conceit—that Michael perceives everyone as the same person
—can only be shown in animation. In live-action, you would need to
cast one actor in every role (which was the solution for the original
stage play, using only voices). Stop-motion animation allowed
Kaufman and Johnson to give every character the same face while
still having them appear as distinct people through hairstyle, clothing,
and body type.[6][3]
Crucially, the filmmakers chose to leave the visible seams on the
puppet faces. "The option typically is to erase them in VFX, but that
was counterintuitive to [our approach]," Kaufman explained. These
seams—lines across the bridge of each puppet's nose—serve a dual
function: they remind the audience that these are constructed beings
(reinforcing the theme of manufactured identity), and they create a
subtle uncanny valley that makes the mundane hotel setting feel
slightly nightmarish.[14][6]

The screenplay-level choices that only work because it's animated
include: Lisa's face being slightly different from everyone else's (same
mold but with a scar and different eyes—impossible to execute subtly
in live-action); the moment where Michael tries to pull off his own
faceplate in the bathroom mirror (a moment of identity crisis
rendered as a literal physical action); and the breakfast scene where
Lisa's voice begins to duplicate and fade into Noonan's, suggesting
that even she is becoming "everyone else" to Michael.[15][3]
Visual Metaphors Set Up at the Screenplay Level
Kaufman's visual metaphors are not directorial flourishes—they're
embedded in the screenplay's structural DNA.
The Portal (BJM): Craig discovers the portal only after being rejected
by Maxine—the screenplay species that he "sits on a box, puts his
head in his hands and sighs," then "across the room he notices a very
small door". The portal is positioned as a response to emotional
failure: Craig can't connect to a real person, so he discovers a literal
mechanism for inhabiting another person's body. The script's
dialogue even draws the line explicitly: Craig's pitch to Maxine about
puppeteering—"It's the idea of becoming someone else for a little
while. Being inside another skin"—is the thematic statement that the
portal literalizes.[5]
Memory Erasure (Eternal Sunshine): The screenplay describes
memories deteriorating as physical spaces collapsing—houses
crumbling, faces blurring, settings going dark. Joel's subconscious
resistance manifests as him trying to hide Clementine in childhood
memories where she doesn't belong. The visual metaphor is set up
entirely through stage direction: the screenplay instructs that "the
diegesis of his mind literally begins to collapse around him".[9]
The Puppet Theater (Anomalisa): The stop-motion medium is the
metaphor. Michael is a man who sees others as puppets (same face,
same voice), and the audience watches literal puppets performing his
story. The screenplay sets this up by drowning the reader in identical
dialogue patterns from interchangeable characters for the first 15
pages, so that Lisa's arrival feels like oxygen.[14]

5. Transferable Techniques for Short-Form
What Survives Compression to 3–5 Pages
The techniques that compress best are those that operate at the scene
level rather than the act level:
1. The Contradictory Opening Image. BJM's first scene—a puppet
weeping while its puppeteer sweats—establishes character,
theme, and emotional state in under a page. For a 3-minute

short: open on a character performing a task that is the
metaphor for their problem, before any dialogue.[5]
2. The Behavioral Subtext Beat. Michael rehearsing "Hi Bella, it's
Michael" three times before calling—and hanging up the first
time. This takes 10 seconds of screen time and communicates
volumes about loneliness, regret, and cowardice. It needs zero
setup or context.[3]
3. The Stammering Precision Exchange. Joel's "Sorry. Hi. Hello.
Hi." or Craig's "I'll be in in a little while. I need to unwind a little.
I'll be in soon. A little while". These short bursts of repetitive
dialogue communicate character state faster than any
exposition could.[1][5]
4. The Single Tonal Pivot. Lisa singing → Michael crying works in
a single scene. The formula: establish comic rhythm → introduce
one genuine sensory/emotional detail → let a character respond
honestly, breaking the comedy. This is the minimum viable
Kaufman unit.
Humor as Trojan Horse: The Single-Scene Mechanic
The mechanical structure of Kaufman's "laugh then punch" works as
follows in a single scene:
Step 1: Absurd Premise. Ground it in a specific, relatable mundane
situation. Not "a man enters a portal" in the abstract—"a man pries
open a tiny door with a two-by-four in a cramped office and crawls
into a wet membranous tunnel."[5]
Step 2: Escalating Comic Specificity. Stack increasingly absurd but
internally logical details. The taxi driver in Anomalisa doesn't just
recommend the zoo—he species "Pandas. Other things. Owls." then
insists Michael try Cincinnati chili: "They make it here with cinnamon
and chocolate and they serve it over pasta. Imagine that if you can."
The comedy comes from excessive helpfulness, which is funny
because it's recognizable.[3]
Step 3: The Human Moment. Once the audience is laughing,
introduce a line that is completely, unguardedly honest. After all the
chili banter, Michael asks: "Is there a toy store near the hotel?" The
driver: "A toy store? You mean a TOY store?" It turns out to be a sex
shop. The comedy continues—but underneath it, Michael is trying to

buy something for his son, and the city keeps failing him even in this
tiny parental gesture. The humor stays, but it now sits on top of
sadness.[3]
Step 4: Don't Comment on the Shift. Kaufman never has a character
say "This is sad." The shift happens in the audience, not in the text.
The Absurdism Anchor: What Keeps It Grounded
Kaufman's absurdist premises feel emotionally true because every
fantastical element is an externalization of a common interior
experience. The portal in BJM externalizes the desire to be someone
else. Memory erasure externalizes the wish to forget an ex. The
Fregoli delusion externalizes the experience of feeling like everyone
around you is the same.[16][12]
The anchor is always a specific, unglamorous emotional state:
loneliness, creative failure, romantic rejection, the inability to
connect. Kaufman described this in his BAFTA lecture: "I do believe
[people] have a wound… It is the thing that must be hidden and
protected, it is the thing that must be tap danced over five shows a
day". The fantastical premise is the tap dance; the wound is the
anchor.[17]
For short-form: pick a specific, small emotional wound (not
"existential dread" but "the feeling when someone doesn't text back"),
then build the absurdist premise as a literal manifestation of that
wound.
Minimum Viable Character Establishment
Kaufman establishes character in the first 60 seconds through one
behavioral choice that reveals the gap between who the
character wants to be and who they are:
Craig (BJM): Makes a puppet of himself that dances beautifully,
then collapses and weeps. Craig aspires to transcendence; he
achieves only self-pity.[5]
Joel (Eternal Sunshine): Sits on a train, watches desolate
terrain, then writes in his journal: "Why do I fall in love with

every woman I see who shows me the least bit of attention?" Joel
aspires to self-sufficiency; he's actually desperate.[1]
Michael (Anomalisa): Takes a pill, unfolds a hate letter,
responds to a stranger grabbing his hand with "I said it's okay.
You can let go now though." Michael aspires to politeness; he's
actually repulsed by human contact.[3]
Charlie (Adaptation): In a meeting with a studio executive, says
he doesn't want "characters learning profound life lessons or
growing or coming to like each other or overcoming obstacles".
Charlie aspires to artistic integrity; the entire movie will be him
failing to maintain it.[10]
The pattern: one action or line that contains both the aspiration
and the failure, simultaneously. For a 3-minute short, this is your
first 15 seconds.
6. Process
Idea Development and Writing Process
Kaufman described his process in a 1999 interview: "I think about
things for a long time. I think about things a lot. I write notes to
myself, I take long walks and write things down on a little pad in my
pocket". He then starts writing, sometimes outlining first but typically
abandoning the outline: "Then maybe I'll outline and I'll get bored
with it and it'll be lifeless. It's like without a safety net, it makes it
more interesting, because you can fail".[18]
His scripts typically take two to three years. Being John Malkovich was
an anomaly at six months, written while he was waiting for TV hiring
season. He has never used an assistant for writing: "Research for me
is part of the learning process. No one else could do it for me. I read
things really haphazardly".[19]
The critical insight into his development process is that he writes
about whatever he's currently thinking about, even when it
contradicts the assignment. For Adaptation: "I find I write best when I
write what I'm thinking about. What I was thinking about was that I
was completely unable to write this script. When I started to think
about what that meant, and put myself in there, I started seeing

connections". This is not a method anyone can simply adopt, but the
underlying principle is: follow the energy of your actual
preoccupation, not the assignment. If you're stuck, the stuck-ness
itself is material.[18]
Rewriting and Discovery
Kaufman does not pre-plan; he discovers through the writing itself.
His BAFTA lecture framed this as philosophy: "A screenplay is an
exploration. It's about the thing you don't know. It's a step into the
abyss. It necessarily starts somewhere, anywhere; there is a starting
point but the rest is undetermined. It is a secret, even from you". He
reinforced this in the Reverse Shot interview: "I find it very
uninteresting going in with a clear idea of what I'm going to do. For
me, not knowing is the most important thing about writing".[20][17]
[11]
He also resists the concept of craft-as-formula, telling BAFTA: "I think
craft is a dangerous thing… I think the danger of craft is that it needs
to be in second position to what it is that you're doing". He described
watching a technically impressive trailer and thinking "I know that
this movie is going to do really well, and I know that the people who
made it are going to get rewarded for it, and so the cycle continues".
[21]
His rewriting is often forced by collaborators rather than self-
initiated. One Reddit user who binge-read all Kaufman scripts noted:
"He's discovering his characters in that first 2/3, he's discovering
structure and theme and everything else. Then he realizes he's on
page 60 or even 90 and needs to wrap up, so the pace goes to light
speed… Only when a director comes in is he forced to significantly
rewrite".[22]
The BAFTA Lecture: Core Principles
Key craft principles from Kaufman's 2011 BAFTA lecture, distilled to
actionable maxims:[23][24][17][21]
1. "Don't let anyone tell you what a story is." Go out of your way
to write a "non-story"—it will still be a story, but it will have a
chance of being a different one.[20]

2. "With a screenplay you're creating a world." Consider every
character, room, juxtaposition, and increment of time as an
embodiment of that world. Nothing in the screenplay should be
separate from the unified composition.[21]
3. "Do not write jokes to your readers in your stage directions."
Your job is to establish mood and tone, not wink at the reader.
[17]
4. "Know why it's a movie." If the story doesn't need to be told in
this specific medium, it shouldn't be told in this medium.[25][21]
5. "Failure is a badge of honor." It means you risked something. If
you don't risk failure, you'll never produce anything that differs
from what already exists.[23][17]
Commercial Expectations vs. Personal Vision
Kaufman navigates commercial pressure primarily through budget
control. He explained that Amy Pascal at Sony offered two deals for
Adaptation: a higher budget with studio interference, or a lower
budget with creative freedom. He and Spike Jonze chose the lower
budget, and Pascal kept her word. Kaufman has stated flatly: "I'm not
interested in it as a product, not interested in trying to flgure out how
this movie will attract a certain demographic, not interested in fltting
into a genre".[11]
When asked about the tension between vision and commerce, he
pointed to the industry's structural incentives: "There are really good
comedy writers out there, but there are a shitload of people who are
not, and they also work, and the stuff that you see on TV is a fair
reflection of the level of skill out there". His own solution has been to
work cheaply enough that studios accept the risk, and to trust that
honest work will find its audience—a model that has produced
critically celebrated work with modest box office returns.[26]
Applying Kaufman to 3–5 Minute Animated
Shorts: A Synthesis
The minimum viable Kaufman unit for a 3–5 minute 2D animated
comedy short consists of four structural elements:

1. Opening Image (0:00–0:15): A character performs a mundane
action that IS the metaphor for their emotional wound. No
dialogue necessary. The animation medium lets you visually
literalize the internal state.
2. Comic Escalation (0:15–2:00): Build specific, absurd details that
are internally logical. Each detail is funny on the surface and
thematically loaded underneath. Characters speak in their
verbal fingerprints. Repetition drives rhythm.
3. The Pivot (2:00–2:30): Introduce one genuine, sensory, human
moment that breaks the comic contract. A character responds
honestly instead of performing. The animation style can shift
here—frame rate change, color palette shift, visual
simplification.
4. Open Ending (2:30–3:00): Do not resolve the emotional wound.
Leave the character in a state of having recognized the wound
without solving it. Button with a single image or line that
contains both humor and sadness simultaneously.
This structure honors Kaufman's core principle—that absurdism
works when it's anchored to a specific human wound—while
operating within short-form constraints. The techniques that require
feature-length runway (identity dissolution, gradual tonal thermal
change, meta-structural commentary) can be acknowledged but not
attempted at this scale. What survives compression is the mechanics
of a single scene: behavioral subtext, the stammering precision
exchange, the humor-to-devastation pivot, and the open ending.
