Here is the SCREENPLAY CRAFT SYSTEM designed specifically for 3-5 minute 2D animated
comedy shorts with emotional depth. This framework operates as a modular engineering toolkit,
organized by narrative function and stress-tested for the constraints of short-form animation.
1. DIALOGUE TOOLKIT
This module dictates how characters sound, process information, and fail to communicate.
Application Mapping:
● Naturalistic / Improvised-Feeling: Utilize Waititi’s Scripted Improv and Burnham’s
Textual Phonetics. Script the hesitation of the human mind using mid-sentence periods,
double-dashes for self-correction, and filler words ("um," "like") hard-coded into the text
1-5. Characters should use "Diagonal Communication"—ignoring the main point to fixate
on a minor, irrelevant detail 1, 5.
● Subtext-Heavy / Literary: Deploy Kaufman’s Simultaneous Parallel Monologue.
Characters talk past each other, responding only to their own internal anxieties rather
than their scene partner's dialogue, creating subtext through communicative failure 6-8.
● Rapid-Fire Comedic: Use Fey’s Lexical Fingerprinting and Layered Volleys. Assign
characters mutually exclusive reference pools (e.g., corporate jargon vs. food
obsessions) and compress 3-4 comedic payloads into a single line of dialogue 9-12.
● Silent or Minimal: Apply Miyazaki’s Yakuwarigo (Role Language) and Glover’s Dead
Air. Define a character's authority or submissiveness instantly through their pronouns
and sentence-final particles, reducing the need for exposition 13-15. Use silence as an
aggressive conversational response that builds dread 16, 17.
● Ensemble (3+ Voices): Rely on Pixar’s Functional Division. Assign one character as
the "Emotional Anchor" (carrying the thematic need) and another as the "Comedic
Disruptor" (challenging the anchor's worldview) 18-20. Further differentiate them using
Waititi’s Self-Awareness Spectrum—where each character sits between pure delusion
and exhausted omniscience 5, 21, 22.
Concrete Combination Example: Waititi’s Diagonal Communication + Fey’s Lexical
Fingerprinting.Character A (using corporate logistics) is trying to defuse a literal ticking bomb,
while Character B (using dating-app jargon) is fixated on a text message.
A: The red wire dictates the payload synergy—hand me the wire cutters!B: He left me on read.
That’s classic ghosting syntax, right?A: If we don’t synergize the blue wire, we die. Cutters!B: I
mean, his profile said he was emotionally available, but—A: Actually, wait, did he use a period
after "Okay"?(Character A abandons the bomb to look at the phone, engaging in diagonal
communication while maintaining their respective lexical fingerprints).
2. SCENE CONSTRUCTION TOOLKIT
How to architect the micro-units of your short film.
Scene Openings:
● The Visualization of Delusion (Waititi/Kaufman): Open directly on a character
performing a low-status, mundane action while delivering a high-status,
self-aggrandizing monologue 23-26. Use case: Instant character establishment in the
first 15 seconds.

● Show-The-World-First (Miyazaki): 10-15 seconds of ambient environment (wind
blowing, minnows in a stream) before introducing narrative pressure 27, 28. Use case:
Grounding a highly fantastical or magical-realist premise.
● The Micro-Thesis Cold Open (Fey): Establish a familiar trope and violently subvert it
with high joke density in 30 seconds 29, 30. Use case: High-BPM comedy shorts
needing immediate momentum.
Tension & Comedy Building:
● Bureaucratic Absurdism (Kaufman/Waititi): Introduce a terrifying or surreal element,
but force the characters to interact with it as a mundane logistical annoyance (e.g.,
complaining about the ceiling height of a portal, or vampires arguing over dirty dishes) 4,
31-33.
● The Therefore/But Engine (Pixar): Ensure every beat happens because of the last
one, or is interrupted by a but. Never connect beats with "and then" 20, 34.
● The Say/Feel Gap (Burnham): Script dialogue that projects confidence while writing
action lines where the character self-soothes (twirling hair, shifting weight), building
tension through the absence of social rescue 34-36.
The Tonal Pivot (Ranked by Speed & Intensity):
1. The Mid-Laugh Turn (Waititi): Fastest/Highest Intensity. The devastation arrives
mid-joke. Do not signal it with darkening tone or music. Let the visual motif (coded as
warm/comedic) abruptly recontextualize into tragedy 37-39.
2. Violent Juxtaposition (Burnham): High Intensity. Abruptly cut the backing track, freeze
the kinetic animation, and drop into stark, whispering sincerity while keeping the visual
container constant 40-42.
3. The Uncanny Click (Kaufman): Medium Speed. Establish a comedic rhythm, then
introduce one genuine sensory detail (a unique singing voice) that the character reacts
to with absolute, shield-less honesty 43-45.
4. The Misdirection (Pixar): Slowest/Highest Empathy. Place your hardest emotional hit
immediately after a sequence of levity or false hope, exploiting the contrast effect 46-48.
Scene Closings:
● The Button (Fey): A high-impact punchline that slingshots momentum forward 29, 49.
● The Vacuum Effect (Burnham): Stripping away all audio post-climax to sit in the
unvarnished reality 50-52.
● The Ma Beat (Miyazaki): A 3-5 second hold on a static wide shot after chaos, featuring
a single micro-action (a blink, a sigh) and ambient audio 28, 53-55.
3. STRUCTURAL OPTIONS FOR 3-5 MINUTE SHORTS
Feature-length mechanisms like the A-B-C sitcom weave 56 and 60-minute setup/payoff
windows 57 will fatally bloat a short. Use these proven compressed models:
Model A: Linear Emotional Arc (The Pixar Model)
● Mechanic: The Single-Minded Protagonist (Want Engine). The comedy stems entirely
from their grueling, absurd attempts to achieve one objective 58, 59.
● Beat Count (5 Beats): 1. Establish (0:00-0:30) -> 2. Disruption (0:30-1:00) -> 3. Iterative
Escalation (1:00-2:15) -> 4. Crisis/Shift (2:15-2:45) -> 5. New Equilibrium (2:45-3:00) 60,
61.

● Pacing: Accelerating until the crisis, then releasing 62.
Model B: Visual/Environmental Arc (The Miyazaki Model)
● Mechanic: Kishōtenketsu (four-act, change-driven, conflict-free). Driven by visual
discovery rather than an antagonist 63, 64.
● Beat Count (4 Beats): Ki (Routine/Intro) -> Sho (Development/Escalation of routine) ->
Ten (Twist/Environmental chaos) -> Ketsu (Reconciliation) 65-67.
● Pacing: Deceleration (Ma) before sudden acceleration 28, 68.
Model C: Comedy with Hidden Payload (Waititi/Burnham Model)
● Mechanic: Humor as Trojan Horse. Use comedy to lower defenses, then weaponize the
premise 69, 70.
● Beat Count: 15% emotional grounding -> 60% comedic escalation -> 25% devastating
turn/payload 71, 72. (Note: This inverts Waititi's feature-length 75/25 ratio because
shorts lack the runway to earn empathy later 71).
● Pacing: Frantic comedic rhythm abruptly hitting a brick wall of silence 41, 42.

5. VISUAL STORYTELLING INTEGRATION
Techniques for writing action lines that direct the animator's pen.
● Haptic Visuality (Miyazaki): Script sensory stimuli that the audience can "feel." Do not
write "she runs." Write the physical friction: "She braces her boots against the warped
floorboards—friction lines streak the wood. Her shoulders shake under the gravity"
82-84.
● Physical Comedy as Clause Accumulation (Waititi): Write visual gags rhythmically. "It
flies through the air, bounces off a tree, hits another one, deflects off a nearby branch
and ricochets..." Each clause is a distinct animation staging 25, 85.
● Metaphor Compression (Pixar): Make abstract subtext the literal, physical text. Instead
of writing that a character feels empty-nest syndrome, script a dumpling coming to life
and trying to leave home 86, 87.
● Environmental Accumulation (Burnham): Script the background's progressive decay.
"Minute 1: Clean desk. Minute 2: Tangled cables accumulate. Minute 3: Color palette
desaturates by 20%, line-art becomes frantic." The background narrates the mental state
88-90.
● Deadpan Reaction Holds (Waititi): Give the animator instructions to hold a cel.
"Background: Explosions, 24fps. Foreground Character: Static hold for 12 frames,
maintaining a 'processing stare' before executing a single defensive blink" 91, 92.

6. COMBINATION MAP
How to synthesize techniques into new mechanical tools for animation.
5 Powerful Pairings (With Scene Sketches):
● Fey’s Bait-and-Drift + Glover’s Impossible Object:
● Sketch: Two baristas argue in a rapid-fire, pop-culture-dense volley about frothing milk
(Fey). They completely ignore a literal, animated demon melting a customer in the
foreground (Glover) 93, 94.
● Waititi’s Comedy Under Pressure + Miyazaki’s Soot Sprite Chorus:
● Sketch: Characters are hiding from a terrifying monster in dead silence, playing puerile
shadow-puppet pranks to cope (Waititi) 95, 96. In the background, tiny dust-creatures
break their labor routine to silently cheer for the shadow puppets, before fleeing when
the monster looks (Miyazaki) 97, 98.
● Pixar’s 2+2 Rule + Burnham’s Say/Feel Gap:
● Sketch: A character records a flawless, hyper-confident voiceover (Burnham) 35, 36,
while the animation shows them utterly failing at the task, leaving the audience to infer
the character's crippling impostor syndrome without it being stated (Pixar) 99-101.
● Kaufman’s Bureaucratic Absurdism + Waititi’s Deadpan Hold:
● Sketch: A glowing dimensional rift opens in a kitchen. The character stares blankly for 4
seconds (Waititi) 91, 102. They sigh, grab duct tape, and angrily try to tape a rug over it
because the draft is annoying (Kaufman) 33.
● Burnham’s Container Constancy + Pixar’s Misdirection:
● Sketch: An incredibly upbeat, vibrant 2D musical sequence about baking (Burnham
container) 103 occurs right before the climax. The song hits its peak joy—and the
character drops the cake, sacrificing their ultimate goal to save a secondary character
(Pixar execution) 46, 48, 104.
Conflicting Techniques (Do Not Combine):
● Fey’s Joke Density (7.44 JPM) + Miyazaki’s Ma. You cannot sustain a machine-gun
rhythm and then suddenly ask the audience to sit in 7 minutes of atmospheric silence 16,
28, 105, 106. The whiplash will break the viewer's engagement.
● Waititi’s Mid-Laugh Turn + Kaufman’s 50/10/40 Meta-Inversion. Kaufman's structure
requires 60+ minutes of baseline to subvert 107, 108. Attempting a feature-length
meta-collapse inside a 3-minute short will read as incoherent, not tragic 26, 109.
The Minimum Viable Technique Set (A Synthesized Personal Voice):To draw from all six,
your baseline operating system should be: Pixar's Single-Minded Protagonist navigating
Kaufman's Bureaucratic Absurdism, speaking in Burnham's Scripted Disfluency, shot with
Miyazaki's Haptic Visuality, and culminating in Waititi's Mid-Laugh Turn.
7. ENHANCING THE WRITER'S EXISTING SIGNATURE MOVES
Mapping the extracted mechanics to amplify your established prose voice in a screenplay
format.
● Hard Cut / Deflation
● Enhanced by: Burnham’s Vacuum Effect and Fey’s The Button.

● Application: Build a massive, grandiose visual escalation, then use a hard cut to black,
instantly dropping the audio track to dead zero (Vacuum) 50-52. Punctuate the silence
with a single, devastatingly mundane punchline (Button) 29, 49.
● Humor as Trojan Horse
● Enhanced by: Waititi’s 75/25 Turn and Kaufman’s Absurdism Anchor.
● Application: Base your Trojan Horse on a specific, unglamorous human wound
(Kaufman) 76, 77. Use comedy to build the audience's investment in the premise, then
pull the rug out mid-laugh, ensuring the setup was coded warmly (Waititi) 39, 110, 111.
● Screenwriting Cut-To
● Enhanced by: Waititi’s Visualization of Delusion and Glover’s Confessional
Shortcut.
● Application: Character delivers a confident statement directly to the camera (the
delusion). CUT TO: B-roll of them spectacularly failing in the exact way they just denied,
generating instant interiority 112-114.
● Rule of Three with Emotional Pivot
● Enhanced by: Burnham’s Nested Hierarchy and Kaufman’s Uncanny Click.
● Application: Beat 1: Absurd joke. Beat 2: Escalated absurd joke (Burnham) 3, 115. Beat
3: The pivot into unshielded sincerity, triggered by a specific, honest sensory detail that
breaks the comedic contract (Kaufman) 43, 45, 116.
● Self-Deprecation as Structure
● Enhanced by: Burnham’s Self-Implicating Pivot.
● Application: Never let the protagonist sit above the critique. When they deconstruct a
flaw in the world, the structure must immediately reveal how they are a pathetic victim of,
or contributor to, that exact flaw 117-119.
● Pop Culture Anchoring
● Enhanced by: Fey’s Lexical Fingerprinting and Waititi’s Semantic Noise.
● Application: Lock specific pop-culture eras to specific characters 12, 120. During a
high-stakes emotional climax, have them diagonally communicate by fiercely arguing
over a pop-culture trivia detail, using the triviality to mask their actual vulnerability 1, 121,
122.
● Hyper-Specific Anecdotes
● Enhanced by: Pixar’s Rule of Specificity and Miyazaki’s Mundane Micro-Actions.
● Application: Never let characters speak in generalities 101, 117, 123. When delivering
the anecdote, anchor it to a hyper-specific, animated micro-action (e.g., struggling to
open a stubborn jar while recounting a childhood trauma), forcing the animation to carry
the subtext of the story 83, 124.
