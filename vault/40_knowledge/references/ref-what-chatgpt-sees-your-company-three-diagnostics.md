If I were walking into a marketing role in 2026, I would not start by asking which AI tools the team uses.

The content workflow is not where I would start. Neither is the lifecycle automation stack, and I would not waste an early conversation asking whether the team is on ChatGPT, Claude, Jasper, Canva, Midjourney, Clay, HubSpot Breeze, Salesforce Agentforce, or some agentic Frankenstein the growth team duct-taped together over a long weekend. Those questions answer themselves once you know what the team is responsible for.

I would start with a more basic question, the kind that determines whether everything downstream is sane:

*Does this company understand that marketing now has two audiences?*

Most leadership teams think they already know the answer. Almost none of them have internalized what it means. Marketing now serves two audiences at once. It serves the humans it has always served, and the agents that increasingly read, summarize, compare, and recommend on behalf of those humans before any human decision gets made. Companies that miss the split lose on both sides.

The losses are already showing up in the data. In a March 2026 survey of 1,076 B2B software buyers, sixty-nine percent reported choosing a different vendor than they originally planned because of guidance from an AI chatbot, and roughly a third bought from a vendor they had never heard of before the assistant surfaced it. That is not the work of better marketing. It is the work of a comparison system that read the public surfaces, weighed them against each other, and produced a shortlist — one that often did not include the buyer’s original preferred vendor.

I’m starting a series on what I would do walking into a job today across the functions AI is reshaping. Marketing first, because I spent a long stretch of my career in and around it. Most of the AI-marketing conversation right now is about tools and adoption curves. This piece is about what the job has to become.

**Here’s what’s inside:**

- **The two audiences, and why most companies serve only one.** What agents need is not a better tagline. It’s legibility, and legibility is a different job than persuasion.
- **The “make more stuff” trap.** Why an AI strategy built around content velocity puts your career on the most commoditized layer of the role.
- **The truth layer.** Why marketing has to become the steward of the company’s claims, proof, and product reality, not the decorator of decisions made elsewhere.
- **Why AI-washing is a trust-debt loan.** What happens when companies (and candidates) stretch their AI story past what they can defend, and why the agents catch it first.
- **What to look for in a marketing role in 2026.** The questions to ask before you take the job, and the surfaces marketing has to be allowed to touch.
- **The AI-washing audit.** A skill pack for Claude Desktop, Claude Code, ChatGPT, and Codex that builds your company’s truth layer from the ground up — diagnosis, claims-and-evidence map, AI-washing risk register, and a prioritized fix list.

Let me show you what the split looks like, why most companies are sitting on the wrong side of it, and what the marketers who win the next decade will be doing differently.

## The funnel marketing inherited

For the last twenty years, the internet handed marketing a fairly stable mental model. You fought for human attention somewhere you did not control: search results, social feeds, paid placements, earned media, creators, newsletters, podcasts, events, communities, review sites, analyst reports, the occasional tradeshow booth. The job was to win that attention long enough to pull the human onto a surface you *did* control: your website, your app, your signup flow, your sales process, your checkout page.

The basic shape was: win attention, capture intent, control the experience, move the human through the funnel. Every modern marketing org chart, every category of tool, every measurement framework — MQLs, attribution, conversion rate optimization, lifecycle automation — assumes some version of that loop.

That model is not dead. People are still people. They still discover things socially. They still remember brands. They still click links, watch demos, buy emotionally and justify rationally, ask friends what to use, get annoyed by vague positioning and ugly websites. None of that goes away because agents exist.

But the loop is no longer the whole game.

## Agents enter the buying loop

Buyers are starting to bring agents into the decision. Sometimes explicitly. They ask an AI system what to buy, which vendors to shortlist, which product fits their specific situation, what tradeoffs to weigh. Sometimes implicitly, because the tools they already use summarize, rank, recommend, compare, retrieve, and filter on their behalf without anyone framing it as “using AI to buy software.” The agent may not own the entire purchase, but it will sit in the middle of more and more of it: the discovery, the evaluation, the comparison memo, the RFP draft, the procurement justification, the renewal review.

This is not a future scenario. Gartner projects that by 2028, ninety percent of B2B buying will be intermediated by AI agents, with roughly fifteen trillion dollars in B2B spend flowing through agent-mediated exchanges. It is already happening at the early end of the curve, especially in technical buying, in long-tail discovery, and inside companies that have rolled out internal copilots tied to their own document stores. It is becoming normal enough that ignoring it will look, in retrospect, like ignoring mobile looked in 2012.

The point is not that agents replace human judgment. They don’t. The point is that more and more buying decisions now involve a non-human reader making sense of your company before any human decides anything.

Here is what that looked like for me recently. I needed a sound system. I had a vague preference, no strong brand allegiance, and a budget I was willing to defend. I went to an AI assistant and asked it to compare options against my room size, my listening habits, and what I’d actually use it for. It came back with three contenders, ranked, with tradeoffs explained in a paragraph. I picked one of them.

The company I had been buying from for years before that conversation, despite having decent products and fair pricing, was not in the three. They could have been — the specs were there, the reviews were there. But they had never given me a reason to remember them, never built a moment of preference strong enough to override a six-second comparison memo generated by a tool I trusted more than my own search habits. So when I outsourced the comparison, the comparison flattened them. They lost the sale without ever knowing they were in the running.

That is what the agent layer does at the buyer level. It does not replace the buyer. It replaces the part of the buyer that used to do the comparison work and carry the brand preference into it. When the brand preference is weak, the agent fills the vacuum with whatever its retrieval surfaces best.

That changes marketing at the root.

## Two audiences, two jobs

Agents do not care about your tagline or your brand film. Your manifesto does not move them. The homepage that says “AI-native” in a tasteful gradient over a hero video of a knowledge worker laughing at her laptop? Invisible.

What agents need is legibility. They need to know what your product does, who it is for, what it costs, what it integrates with, where it is strong, where it is weak, how customers describe it, and how it compares to the alternatives. They need that information in a form that survives compression: dense, structured, internally consistent, supported by evidence that can be cited.

Humans still need the older things: story, trust, memory, status, emotional resonance, reasons to care.

So the future marketer is not the person who makes more content with AI. The future marketer is the person who makes the company *memorable to humans and legible to agents.*

Those are different jobs. They have to be done together.

## The “make more stuff” trap

Most of the AI-in-marketing conversation feels too small to me. It is almost entirely focused on internal workflow, which makes sense as the first thing a marketer can see, touch, and adopt without organizational drama. AI can generate ad variants, draft lifecycle emails, and build landing pages. AI can turn webinars into clips and summarize customer calls. AI can produce sales collateral, image concepts, SEO briefs, personalized outbound, and roughly forty versions of the same launch tweet.

All of that is real. It will change team structures, agency relationships, headcount math, and the day-to-day texture of the work. None of it is the deepest shift.

The deepest shift is that the cost of producing assets is collapsing toward zero.

Not literally zero — there is still strategy, taste, channel understanding, claims review, legal review, visual standards, production judgment, measurement, and editing. But the raw act of *making another asset* is no longer scarce. A team with clear inputs and a halfway-decent pipeline can produce competent marketing material at a volume that would have required a thirty-person studio five years ago.

If you know what you want to publish, at what cadence, with what design standards, with what voice, with what claims, and with what evidence, much of marketing production becomes pipeline work. You feed in brand guidelines, customer language, product truth, proof points, visual standards, compliance rules, channel requirements, and campaign goals. The system produces drafts, variants, hooks, pages, sequences, ads, posts, and sales enablement. Evals check whether the output is on-voice, specific, truthful, legally safe, differentiated, visually acceptable, and grounded in claims that exist somewhere defensible.

Every serious marketing organization will have some version of that within two years. So yes. Learn to build the pipeline. Stop burning human hours on resizing, repackaging, reformatting, and first-draft production.

But do not mistake that pipeline for the strategic center of the role. If your entire AI strategy as a marketer is “we can make more stuff,” you are building your career on the most commoditized layer of the job.

The harder question is what all that stuff is supposed to *do.*

## When every surface becomes source material

Agents force a much deeper rethink here, and it shows up in places marketing did not traditionally own.

Most marketing surfaces were designed primarily as persuasion surfaces. The homepage persuaded. The landing page converted. The case study reassured while the comparison page captured competitive search demand. The pricing page pushed someone toward a sales conversation or a checkout flow. Docs were post-sale support and developer enablement. The help center belonged to support. The changelog belonged to product. The sales deck belonged to sales. Marketing’s territory was the soft stuff at the top of the funnel and the polished stuff aimed at humans.

That division collapses in an agent-mediated world. Agents do not respect the org chart. They read what is publicly available, weigh it against everything else, and form a model of your company.

Your website becomes source material. Your docs become marketing. Your pricing page becomes a trust signal, not conversion infrastructure but evidence of how you do business. Comparison pages become evidence surfaces, customer stories become retrieval material. Your changelog signals velocity and seriousness while your integration pages may decide whether an agent understands that you fit a buyer’s actual workflow.

Every surface an agent can read is now a marketing surface, whether marketing built it or not.

The change is profound, and it has a specific consequence: marketing has to get much more serious about *product truth.*

## The truth layer

Humans tolerate a surprising amount of fuzziness. A human can watch a founder interview, read between the lines, and infer what the company is trying to become. A human can forgive a vague homepage if a friend says the product is good. A human can hear a story and fill in the missing context. Humans are messy, social, generous in strange ways, and pattern-matchers by default.

Agents are different. They build a map from the evidence available. If your public surfaces are inconsistent, the map is inconsistent. If the homepage says one thing, the docs say another, the pricing page hides the real packaging, the sales collateral overclaims, the help center is two product cycles stale, and the customer stories never explain what actually changed for the customer — the agent ends up with a bad map of your company.

When the agent has a bad map, you lose invisibly. The G2 finding from earlier — that roughly a third of B2B buyers bought from a vendor they had never heard of before an assistant surfaced it — is the same dynamic from the buyer’s side. Someone, somewhere, lost that sale because the agent’s map of their company was a worse map than the agent’s map of the eventual winner.

You may never know that a buyer asked an assistant to compare three vendors and you were summarized poorly. You may never know that your product was left off a shortlist because your integration story was buried three clicks deep. You may never know that a procurement workflow categorized you incorrectly because your category language was vague. You may never know that a customer’s internal copilot returned “unclear fit” because your proof points were too fluffy to survive compression into a paragraph.

Clarity becomes a marketing function, not as a stylistic preference but as a structural requirement. Not “write shorter sentences.” Not “make the homepage cleaner.” Clarity in the sense of: *can the market understand what you do, who you serve, where you win, where you lose, what you cost, what proof exists, and how to make a decision?*

The marketer has to become the advocate for the company’s truth layer: a living system of claims, proof, use cases, customer language, objections, competitive comparisons, product constraints, pricing logic, implementation details, and category beliefs. Not a static brand book that gets refreshed twice a year and then ignored. Operating infrastructure.

The truth layer is what every function quietly draws on. When the website changes or an AI system drafts lifecycle copy, the new language pulls from it. Sales reaches for it when responding to a competitor. Product connects to it when launching a feature. Customer success reinforces it when explaining value. And when external agents retrieve, compare, and summarize the company, the truth layer is what they encounter as a coherent public surface.

This work is not glamorous. It does not get people excited the way a beautiful campaign does. It is closer to library science than to advertising. But it is what determines whether the company is *understandable*, and most companies are not.

## The unresolved-strategy problem

Marketers know this part. They do not always say it out loud.

A lot of marketing teams are sitting on top of unresolved product strategy. They are asked to make the company sound differentiated before the company has done the work to actually be differentiated. They are asked to make the roadmap sound inevitable before the product is ready. They are asked to make the category feel urgent before the company has a sharp point of view. They are asked to put AI on the homepage because the board wants an AI story. They are asked to keep up with competitors who are exaggerating too.

AI makes this dynamic worse if you let it.

A mediocre marketing team with AI can produce a flood of plausible nonsense. The copy will sound fine. The images will look fine. The launch post will have the right rhythm. The campaign will hit the right number of assets. The lifecycle sequence will feel personalized. The social posts will sound appropriately punchy. And underneath, nothing gets clearer. The company looks more polished and remains, at the level of meaning, a blur.

That is the real danger for marketers in 2026. Not that AI makes marketing disappear, but that it lets bad marketing scale without friction.

## Taste matters more now

Taste matters more, not less. But taste cannot only mean “I know what good copy sounds like” or “I can tell when a visual is off-brand.” Those still matter. Taste in 2026 also means knowing whether a claim deserves to exist: whether the proof is strong enough, whether the customer language is real or synthetic, whether the company is saying something because it is true or because it is convenient, whether an asset creates memory or fills a slot on the calendar.

The future marketer is not the person who generates infinite variations. The future marketer is the person who *prevents the company from scaling weak taste.* That is closer to editing than to creation, and it requires conviction the AI cannot supply.

## What memory will the human carry?

That brings us to the human side of the split, because agents do not make brand less important. They make brand different.

People are still going to remember companies, trust certain names more than others, and carry preferences into agent-mediated decisions. They will say, “Find me something like this.” They will say, “I saw a demo from that company and it looked real.” They will name the brands their friends mentioned. Emotional residue survives long after the demo is over.

If anything, human memory becomes more precious as more of the transaction is mediated. The agent may do the comparison, retrieve the options, summarize the tradeoffs, draft the RFP, and recommend a shortlist. But the human still supplies the preference. The human names the brands they remember and sets the constraints. The human says what feels credible. The human decides, in the end, what kind of company they want to trust.

So the marketer has to ask a sharper brand question. Beyond *how do we get attention?* the real question is *what memory do we want a human to carry into an agent-mediated decision?*

That memory has to be specific. It cannot be “innovation” or “productivity” or “AI-powered growth” or “do more with less.” It cannot be a generic claim that any competitor could produce after one decent prompt.

A useful brand memory is closer to a specific category belief. A repeated phrase. A product behavior people associate with you, or a demo that sticks. A customer transformation. A founder point of view that no competitor could fake, paired with a visual language someone recognizes across surfaces. A use case rendered so clearly it becomes a reference point. A contrast that helps the buyer understand the market itself. And binding it all together, a proof point that is hard to forget.

The human should remember something clear enough that it can become an *instruction* to an agent later.

“Find me the one that does X.” “Compare this to that company I saw.” “Show me tools like this, but for my industry.” “Don’t include vendors that require a six-month implementation.” “Find the product with the best workflow for a sales-led team of fewer than fifty.”

That last one is the test. Did your brand work hard enough that a buyer can describe you in a single, specific sentence to a piece of software they trust? If yes, the agent layer becomes an amplifier. If no, the agent layer flattens you into the category mean.

Weaving humans and agents together means exactly this. The human-facing work creates memory, preference, trust, and language. The agent-facing work creates clarity, structure, evidence, and retrievability. The two layers should reinforce each other. If your human brand says one thing and your agent-readable reality says another, the company gets weaker in both directions. If your story is memorable but your product truth is incoherent, the agent will flatten you badly. If your public materials are well-structured but the brand has no memory, you may show up in comparisons but fail to be chosen. The best marketers refuse to choose between the two.

## Why AI-washing is a trust-debt loan

If you are working at a stuck-in-the-mud brand, the pressure to AI-wash will be intense. Competitors will say they are AI-native. The board will ask what your AI story is. The CEO will want the company to sound current. Sales will ask for sharper AI language because prospects are asking. Someone will point to a competitor’s homepage and ask why you do not sound like that.

The temptation will be to stretch.

Don’t.

AI-washing buys short-term air cover and creates long-term trust debt. It makes the company harder for agents to understand and harder for humans to believe. It forces sales to explain *around* the website. It forces customer success to manage disappointment they did not create. It forces product to live under promises it did not make. It makes the marketer feel modern while making the company less coherent.

A few specifics, since this is where most companies trip. If the product is not AI-native, do not call it AI-native. Describe what it is, plainly. If the company has one narrow AI feature, explain the narrow feature in plain terms. If the AI work is mostly internal, better tooling for support, faster ops, smarter search, say that. Internal AI is not embarrassing. Pretending it is external is. If the company’s actual strength is workflow depth, trust, customer intimacy, regulatory knowledge, distribution, or service quality, do not bury that under fake frontier language. Those are real moats. AI fluency is rarely the moat people imagine. The underlying advantage usually is.

The marketer’s job is not to make the company sound like the future. It is to find the honest wedge that gives the company a future.

The same trust-debt logic applies to you as an individual. If you are a marketer positioning yourself for a role in 2026, the pressure to AI-wash your own profile runs at least as strong as the company’s pressure to AI-wash its homepage, and the same structural problem catches up with you eventually. A LinkedIn bio polished into AI-native language by whatever rewrite tool is in vogue this quarter signals nothing distinctive to a recruiter’s research workflow that has already absorbed ten thousand identical bios, and a portfolio of slick-but-generic case studies tends to read, to a hiring system doing first-pass discovery, as either synthetic or interchangeable with everyone else’s.

The agents reading candidate surfaces are not different from the agents reading company surfaces. They flatten what cannot be cited cleanly, and they reward what is specific enough to survive compression into a paragraph. What gets through is the opposite of polish: a point of view someone could quote back to you, a project described in enough operational detail that the reader can picture the work being done, a tradeoff you made that another candidate could not credibly claim. The candidate’s job, in the end, is the same as the company’s job — become memorable to a human and legible to an agent in language that could not have come from anybody else.

## What to look for in 2026

The career advice gets practical here.

If you’re a marketer joining a company in 2026, look carefully at whether marketing is allowed to touch the surfaces that now matter. Can marketing influence the website, docs, pricing clarity, claims, customer proof, product messaging, launch process, sales collateral, and support-facing explanations? Or is marketing only allowed to decorate decisions made elsewhere, handed a roadmap and asked to make it sound exciting on launch day?

If marketing is just the content factory, that is a problem. If the company says it wants AI marketing but only means “make more assets with fewer people,” that is a problem. If the company wants to AI-wash but not clarify, that is a problem. If there is no claims discipline, no customer language library, no proof library, no design standards, and no working relationship between product truth and public messaging, AI will not save you. It will accelerate the mess. You will spend two years producing very fluent versions of confusion.

A good marketing role in 2026 gives you reps on the new control surface. You are learning how to make the company more legible to agents, more memorable to humans, and more operationally capable internally. You are close to product, close to sales, close to customer reality, and close enough to technical teams to understand how the company’s AI systems actually work: which prompts run where, which knowledge bases get retrieved, which evals fire, what breaks.

## Marketing becomes more technical

You do not need to become an engineer. You do need to become more technical than marketers used to be.

You should understand what your website and docs expose to retrieval, and which parts are invisible because they live behind login walls or in PDFs no one indexes. You should understand how agents compare products: what fields they look for, what they treat as authoritative, how they weight third-party reviews against vendor claims. You should understand how internal content pipelines are evaluated and where they fail. You should understand why structured claims matter, not as an SEO trick but as a way of giving the agent something specific to cite. You should understand, at a working level, how memory, RAG, search, answer engines, and agentic workflows shape discovery and decision-making.

You are not trying to become the CTO. You are trying to stay relevant to a function that has become more technical, and the marketers who refuse to learn that will spend the rest of the decade complaining that the website team will not take their meetings.

The old version of marketing could sometimes live at the surface of the company. The new version cannot. If agents are going to interpret the company, marketing has to care about the underlying truth. If internal pipelines are going to generate assets, marketing has to care about the standards those systems use. If buyers are going to use AI to compare options, marketing has to care about what evidence exists in public.

## Operators of meaning systems

The best marketers will feel more like operators of meaning systems than producers of campaigns. That sounds abstract. It becomes concrete fast.

You build the *agent clarity audit.* You walk every public surface an agent might use to understand the company: homepage, product pages, docs, pricing, comparisons, integrations, customer stories, changelog, blog, help center, third-party listings. You ask what picture emerges from each, and what picture emerges in aggregate. You identify contradictions. Missing proof. Vague claims. Stale docs. Buried integrations. Pricing that hides the real shape of the business. Comparisons unsupported by evidence. Places where customers describe the product better than you do.

You build the *human memory map.* You ask what one or two things a person should remember after encountering the company once. Then you test whether the homepage, demo, founder narrative, customer stories, sales motion, and product experience actually create that memory, not in your imagination but in interviews and replay sessions and quiet hallway tests with people who are not on your team.

You build the *production pipeline.* You automate what can be automated, but you do it with standards. The pipeline generates, yes. More important: it rejects. It rejects vague copy, unsupported claims, off-brand visuals, generic positioning, and content that is technically correct but strategically useless. The discipline of saying no inside the pipeline is what keeps quality from collapsing as volume scales.

## AI-enabled vs. AI-native

The real difference between an AI-enabled marketing team and an AI-native one.

The AI-enabled team uses tools to do the old work faster. The AI-native team redesigns what marketing is responsible for. Faster spreadsheets versus a different operating model.

And the responsibility is bigger than content. Marketing becomes the steward of how the company is understood across human attention and machine interpretation. The marketer knows what the company can honestly claim, what customers actually say, what proof exists, what agents will retrieve and how, and which memories matter. The marketer also knows where the company is being vague because the *strategy* is vague, and has the standing to say so.

A much better job than “make more stuff.”

It is also a much harder one.

---

The marketers who win in 2026 will not be the ones celebrating content velocity. Content velocity will be table stakes. Everyone will have it. The winners will be the marketers who can make a company clearer, more trusted, more memorable, and easier to choose.

Marketing is splitting in two. Not persuasion for humans alone. Not content automation for internal efficiency alone.

Memory for humans, clarity for agents. The marketers who can hold both layers at once are the ones worth betting on.
