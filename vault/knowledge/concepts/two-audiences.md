## Definition

A dual-targeting mechanism where marketing must simultaneously address human decision-makers and AI agents that act on their behalf. This requires content to be persuasive to humans while also being structurally legible and interpretable by AI systems, often through formats like daily notes. The tension arises when content optimized for one audience degrades the effectiveness of the other, creating a hidden friction in cross-agent communication.

## Context

For Sean, this concept is vital because as he navigates product management and AI agent development, ensuring content works for both humans and algorithms is key to scalability. Misalignment between the two audiences can lead to inefficiencies in automation, impact brand visibility through algorithmic systems, and reduce the effectiveness of agent-driven workflows like Daily Note Generation.

## Evidence

> Marketing now serves two audiences at once. It serves the humans it has always served, and the agents that increasingly read, summarize, compare, and recommend on behalf of those humans before any human decision gets made.

> The biggest shift in thinking — start asking AI models to do more for you, especially if you're a PM that isn't the most technical person.

## Examples

- Daily Note Generation systems that are inconsistent or incomplete may hinder AI agents from making accurate recommendations based on marketing content.
- A brand's persuasive messaging might be strong for humans, but if it lacks structure or clarity for AI agents, algorithmic visibility could suffer.
