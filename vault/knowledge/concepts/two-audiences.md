## Definition

A dual-targeting mechanism where marketing must simultaneously address human decision-makers and AI agents that act on their behalf. The former requires persuasive messaging, while the latter demands legibility and structured data to be effectively parsed by algorithms. This divergence creates a hidden tension within marketing strategy, as traditional tactics may neglect the needs of AI agents that increasingly influence buyer decisions.

## Context

For Sean, this dual-audience model is critical as he navigates roles in product management and creative studio. His work must align with both human stakeholders who rely on traditional storytelling and AI systems that require precise, structured information for decision-making.

## Evidence

> Marketing now serves two audiences at once. It serves the humans it has always served, and the agents that increasingly read, summarize, compare, and recommend on behalf of those humans before any human decision gets made.

> Sixty-nine percent reported choosing a different vendor than they originally planned because of guidance from an AI chatbot, and roughly a third bought from a vendor they had never heard of before the assistant surfaced it.

## Examples

- A company might invest heavily in a compelling brand narrative but fail to structure its public data in a way that AI agents can easily process.
- A product's marketing may be highly persuasive for humans but lack the legibility required by AI models, leading to missed opportunities in automated decision-making.
