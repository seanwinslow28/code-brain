## 1\. The Basics

## 1.1 What is vibe coding and what is the format of the interview

Vibe coding, in this context, is the use of an AI coding (eg, [Cursor](https://cursor.com/), [Windsurf](https://windsurf.com/), or [Replit](https://replit.com/)) or [AI prototyping](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-prototyping-tutorial) tool (eg, [Lovable](https://lovable.dev/), [Bolt](https://bolt.new/), [Base44](https://base44.com/), or [v0](https://v0.dev/)).

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!pJuB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4f23f86b-44d8-41b0-8f44-21a0b0149fdb_1572x1282.png)

Strengths of each tool.

My mentees have encountered *three* different formats of this interview

1. The 45-minute prototyping case
2. The product design case with a prototyping component
3. The homework assignment where a prototype is mentioned in the prompt

*Here’s how each actually unfolds in the room.*

### Type 1: The 45-minute prototyping case

This first type of case is what the Google AI PM interviewee in India faced.

Typically, the prompt is clear: use any vibe coding tool of your choice to create a version of x feature. The prompts fall into three major buckets:

- Design X feature for us
- Design Y feature on Z famous product
- Build a prototype of X new 0 to 1 product

In this type of case, you need to build a structured framework to **design the feature before just prototyping it**.

But a lot of the assessment is *how well you prototype*.

### Type 2: The product design case with a prototyping component

This second case is anywhere from 30-60 minutes and typically involves a **more challenging design prompt**. For instance:

- Design Google Maps for the Blind
- How would you prototype a Facebook Dating app

These types of cases require a *strong product design case up front* (identifying user needs and brainstorming creative solutions), followed by a good but quick prototyping session.

### Type 3: The homework assignment where a prototype is mentioned in the prompt

This final type of vibe coding interview has been used at places as mainstream as Stripe and Netflix.

They give you a broad prompt, like:

> *Design a Netflix product to support Partner Studios.*

Then you have to go through all the elements of a strong product design interview.

Multiple candidates I’ve coached had to submit a final 1 pager with a link to the prototype.

*So, that covers the three main types of PM vibe coding interviews!*

## 1.2. The new PM skillset and why they’re asking (plus more will in the future)

The nature of the PM role is undergoing its biggest shift since the shift from input to output metrics ~5 years ago.

AI is ushering in a new era of [AI PMs](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/complete-course-ai-product-management), who:

- [Build AI features](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/how-to-build-ai-products), getting into technical details like evals
- [Use AI](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-pm-playbook) to focus on high leverage tasks
- And [prototype with AI](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/ai-prototyping-tutorial)

That’s why PM interviews are now focusing on all three of these topics.

Just like the shift to output metrics and strategy led to the growth of [product metrics](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/product-metrics-interview) and [product strategy interviews](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/crack-the-product-strategy-interview), this latest shift to AI PMs is driving the growth of the product vibe coding interview.

Many companies are still in the process of adapting to this AI-first reality. Their PMs don’t have licenses to AI prototyping and vibe coding tools.

But as those companies fall further behind and replace their old IT policies with AI-first ones, the nature of the PM job will shift there as well.

And a short while after the job shifts, the interviews will have to shift as well.

Interviews exist to find candidates who can do the job: and vibe coding (aka AI prototyping) is a core part of the PM job for forward-looking companies.

AI prototyping tools are a better way to convey ideas than a PRD, and help PMs actually think through all the edge cases & flows around the periphery.

So the growth of this interview type is *inevitable*.

## 2\. Nailing the Interview

## 2.1 What separates a winning from a failing response in this interview

*Having worked with so many different candidates who have failed and succeeded on this round, here’s what I’ve learned.*

People fall into three major buckets on this round:

#### Bucket 1 - Instant Fail

These are people who haven’t vibe coded or AI prototyped before. Just using the tool is a new experience for them.

They almost always fail, as they spend the entire time fighting the tool instead of solving the problem.

To be better than bucket 1, make sure you watch the resources I’ve created on vibe coding and AI prototyping:

- [Vibe coding tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oLmHdymHHg0)
- [AI prototyping tutorial](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Gp6tMkCr_0w)

I also have [Cursor](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ej4pBDaHspk), [v0](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1TpN4dgFg48), and [Bolt](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FE20SlPGSMw) tutorials. (*If you’ve been a long-time [subscriber](https://www.news.aakashg.com/) to the newsletter, you got all those e-mailed to you*.)

And then make sure you *actually use* all of the tools.

#### Bucket 2 - Knowledgeable, But Not Nailing the Rubric

The second bucket of folks have watched the tutorials and even tried out the tools themselves.

But they look like beginners, not masters:

1. They don’t have a workflow for using these tools (eg, writing a PRD first)
2. They are unfamiliar with how to toggle between UI and code
3. They are weak at connecting databases, auth, and payments
4. They jump right into prototyping without doing initial steps around the problem and solution space first

You need to have a strong workflow, know how to use the tools, know how to extend the tools to make them functional, and also conduct the normal PM work before getting into coding.

#### Bucket 3 - Demonstrate Clear Mastery

The final group of people look like clear passes. They:

1. Know exactly how they like to use the tools due to having used them a lot, with a clear workflow
2. Understand all the intricacies of using the tool and aren’t confused by windows, they know the shortcuts
3. They can easily speak to how to add in advanced capabilities like databases, auth, and payments
4. They go through a quick but effective process of problem and solution exploration first

*Putting this all together… this is my recommended framework for these cases:*

## 2.2 My framework to use for any vibe coding interview

The framework I want you to remember is UPS-PPPB:

- **U** ser
- **P** roblem
- **S** olution
- **P** RD
- **P** rompt
- **P** arallel
- **B** ackend

UPS is the setup (fundamental PM work), and PPPB delivers the prototype (vibe coding workflow).

Let’s walk through it in detail.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7aM3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9ade2bee-1694-420f-a34e-dbd3ce3ad5ec_3600x4500.png)

### U - User \[3-5 mins\]

Like any good product design or product sense case, you have to start with the user! This is, after all, the core role of the PM on the team to begin with.

Spend a few minutes covering:

- Who are the various stakeholders in the prompt?
- What is their context and environment?
- What constraints do they face?

This is the time to ask clarifying questions and show your user-centricity.

The interviewer wants to see you think like a PM first, coder second.

### P - Problem \[3-5 mins\]

Now that you understand the stakeholders, identify the specific problems that each is facing.

Get really crisp on:

- What job-to-be-done are we addressing?
- What’s the core problem statement we are prioritizing?

Write this down. Narrate your thinking out loud. Say something like "So the core problem I'm solving is..."

The interviewer needs to see your PM problem-solving process.

Ideally, prioritize the problems as well.

### S - Solution \[5-10 mins\]

This is where you go through the typical product design/ product sense process of brainstorming creative solutions to the prioritized problems.

You want to think divergently about a wide variety of solutions (and even ask for a minute to think to yourself if needed so they are truly creative).

The worst thing to do is go prototype some really basic feature that has nothing interesting going on.

So before you go into the prototype, take time to think about the solution space.

Use some method to determine what the best solution is. Then prioritize that and move into the vibe coding workflow portion of the framework.

### P - PRD \[2-3 mins\]

Once you have the basics if your solution, create a very basic PRD.

Write down:

- The user and problem to be solved
- The basic user flow
- The metrics it’s going after
- What success looks like
- Prominent edge cases

Don’t use AI here just yet.

### P - Prompt \[3-5 mins\]

Now what you want to do is create the final prompt that you’re going to plug into the prototyping tools.

So work with an AI and plug in your mini-PRD:

> Take my basic PRD I have put together and create a full-fledged clear prompt for an AI prototyping tool (eg Bolt or Lovable) that will guide it to create the prototype well.

Edit the prompt as needed.

### P - Parallel \[15-20 mins\]

Then you want to select what tools are the best fit from the job amongst:

- **Vibe coding**: Cursor, Windsurf
- **AI prototyping**: Bolt, Lovable, v0, Replit, and Base44

Reason with the interviewer why you are choosing the tools you do to show mastery over the entire space of tools. For example:

> *This is a mostly front-end based feature, so I’m going to choose the best front-end tools: v0, Bolt, and Lovable.*

Then drop the prompt into all 3 tools.

Based on the first response, choose which tool is doing the best job and then iterate!

Notice how this step has the most time.

This is your time to shine, showing that you can give clear and accurate instructions to the AI that help it get to a good end state.

Use the prototype and make sure all edge cases work.

### B - Backend \[10-15 mins\]

Once you have the frontend nailed, work on the backend architecture. This will really separate you:

1. Add in a database
2. Add in auth, payments or whatever else you need
3. Add in analytics telemetry and traces for observability

This takes your product from a prototype to truly a vibe coded feature.

(So make sure you know how to do this in every tool.)

Then end with tying together everything you put together.

### Another Way to Remember

If UPS-PPPB isn’t your cup of tea for whatever reason, the keys here are to:

1. Do the setup and solve a normal product design / product sense question (UPS)
2. Have an advanced vibe coding workflow (PPPB)

You don’t want to skip either step. You need to do the PM work and have an advanced vibe coding workflow.

### Remember - Unified Analysis Wins

As I teach for all case interviews, don’t just use UPS-PPPB for every interview! There are many flavors of interviews. So instead, say:

> *Can I have a minute to create a structure we can follow?*

Then write down UPS-PPPB vertically and decide which of the sections you need, and if you need to add any others.

## 2.3 What to do if you’re totally caught off-guard

So what if you get into another interview like vibe coding but you’re not prepared?

#### During the Interview

First, ask for time! Say something like:

> This is a really exciting interview challenge. I’ve never quite done one like this before, or prepared explicitly for this type of interview.
> 
> Can I have a few minutes to think through my approach?

Then, take up to 5 minutes to think through:

- What is going to be my structure for this interview?
- What additional information do I need?
- Where can this interview go wrong and how could I have done better?

The Reddit interviewee themselves states in the comments that they had done a bunch of vibe coding. They just failed the interview because they jumped in.

Always avoid the temptation of just jumping in! 5 minutes of good thinking to yourself at the beginning can be all you need to figure out what NOT to do in the remaining 45 minutes.

#### After the Interview

Second, after the interview, consider asking the recruiter for a **re-roll**.

Basically, you email them something after to the effect of:

> Hi Sally,
> 
> Thanks so much again for scheduling my interview with Ishan!
> 
> I was a bit caught off guard with the format. I know you and I had discused role related knowledge being a part of the interview, but I didn’t realize it was going to be a vibe coding interview.
> 
> As a result, I’m not sure I performed my best. I was wondering if I could interview again (perhaps for a different team) now that I’m more aware of the format?
> 
> I’m actually quite adept at vibe coding, but I just wasn’t ready for that format.
> 
> Thanks,
> 
> Aakash

It’s a quick and short email where you explain how you were caught off-guard.

While these have a low success rate, they do have a success rate.

And you miss all the shots you don’t take.

## 3\. Examples and practice

## 3.1 The most common mistakes I’ve seen coaching candidates on it

I see folks making the same mistakes on this interview. (Some of these I touched on 2.1, but they’re so important that I need to re-emphasize them.)

#### Mistake 1 - Forgetting the UPS

Remember part 1 of the framework was user, problem, and solution. Don’t skip this step!

This is the classical [product sense](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/master-the-product-sense-interview) + [product design](https://www.news.aakashg.com/p/master-the-pm-product-design-interview) stuff you’ve been practicing for years. Demonstrate it!

#### Mistake 2 - Going too Slow

This is the second mistake I see people making. They do UPS but they spend too long on it!

Remember, this is a vibe coding interview. They want to see your advanced vibe coding workflow.

Leave time for PPPB.

#### Mistake 3 - Not Iterating Smartly

Many people just take the AI prompt and don’t iterate with it. You can’t assume the AI is going to get everything right.

Click around, criticize the design as you do.

Then iterate and make it better.

#### Mistake 4 - Not showing mastery of the workflows

Many people act shy about the tools:

> Oh!

> Didn’t expect that!

You want to act calm, cool, collected, and experienced.

#### Mistake 5 - Forgetting to tie it back to success

Although it’s not part of my framework, don’t forget to tie everything together at the end.

Spend 30 seconds to give an executive summary:

> So we’ve built X solution to X problem. And our vibe code feature does X on the frontend and X on the backend. With more time, I would do X and Y.

This ties everything up nicely and leaves a good last taste in the interviewer’s mouth (especially if they weren’t taking good notes, or got lost along the way.)
